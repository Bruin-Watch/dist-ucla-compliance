II. Standing

UC Whistleblower Protection Policy 1100563 § B (Definitions)
“Complainant — A current University employee or a former University employee who was employed at the time the relevant events occurred. The term includes academic appointees and applicants for employment.” — UC Policy 1100563

The Complainant was employed by The Regents of the University of California at the UCLA Police Department from December 1989 through March 1, 1996. Since March 1, 1996, the Complainant has been a continuous recipient of Duty Disability Income (DDI) under the University of California Retirement Plan. The Octagon Risk Services permanent and stationary disability determination of August 8, 2001 establishing a 6¾% permanent disability rating in connection with Workers’ Compensation Claim No. 199650445 (Date of Injury February 16, 1996) has never been revoked, modified, or superseded by any medical authority, by the Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board, or by any other tribunal of competent jurisdiction. No WCAB modification order has ever issued in this matter. (See Schödinger’s UC Retiree, CH_06; CH_09; Act III, chapter_05.)

UCLA Police Department records, statements by sworn UCLA PD personnel, and UC’s own published Gold Book establish a continuing institutional position regarding the Complainant’s status:

  • Captain Jeff Chobanian (Badge #307) wrote on August 25, 2023: “After a comprehensive review of your file, I have determined you are ineligible for a CCW. Your status was designated as medically separated on March 1, 1996.” (EX 19)
  • Captain Chobanian wrote on February 10, 2026: “I reviewed your ‘file’ and cross-referenced the records, including your date of separation, classification, status, and our internal records.” (EX 17a, p. 11)
  • UC Gold Book General Order 21-02, § 1700.1(b)(1): “An officer receiving duty disability income has not retired or separated from the University of California …”
The Two-Path Standing Trap

Path A. If the LDO determines that the Complainant is not a current University employee, the Complainant qualifies under Policy 1100563 § B as “a former University employee who was employed at the time the relevant events occurred” — the relevant events being the 2025-2026 Adverse Personnel Actions catalogued in Section IV below. Standing is established. A Path A finding is also an institutional admission that the Complainant has separated, which directly contradicts the August 25, 2023 CCW denial premised on continuing medical-separation classification and the UC Gold Book GO 21-02 position that DDI recipients have not separated.

Path B. If the LDO determines that the Complainant has not separated and remains in DDI status — consistent with the Captain’s August 25, 2023 letter, his February 10, 2026 cross-reference, and UC Gold Book GO 21-02 § 1700.1(b)(1) — the Complainant qualifies as a current University employee. Standing is established under that path as well.

The Complainant adopts whichever classification UC defends. Standing is established under either path. There is no third path on which the LDO can dismiss this complaint for lack of standing without resolving the institutional contradiction in the Complainant’s favor.

IV. Adverse Personnel Actions (2025–2026)

UC Whistleblower Protection Policy 1100563 § B (Definitions) & § C.1.a.ii
“Adverse Personnel Action — A management action taken with respect to a Complainant that affects the Complainant’s existing terms and conditions of employment in a material and negative way.”  |  The Complaint must describe each Adverse Personnel Action, identify the responsible University employee(s), and specify the date or approximate date.

The Adverse Personnel Actions catalogued below were not administrative errors. They were acts taken by sworn California peace officers and UC administrators functioning as state actors under color of authority — within the meaning of 42 U.S.C. § 1983 and 18 U.S.C. § 242 — whose collective conduct has deprived the Complainant of constitutionally protected property interests without notice or hearing, in violation of the Fourteenth Amendment’s Due Process Clause as applied in Goldberg v. Kelly, 397 U.S. 254 (1970), Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill, 470 U.S. 532 (1985), and Monell v. Department of Social Services, 436 U.S. 658 (1978). The state actors whose conduct is catalogued below include: Captain Jeff Chobanian (Badge #307); Lieutenant James Echols; Lieutenant Richard L. Davis; Chief Craig Valenzuela; Beverly Jeanine Williams-Cook, Benefits Analyst III; and Erin Morse, Benefits/Insurance Supervisor — each acting within the scope of institutional authority, each on a documented written record, each on dates that place their actions in direct temporal proximity to the Complainant’s Protected Disclosures.

The central instrument of that deprivation is the Complainant’s sworn peace officer personnel file — a record protected under California Penal Code § 832.7 and the Pitchess discovery framework; required to be maintained under Government Code § 3306.5(b) and California Code of Regulations Title 8 § 15400.2; subject to the Complainant’s right of inspection and response under POBR Government Code §§ 3305 and 3306; and independently required to be preserved without destruction under California Code of Regulations Title 8 § 10102, which mandates that workers’ compensation claim files where future benefits may be payable “shall not be destroyed” — a requirement that applies here because Workers’ Compensation Claim No. 199650445, settled March 2003 with lifetime paid health insurance and continuing disability income, has never been formally closed, the WCAB has never issued a modification order, and future benefits remain payable under the terms of that settlement and the Octagon Risk Services permanent and stationary determination of August 8, 2001. The Complainant’s personnel file contains the workers’ compensation records, medical releases, and disability classification records required to establish the continuing benefit obligations UC now disputes. Under Title 8 § 10102, that file cannot legally be destroyed. Under Title 8 § 15400.2, it shall not be destroyed. Under the litigation hold of February 9, 2026 (EX 20), it was expressly forbidden from destruction by written legal notice. Three independent legal authorities prohibit its destruction. It may not exist.

That file contains the documentary basis for the Complainant’s true employment classification. Captain Chobanian cited it as the source of the “medically separated on March 1, 1996” determination that denied the Complainant’s CCW and anchored UC RASC’s subsequent coverage termination. Captain Chobanian confirmed in writing that he reviewed the file on August 25, 2023 and again on February 10, 2026 — one day after the Complainant served a written litigation hold on him expressly forbidding its destruction. Fifty-one days after that litigation hold, Lieutenant Echols stated in writing that the agency had received the wrong box and that the Complainant’s files, “if they still exist,” are difficult to locate. The four mutually irreconcilable custody-of-file statements issued between February 24, 2026 and April 1, 2026 — on UCLA PD letterhead, with Captain Chobanian CC’d on each — cannot all be true on the same set of facts. At least one is false. Sworn California peace officers who issue written statements they know to be false regarding a protected personnel file subject to a documented legal hold trigger California Penal Code § 118.1 (false reports by peace officers), § 135 (destruction or concealment of documents subject to legal process), and § 135.5 (concealment of evidence in a public investigation — the Labor Code § 132a crime report of February 19, 2026 and the POSAD complaints PUBLIC-26-000212/213/214 are public investigations within the meaning of § 135.5).

The classification stands. The file does not. If the file that Captain Chobanian twice confirmed reviewing is produced, it will establish the Complainant’s classification — one way or the other — and resolve every disputed question in this Sworn Retaliation Complaint. If it is not produced, California Evidence Code § 413 permits the inference that it would be adverse to UC’s position, and Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court, 18 Cal.4th 1 (1998), and Williams v. Russ, 210 Cal.App.4th 35 (2012), permit the presumption that the file, if produced, would support the Complainant’s position. The state actors who issued the classification, confirmed reviewing the file, and then watched it disappear have not explained the contradiction. The Locally Designated Official is respectfully asked to explain it.

7. Extrinsic Fraud — The Doctrine That Captures This Conduct

California recognizes the doctrine of extrinsic fraud as a basis for setting aside an institutional determination that has been procured or maintained through conduct that prevents the affected party from fully participating in or fairly contesting the underlying matter. Kulchar v. Kulchar, 1 Cal.3d 467, 471 (1969). Extrinsic fraud reaches conduct outside the four corners of a determination — fraudulent concealment of evidence, manufactured records, suppression of contradictory documents — that operates to insulate the determination from the scrutiny it would not survive on a true record.

The conduct documented in Sub-Sections 1 through 6 above fits the extrinsic-fraud doctrine on its face. UCOP has:

  • Manufactured a false start date on the address-of-record field in UCRAYS, manifestly inconsistent with UCOP’s own continuous federal ACH payment stream and UCOP’s own annual IRS sworn filings;
  • Truncated approximately six years of pay-stub history from the records visible to the affected party while continuing to issue payments under that same record;
  • Issued a phantom clawback against the affected party with no corresponding banking event, internally inconsistent on the face of UCOP’s own pay-stub document, with no advance written notice, statutory citation, or explanation; and
  • Continued to deny, close, transfer, and reduce benefits in reliance on the reconstructed records while withholding from the affected party the unaltered originals.

Each of those acts independently prevented the Complainant from fully participating in or fairly contesting the determinations made against him. Cumulatively, the acts constitute the extrinsic-fraud pattern Kulchar was decided to reach.
