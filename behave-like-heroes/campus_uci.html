July 2008 — Identity Theft Investigation; IACP Commendation
FieldDetail
DescriptionUCIPD dispatched three detectives to Texas to investigate an identity theft ring targeting UCI graduate and medical students. A suspect had stolen Social Security numbers from United Healthcare (the university's student insurance provider) computers in Dallas and used them to file false tax returns. UCIPD received 163 identity theft reports. The investigation involved Dallas County DA investigators and Fort Worth PD, requiring hundreds of hours, dozens of interviews, and complex computer analyses. The suspect was arrested for fraudulent use of identifying information. The UCIPD investigative team was recognized the following year by the International Association of Chiefs of Police.
Applicable Law / ReportingStandard law enforcement investigation. No POST reporting deficiency identified.
Compliance StatusN/A — PROPERLY HANDLED Included to document UCIPD investigative capacity and inter-jurisdictional coordination capability.
Source(s)UCI News, March 5, 2009
December 2013 (Discovered) / November 2014 (Lawsuit Filed) — FUPOA v. UC Regents — Illegal Audio Surveillance Lawsuit
FieldDetail
DescriptionIn November 2014, FUPOA — representing 250+ UC police officers — filed a class-action federal lawsuit against the UC Board of Regents, UCIPD, Chief Paul Henisey, Assistant Chief Jeff Hutchison, and Johnson Controls Inc. The lawsuit alleged that Chief Henisey and Assistant Chief Hutchison hired Johnson Controls to install "highly advanced" audio-visual recording devices in offices, hallways, and bathrooms at UCIPD headquarters before December 2013. The devices allegedly could "amplify recordings past those normally perceptible" and "record audio through interior walls." The lawsuit alleged the system recorded officers' private conversations about finances, medical treatments, working conditions, union grievances, and internal affairs investigations. When the surveillance system was discovered in December 2013, UCI police officials allegedly deleted several months of recordings.

FUPOA President Andrew Lopez stated: "Our privacy rights continue to be eroded by unauthorized government recording of private citizens." Attorney David Mastagni Sr. stated: "It should be looked in the point of view of the regents and [the police's] managers in the UC system conducting surveillance on everyone." UCI spokesperson Cathy Lawhon claimed the audio capability "was not initially disabled during installation" and that Chief Henisey "immediately directed the installers to disable that function." UC lawyers sought dismissal arguing officers lacked reasonable expectation of privacy in the workplace. Johnson Controls separately sought dismissal. Final disposition has not been located in available public records. All characterizations above reflect allegations as reported in contemporaneous news sources cited; no judicial findings on the merits have been located.
Applicable Law / ReportingCalifornia Invasion of Privacy Act (PC §630 et seq.); Fourth Amendment; California Labor Code (workplace privacy); Peace Officers Bill of Rights (Government Code §3300 et seq.); Federal wiretapping statutes (18 U.S.C. §2511)
Compliance StatusUNKNOWN — FINAL DISPOSITION NOT LOCATED Federal lawsuit filed. If allegations proven, would constitute violations of officer privacy rights by UCIPD management under Chief Henisey's command.
Source(s)New University, November 25, 2014; LRIS, November 2014; OC Register, February 6, 2015; Daily Californian, November 19, 2014
February 20, 2020 — Arrest of Black Alumna Shikera Chamndany; Body Camera Footage Refused
FieldDetail
DescriptionShikera Chamndany, a Black alumna holding a biomedical engineering degree from UCI, went to Aldrich Hall to collect transcripts. That same day, UCI students were protesting in solidarity with UC Santa Cruz COLA strikers and Aldrich Hall was on lockdown. Chamndany was NOT affiliated with the protest. Per a UCIPD Facebook post, she "forcibly entered" Aldrich Hall and was arrested for "assaulting a police officer." Per accounts from the UCI Black Student Union, United Students Against Sweatshops, UCI Faculty Association, and UCI Anthropology Department, UCIPD Officer Trish Harding tackled and handcuffed Chamndany. She was transported to Orange County Women's Jail and held overnight until release on February 21.

A petition demanding: (1) financial support for Chamndany, (2) free mental health services with a Black therapist, (3) termination of Officer Harding, and (4) abolishment of UCIPD collected 1,500+ signatures within one week. The UCI Faculty Association wrote to Chancellor Howard Gillman characterizing the arrest as "violent and disturbing." The UCI Anthropology Department issued a solidarity statement connecting the incident to "a long history of anti-Black racism on the UCI campus." Chief Griffin declined to comment during the investigation. A second Change.org petition specifically demanded release of Officer Harding's body camera footage — UCIPD refused to release the footage. As of the February 2021 UAW 2865 report, the footage had still not been released.
Applicable Law / ReportingSB 1421 (PC §832.7 — use-of-force records disclosure); AB 748 (PC §832.18 — body camera footage release within 45 days for critical incidents involving officer-involved shootings or use of force causing great bodily injury); POST use-of-force reporting standards; RIPA/AB 953 (data collection began January 2022 — post-dates this incident)
Compliance StatusBODY CAMERA FOOTAGE NOT RELEASED — DOCUMENTED Body camera footage not released despite two public petitions and documentation by the UAW 2865 Research Working Group. UAW 2865 report (February 2021) confirmed the footage remained unreleased as of that report. Whether the Chamndany incident met the specific statutory threshold criteria for AB 748 (officer-involved shooting or use of force resulting in great bodily injury) has not been independently confirmed in available public records. The documented fact of non-release stands on the record; the applicable statutory framework is identified for the reader's evaluation.
Source(s)UAW 2865 "Policing in the UC" report, February 2021 — uaw2865.org; Diverse: Issues in Higher Education, 2020
May 2021 — California State Auditor Report 2020-032: UCI Had Highest Rate of Inaccurate Clery Statistics
FieldDetail
DescriptionThe California State Auditor Report 2020-032 audited UCI as one of six California institutions for Clery Act compliance. UCI was found to have the HIGHEST RATE of inaccurate crime statistics among the four institutions reviewed that reported Clery-reportable crimes. Specific documented findings: (1) A rape accusation was later withdrawn when the reporting individual stated it was a misunderstanding — UCIPD improperly reported it both as a rape AND as an unfounded crime. The State Auditor found that unfounding a crime requires a formal sworn officer determination, not merely a withdrawal of a complaint. (2) The Annual Security Report contained missing or inadequate security policies.

The State Auditor recommended by November 2021 that UCI establish procedures to review all security policies for Clery Act compliance. UCI's documented corrective actions: Updated policies in the 2020 ASR; re-issued ASR to UCI campus and UCI Health on June 29, 2021; published updated Clery Act Compliance Procedures on October 8, 2021 (policies.uci.edu/policies/procs/905-50); added quarterly review of Clery crime classifications. Whether all deficiencies are fully resolved has not been independently verified as of March 2026.
Applicable Law / ReportingClery Act (20 U.S.C. §1092(f)); Higher Education Act §485(f); 34 C.F.R. §668.46
Compliance StatusNOT COMPLIANT (CORRECTED) State Auditor found UCI had the highest rate of inaccurate Clery statistics among reviewed institutions. UCI implemented corrective actions by late 2021. Whether all deficiencies are fully and sustainably resolved has not been independently verified.
Source(s)CA State Auditor Report 2020-032, May 2021 — auditor.ca.gov/reports/2020-032; ABC7 Los Angeles, May 28, 2021; CA State Auditor Recommendation Responses
December 2020 – March 2021 — Accellion Data Breach (UC Systemwide); Class-Action Settlement
FieldDetail
DescriptionOn December 24, 2020, UC's Accellion File Transfer Appliance was targeted in an international cyberattack exploiting a zero-day vulnerability. Over 100 organizations were affected worldwide. On March 29, 2021, UC identified stolen data posted on the dark web. Compromised information at UCI included full names, addresses, telephone numbers, Social Security numbers, driver's license information, passport information, financial information (bank routing and account numbers), health and disability information, and birthdates. Responses to the 2020 UC Undergraduate Experience Survey (UCUES) and alleged medical records were also affected. A class-action lawsuit (Erazo v. The Regents of the University of California) was filed and resulted in a settlement. The UCI Registrar published notice directing the campus community to systemwide breach resources.
Applicable Law / ReportingCalifornia Information Practices Act (Civil Code §1798 et seq.); HIPAA (if medical records affected); Clery Act (campus security infrastructure obligations)
Compliance StatusN/A FOR POST Data breach, not law enforcement misconduct. Included to document institutional data security failures affecting the campus community that UCIPD serves.
Source(s)UCI News, April 9, 2021; New University, April 26, 2021; UCnet Accellion FAQ; BusinessWire/UC Notice of Data Breach, May 10, 2021

May 15, 2024 — Gaza Encampment Clearance: 47 Arrests, 16 Agencies, Largest Police Response in UCI History; Body Camera Footage Refused
FieldDetail
DescriptionAt approximately 2:30 PM on May 15, 2024, following a scheduled rally attended by a crowd of approximately 500, protesters expanded from the encampment and entered Physical Sciences Lecture Hall. A protest leader stated: "We are taking this building and we are remaining here until Gillman listens to our demands and drops the suspensions." A small group barricaded inside using electrical cords. Banners were hung from the balcony. Protesters were observed wearing goggles, helmets, gloves, and other protective gear. UCIPD issued a mutual aid call. AT LEAST 16 LAW ENFORCEMENT AGENCIES responded, including the OC Sheriff's Department and Irvine PD, with hundreds of officers deployed on campus. UCI issued a shelter-in-place alert, characterizing the situation as a "violent protest." All classes were canceled.

Documented timeline of enforcement action: Approximately 4:00 PM — officers begin detaining individuals at perimeter. 5:57 PM — UCI orders building evacuation. 6:46 PM — officers advance. Approximately 6:50 PM — UAW 4811 authorizes a UC-wide strike. 7:00 PM — police rush protesters; multiple individuals arrested; one protester on a planter was grabbed by the legs and removed. 7:30 PM — scuffles described as "increasingly physical"; dozens arrested. 7:35 PM — protesters pushed to Infinity Fountain; water bottles thrown at officers. 8:10 PM — dispersal order issued. 8:33 PM — Aldrich Park cleared.

TOTAL: 47 INDIVIDUALS ARRESTED (27 students, UCI employees, and others). Arrested students placed on interim suspension of up to 14 days. Chancellor Gillman issued a statement at 10:49 PM: "I was prepared to allow a peaceful encampment...My approach was consistent with the guidelines of UC's Robinson/Edley Report." He stated that the building occupation meant "this was no longer a situation that could be handled without police intervention."

CAIR (Council on American-Islamic Relations) designated UCI a "hostile campus" following the police action. Faculty statements from Film & Media Studies and Humanities departments criticized "a pattern of administrative over-reliance on police." As of June 3, 2025, UCI denied requests for body camera footage from the encampment clearance, stating the investigation "remained active."
Applicable Law / ReportingAB 48 (PC §§13652, 13652.1) — if crowd-control weapons were used, reporting mandatory within 60 days of deployment; POST use-of-force reporting standards; Clery Act (timely warnings, emergency notifications); Mutual aid documentation requirements across 16+ agencies
Compliance StatusBODY CAMERA FOOTAGE NOT RELEASED — SEE INCIDENT 11 FOR AB 48 REPORTING STATUS Body camera footage from the May 15, 2024 encampment clearance was not released as of June 3, 2025 — UCI stated the investigation "remained active," more than 12 months after the underlying event. Whether the operational circumstances of this clearance met the statutory trigger thresholds for AB 748 (PC §832.18 — officer-involved shooting or use of force causing great bodily injury) has not been independently confirmed in available public records. AB 48 reporting compliance status for this agency is documented separately in Incident 11, confirmed by LAist investigation of September 12, 2025.

September–October 2024 — OC DA Todd Spitzer Files Criminal Charges Against 50 Protesters Including 2 Professors
FieldDetail
DescriptionOn September 18, 2024, Orange County DA Todd Spitzer filed misdemeanor charges against 10 protesters (including 4 students and 2 faculty). On October 16, 2024, an additional 39 charges were filed. September 12, 2025 — AB 48 Non-Compliance Confirmed by LAist Investigation
FieldDetail
DescriptionThe LAist investigation published September 12, 2025 confirmed that UC Irvine was among 9 of 10 UC campus police departments that failed to comply with the AB 48 crowd-control weapons reporting requirements. Only UCLA appeared on the California DOJ's statewide AB 48 reporting webpage. POST spokesperson Meagan Poulos acknowledged there was "no stated enforcement mechanism" for AB 48. Former Assemblywoman Lorena Garcia, who authored the legislation, stated: "If we don't have enforcement, do we really have a law?" No UCI statement or identified corrective action regarding AB 48 non-compliance was found in available public records.
Applicable Law / ReportingAB 48 (PC §§13652, 13652.1) — mandatory reporting of crowd-control weapon use during protests. Applies to all law enforcement agencies regardless of size.
Compliance StatusNOT COMPLIANT — CONFIRMED Confirmed by LAist, September 12, 2025. No UCI response or corrective action identified in available public records.
Source(s)LAist, September 12, 2025 — laist.com/news/criminal-justice/uc-campus-police-protest-reporting-law; LAist — laist.com/news/politics/california-weapons-protests-reporting-requirements
Key Finding — Dual Non-Compliance in Body Camera and Crowd-Control Reporting: UCIPD has documented body camera footage refusals in at least two separate incidents — the February 2020 Chamndany arrest and the May 2024 encampment clearance — and confirmed AB 48 non-compliance for the 2024 encampment. Both refusals were confirmed by independent investigative reporting. The stated justification for the 2024 refusal — that the investigation "remained active" — was documented by LAist as of June 3, 2025, more than 12 months after the underlying event.

Achieved IACLEA accreditation for UCIPD in December 2024 (93% of optional standards met; announced publicly January 15, 2025).
