Note to Investigators, Attorneys, and Journalists

This campus dossier contains enforcement-level detail not summarized in the main chapters of this series. It documents the complete incident history, POST compliance status, financial exposure, and leadership record for the UC San Diego Police Department. These documents are intended for POST investigators, attorneys, civil rights organizations, journalists, and other parties requiring granular evidentiary detail for enforcement, litigation, or investigative purposes. All source citations are independently verifiable. Readers are encouraged to draw their own conclusions from the documented record.

JurisdictionPrimary jurisdiction: UCSD campus including more than 1,200 acres and 900 buildings, plus the one-mile area surrounding exterior boundaries of each campus. Officers have peace officer authority anywhere in the State of California per Penal Code §830.2(b). Education Code §92600 specifies authority upon: (a) campuses and one mile from exterior boundaries; (b) other grounds or properties owned, operated, controlled or administered by the Regents; and (c) as provided in Penal Code §830.2. Officers receive same basic training as city and county peace officers plus additional campus-specific training.[40] police.ucsd.edu/about/authority
Concurrent JurisdictionUC San Diego Police shares concurrent jurisdiction with local law enforcement agencies. The San Diego County Sheriff's Department has concurrent jurisdiction upon all university-owned or -controlled properties located within the county. The City of San Diego Police Department has concurrent jurisdiction on all campuses and upon all university-owned or -controlled properties located within that city.[40] police.ucsd.edu/about/authority
UC-Wide Mutual AidPursuant to Chapter 13 of the UC Universitywide Police Policies and Administrative Procedures, UCPD staff and equipment from one or more campuses can travel to another UC campus to support that campus' police department with both planned and unplanned events and occurrences. The Chief of Police at the host campus is primarily responsible for initiating UC-wide mutual aid requests. Requests typically made when: civil unrest is anticipated, special campus events are planned, local emergencies occur, or host campus resources deemed insufficient. The Coordinator of UC Police Services must be notified. Host campus reimburses guest campus for direct costs including overtime pay, travel expenses, equipment damage/loss, and consumable supplies.[40][39] police.ucsd.edu/about/authority; UC Universitywide Police Policies Chapter 13
California State Mutual AidUC Police, as a state law enforcement agency, participates in the California State Law Enforcement Mutual Aid Plan issued under Government Code §§8550, 8569, 8615–8619, 8668. The Sheriff of San Diego County serves as the Operational Area (OA) Law Enforcement Coordinator. When requested to provide mutual aid, a reasonable response consists of up to 50% of available on-duty uniformed personnel. Mutual aid may not be used for labor strikes except in event of violence. Pre-planned events (concerts, parades) are NOT mutual aid — those use MOUs and Joint Powers Agreements.[40] police.ucsd.edu/about/authority
Transformational Policing InitiativeUC San Diego continues its Transformational Policing Initiative. Described as a cultural shift that promotes justice and equity, fostering trust, collaborative community relationships and empowering all officers to make positive change. Complements and aligns with: (1) the 58 recommendations encompassed in the six pillars of the 2015 U.S. President's Task Force on 21st Century Policing; (2) the 28 recommendations of the 2019 University of California Presidential Task Force on Policing report; and (3) the UC Systemwide Community Safety Plan. UCSD PD states it has adopted policies, programs and practices aligned with the tenets of transformational policing.[53][47][51] police.ucsd.edu/about/transformational
IACLEA AccreditationACCREDITED. UCSD PD holds IACLEA accreditation. Among 6 UC campuses accredited as of January 2025 (UCB, UCD, UCI, UCSD, UCSF, UCSB). UCSC achieved accreditation October 2025, bringing total to 7 of 10. Panic button testing required quarterly as part of IACLEA accreditation standards.[27][28] police.ucsd.edu/about/security
CALEA AccreditationNOT ACCREDITED. Only UCSF PD holds CALEA accreditation among UC system police departments.[27]
Community Service Officers (CSO)Student employee program providing safety escorts from sunset to sunrise, building checks, and general citizen assistance. CSOs serve as additional "eyes and ears" of the department. Safety escorts available to all UCSD students, staff and faculty. Chief Rose noted program's origin: "The year before I started working there, it was called Joe's Escort Service and it was run by a group of frat guys who saw a need in the community." University formalized program under UCSD PD.[29][48] police.ucsd.edu/about/cso
University Safety Officials (USO)UCSD PD partners with Housing·Dining·Hospitality to offer USO Program. USOs are department employees (not police officers) who complete a civilian field-training program including cultural diversity and inclusion, conflict resolution, crisis intervention and mental health support. Two USOs assigned to residential areas of each college from 8 PM to 6 AM daily. They patrol parking areas, make building checks, and serve as liaisons with Resident Advisors.[29] police.ucsd.edu/about/uso
Complaint ProcessAll citizen and anonymous complaints promptly and thoroughly investigated. Chief of Police or designee reviews complaint and assigns investigator (unless complaint is about Chief, then complaint goes to Chief's manager). Written notification of disposition provided within 30 days per California Penal Code §832.7(e)(1). Anonymous reporting available via P3 Global Intel Tip Management Software (online and mobile app). Department states it "welcomes constructive criticism and valid complaints about the conduct of the department, its officers or its operations."[38] police.ucsd.edu/contact/complaint
Police Accountability Board (PAB)Established pursuant to UC Community Safety Plan. PAB impartially reviews investigative reports related to allegations of police misconduct and makes recommendations in a timely manner regarding complaints filed by members of the public against UCSD PD. Also solicits information and input through public forums. May make policy, procedure and training recommendations. Anyone affected by UCSD PD may file complaint regardless of citizenship or UC affiliation.[35][51] pab.ucsd.edu
Body-Worn CamerasImplemented per UC systemwide BWC policy (Chapter 15 of UC Universitywide Police Policies and Administrative Procedures). Requires sworn personnel from lieutenant through officer to wear BWV systems while on duty, with activation required prior to initiating any criminal investigative or enforcement activity involving a member of the public. San Diego City Attorney's Office referenced review of "substantial body-worn camera footage" in May 2024 Gaza encampment case.[39][18] UC Universitywide Police Policies Chapter 15; SD Union-Tribune Oct 31, 2024
Union RepresentationFederated University Police Officers' Association (FUPOA) under PA unit collective bargaining agreement, same as all 10 UC campus police departments. FUPOA filed PERB petition SF-RR-1052-H in 2025 to expand representation to include sergeants. 2026 successor bargaining notice issued by UC.[44][54] federateduniversitypoa.org/uc-san-diego
Triton COREMobile response team at UC San Diego providing compassionate intervention and support to students experiencing behavioral health crisis. Composed of licensed behavioral health professionals for mobile crisis response, behavioral health wellness checks, and in-person or telehealth assessment. Predecessor: UCSD PD and CAPS piloted Psychiatric Emergency Response Team (PERT) in 2021 under Chief Rose's Transformative Policing Initiative.[29][54] tritoncore.ucsd.edu
Ride-Along ProgramUC San Diego students, faculty and staff can ride along with a police officer performing campus patrol duties. Participants must sign waiver and pass online law enforcement records check.[29] police.ucsd.edu/engagement/ride-along

Pre-Mandatory Disclosure Era — Events Prior to January 1, 2019 (SB 1421 Effective Date)
February 15, 2010 COMPTON COOKOUT RACIAL INCIDENT — Off-campus party organized by several UCSD fraternity members (Pi Kappa Alpha and others) dubbed the "Compton Cookout," intended to mock Black History Month. Facebook invitation urged attendees to "experience the various elements of life in the ghetto" and dress in stereotypical attire. Event gained national media attention. While off-campus and not directly a UCSD PD enforcement matter, the incident triggered campus-wide racial crisis requiring extensive police response for subsequent protests. UCSD had the smallest percentage of African American students in the nine-campus UC system at the time (approximately 1.3% of 20,000 undergraduates). Title IV & Title VI, Civil Rights Act of 1964; UC Principles of Community N/A — Campus climate event, not officer misconduct Pre-SB 1421 Triggered "Black Winter" — series of racially charged incidents including noose found in Geisel Library, KKK hood placed on Dr. Seuss statue, racial slur broadcast on student TV. Black Student Union declared "state of emergency." Led to occupation of Chancellor Fox's office by approximately 300 students/staff/faculty on February 26, 2010. BSU presented 32 demands.
February 25, 2010 NOOSE FOUND IN GEISEL LIBRARY — A noose was discovered hanging from a bookcase in UCSD's main library (Geisel Library) during the ongoing racial crisis following the Compton Cookout. Students stormed and occupied Chancellor Mary Anne Fox's office for six hours. UCSD PD responded to secure campus. CA Penal Code §422.6 (hate crimes); Title VI, Civil Rights Act of 1964 N/A — Criminal investigation matter Pre-SB 1421 Student responsible for noose identified. Incident contributed to national media coverage and calls for reform. BSU demanded vigorous recruitment of underrepresented groups and reapportionment of resources.
March 2010 KKK HOOD ON DR. SEUSS STATUE — A Ku Klux Klan hood was found placed on the head of the Dr. Seuss (Theodor Geisel) statue outside Geisel Library, marking the fourth racially charged on-campus incident during Winter Quarter 2010. CA Penal Code §422.6 (hate crimes) N/A — Criminal investigation matter Pre-SB 1421 Further intensified campus racial crisis. BSU and UCSD administration eventually reached agreement to work together. Actions included: creation of DEI undergraduate requirement; creation of student resource centers; permanent installation of the Chicano Legacy Mural.
April 12, 2012 DOJ/DOE SETTLEMENT — RACIAL HARASSMENT — UC San Diego reached settlement with U.S. Departments of Justice and Education over February 2010 racial incidents. Settlement arose from investigation over complaints of racial harassment against African-American students in violation of Titles IV and VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Under terms: UCSD agreed to revise campus policies and procedures related to racial harassment; maintain Office for the Prevention of Harassment and Discrimination (OPHD); provide mandatory training; take steps to prevent racial harassment, respond appropriately to harassment that occurs, and eliminate hostile environment. Title IV & Title VI, Civil Rights Act of 1964; DOJ/DOE resolution agreement N/A — Federal compliance matter Pre-SB 1421 Settlement reached. UCSD implemented required reforms including revised policies, mandatory training, and enhanced complaint infrastructure.
2012 UC UNDERGRADUATE EXPERIENCE SURVEY (UCUES) — POST-COMPTON COOKOUT — Survey specifically examined campus climate before and after 2010 Compton Cookout. Found that Black students felt both undervalued and disrespected compared to every other group on campus. Black students would be less likely to re-enroll than every other measured group. During 2010 protests, many Black students passed around forms to withdraw or transfer. The 2014 and 2016 UCUES surveys removed questions that documented attitudes of Black students, making ongoing climate assessment more difficult. Title VI, Civil Rights Act of 1964; UC institutional research N/A — Institutional assessment Pre-SB 1421 Survey documented ongoing harm. Removal of relevant survey questions in 2014 and 2016 hindered ability to track campus climate progress.
January 2014 OFFICER JAMES STEVÉ TRANSFERS FROM UCSB TO UCSD PD — Black police officer James Stevé transferred from UC Santa Barbara police force to UCSD PD due to discrimination he faced at UCSB. His subsequent experiences at UCSD PD would form the basis of a federal lawsuit filed in 2021. Title VII, Civil Rights Act of 1964; California FEHA; CA Whistleblower Protection Act Unknown — Complaint involves UCSD PD internal culture Pre-SB 1421 Stevé later filed federal lawsuit against UC Regents and UCSD in July 2021.
2015–2018 STEVÉ ALLEGATIONS OF RACIAL DISCRIMINATION AND RETALIATION — According to filed complaint, Officer James Stevé experienced systematic racism, sexism, and homophobia within UCSD PD. Specific allegations: Captain Morris constantly followed him while at work; unmarked UCSD PD patrol vehicles observed outside his home without notification; in 2018, Stevé removed from Field Training Program by Chief Dave Rose and Sergeant Jones — "not due to merit, but rather because of Sergeant Jones and Chief Rose's personal reasons" — resulting in 5% income reduction. Stevé attempted to file numerous complaints. Claims UC Office of the President, UCSD, and UCSD PD did not investigate any of his reports. Attorney Darren Richie stated lawsuit stemmed from "systematic issues within UCSD PD." Title VII, Civil Rights Act of 1964; CA FEHA; CA Labor Code §1102.5; First Amendment Unknown — Filed complaints with OPHD and UC Labor Relations; claims UCOP, UCSD, and UCSD PD did not investigate any of his reports Pre-SB 1421 / Post for later events Stevé attempted to file numerous complaints; claims were not investigated. Led to federal lawsuit.

January 1, 2019 – May 2020 SB 1421 NON-COMPLIANCE — ZERO RECORDS DISCLOSED — UCSD PD identified as one of six UC campus police departments providing NO records in response to SB 1421 public records requests, per Voice of San Diego investigation published May 12, 2020. UC Irvine professor Emily Owens stated she believes "it's likely easier for police departments within university systems to evade the level of scrutiny that city agencies face." UCSD spokesperson Leslie Sepuka stated UCSD "seeks to enforce fairness, cooperation, and professionalism." May 5, 2024 CHANCELLOR KHOSLA STATEMENT — ENCAMPMENT DECLARED "ILLEGAL" — Chancellor Pradeep Khosla issued public statement calling encampment "illegal" and stating it posed "unacceptable safety and security hazard on campus." May 6, 2024
~5:30–5:45 AM		 POLICE RAID ON GAZA ENCAMPMENT — UNLAWFUL ASSEMBLY DECLARED — At approximately 5:30 AM, dozens of officers in riot gear from UCSD Police, California Highway Patrol, and San Diego County Sheriff's Department arrived at encampment. Key Finding — AB 48 vs. Clery Act Divergence: UCSD PD demonstrates a documented divergence in reporting compliance: the department maintains full compliance with the federal Clery Act (annual campus security report published, no audit findings or fines identified), while simultaneously failing to comply with California's AB 48 crowd-control weapons reporting mandate. During the May 6, 2024 encampment clearance, baton use was documented by multiple media outlets during post-clearance confrontations, and pepper spray was specifically deployed by San Diego County Sheriff's Department (SDSO) deputies — documented by video evidence and named witnesses — in an operation for which UCSD PD served as the host agency and coordinating authority on its own property. No AB 48 report was filed with the California DOJ by UCSD PD. The September 2025 LAist investigation confirmed UCSD PD among 9 of 10 UC campus police departments not listed on the CA DOJ AB 48 reporting website. This divergence documents a pattern in which federally mandated reporting obligations are met while California's state-mandated protest-response transparency requirements are not.
