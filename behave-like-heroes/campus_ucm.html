November 4, 2015 — Stabbing Attack and Officer-Involved Shooting (Ruled Justified)
FieldDetail
DescriptionFaisal Mohammad, 18, a first-year student from Santa Clara, armed with a hunting knife with an 8–10 inch blade, attacked four people in the Classroom and Office Building beginning at approximately 7:53 AM.

Victims (all survived): (1) A male student whose throat was slashed in a classroom. (2) Byron Price, 31, a construction worker, stabbed in the left side requiring 9 staples. (3) A second male student stabbed in a stairway. (4) Danielle Quiroga, a staff academic adviser, stabbed twice in the chest and back — resulting in a collapsed lung, nicked diaphragm, airlifted to Modesto, and 5 days of hospitalization.

Student Tyler Patton intervened by attacking Mohammad with furniture, disrupting the assault. Construction worker Byron Price pushed open a barricaded door and intervened directly.

Officers Olaf Lopez and Kevin Warkentin, responding to a nearby fire alarm, received radio traffic from Dispatcher Cook. On the Scholar’s Lane bridge, approximately 10 feet away, Mohammad turned toward officers with knife at shoulder height, ignored commands, and advanced in a lunging motion. Officer Lopez fired two shots. Mohammad was declared dead at the scene.

Mohammad’s backpack contained: zip-tie handcuffs, petroleum jelly, a night-vision scope, a safety hammer, duct tape, and a photocopy of an ISIS flag. A two-page 31-step handwritten plan found during autopsy included names of targets, instructions to say “Bismillah,” a plan to bind students, steal an officer’s firearm, shoot students in dormitories, and behead someone.

The FBI Sacramento field office stated Mohammad “may have self-radicalized and drawn inspiration from terrorist propaganda” but was not directed by any external organization. Contemporaneous reporting, including CNN (March 18, 2016), described this as among a series of ISIS-inspired attacks in the U.S. at that time. The FBI did not formally classify the event as a terrorist act. The 2015 attack was subsequently included on a list of 78 incidents released by the Trump White House on February 6, 2017, characterized in that release as underreported terrorist acts. Multiple news organizations, including NPR and PolitiFact, independently noted that the UC Merced attack had in fact received media coverage.
Applicable Law / ReportingPOST use-of-force reporting (OIS, fatal); PC §13652 (use-of-force reporting); SB 1421 (OIS records disclosure, retroactive); Merced County DA review required; FBI Joint Terrorism Task Force jurisdiction
Compliance StatusCOMPLIANT Merced County DA Larry D. Morse II ruled the use of force JUSTIFIED on December 7, 2015. SB 1421 records from this officer-involved shooting were subsequently released to Voice of San Diego — one of only two OIS records released by any UC campus. Officer Lopez was named Police Officer of the Year.
Source(s)FBI — initial statement; FBI — investigation update, March 17, 2016; CapRadio — DA justified, December 7, 2015; CNN — ISIS-inspired characterization, March 18, 2016; NBC News — suspect identified; KMPH — Trump administration list reference; NPR — White House list, February 6, 2017

FieldDetail

May 12–17, 2024 — Gaza Solidarity Encampment: Zero Arrests, Zero Force Used
FieldDetail
DescriptionUC Merced Students for Justice in Palestine (SJP) established a “Gaza Solidarity Encampment” at the Wallace Dutra Amphitheater beginning May 12, 2024 — timed with the UC Board of Regents’ first-ever three-day meeting held at UC Merced (May 14–16). The encampment began with approximately 12 people and swelled to over 100 protesters by May 15, including supporters who traveled from other UC campuses.

Encampment demands: immediate ceasefire; UC divestment from Israel; academic boycott of Israeli institutions; amnesty for all protesters systemwide; and reinvestment toward student needs and Palestine humanitarian aid.

UC Merced officials stated they “did not plan to actively disperse the encampment, as long as it remained peaceful.” The university acknowledged the encampment violated campus policy but maintained open communication with organizers. On May 15, some protesters were escorted from a Regents’ committee session, reportedly by California Highway Patrol. On May 16, Regents Keith Ellis and Merhawi Tesfai sat down directly with protesters at the encampment site.

UC Merced officials confirmed: “There were no instances of violence and no arrests were made.” The outcome stands in documented contrast to other UC campuses during the same period: UCLA (210+ arrests, stun grenades, tear gas); UCSD (64 arrests, pepper spray); UCI (47 arrests, 16 agencies).
Applicable Law / ReportingAB 48 — no crowd-control weapons used, no reporting obligation triggered; First Amendment assembly rights; Clery Act (campus safety assessment); AB 481 military equipment not deployed
Compliance StatusN/A — NO FORCE USED, NO ARRESTS No POST reporting obligation triggered. University officials confirmed zero arrests and zero use of force during the May 12–17, 2024 encampment. No AB 48 crowd-control weapon reporting obligation was triggered; no AB 481 military equipment was deployed.
Source(s)KVPR — passions flare, May 15, 2024; KVPR — Regents sit down, May 16, 2024; ABC30 — encampment underway; GV Wire — Regents and protesters, May 17, 2024; Merced County Times — protesters set up camp; Indybay — encampment demands, May 12, 2024
Key Finding — UC Merced Spring 2024 Encampment Outcome: University officials confirmed that no arrests were made and no force was used during the May 2024 Gaza solidarity encampment at UC Merced. The university’s stated policy — permitting peaceful encampments while maintaining open communication with organizers — produced an outcome without incident. For factual context, documented outcomes at other UC campuses during the same spring 2024 period include: UCLA (210+ arrests); UCSD (64 arrests, pepper spray used); UCI (47 arrests, 16 agencies deployed). This documented contrast is presented without editorial commentary for the reader’s independent assessment.

(PC §832.7)
Effective January 1, 2019		 Requires release of records relating to officer-involved shootings, use of force resulting in great bodily injury or death, sustained sexual assault findings, and sustained dishonesty findings.
