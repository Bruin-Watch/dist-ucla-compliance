The Clothes Have No Emperor
They Have Regents Instead

The Constitutional Architecture of the UC Regents — A Fourth Branch of Government With Full Powers, No Oversight, and Its Own Police

MEET THE REGENTS OF THE UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA

In Chapter 1 Officer 341 documented what happened inside the UCLA Police Department over thirty-seven years — the whistleblowing, the retaliation, the fabricated investigations, the exhaustion of every available institutional channel. This chapter explains why none of it worked.

The answer is not corruption.
It is not incompetence.
It is not bad luck.

The answer is constitutional architecture — a legal structure created in 1879, ratified by the People of California, and reinforced by 147 years of case law that makes the University of California functionally immune from external oversight.

Every fact in this chapter is sourced from the California Constitution, published case law, state statutes, and official legislative analyses. Readers are encouraged to verify each citation independently.

The Regents of the University of California are NOT:

NOT a state agency.
NOT a department.
NOT a board appointed to carry out legislative policy.

The Regents of the University of California ARE:

A constitutional corporation — the highest form of legal person known to California law.
Created not by the Legislature but by the People of California directly, through the state constitution.

A constitutional corporation administering a public trust — the terms the California Constitution itself uses in Article IX, § 9. The courts have variously characterized the Regents as a “constitutional department or function of the state government” (Hamilton v. Regents, 293 U.S. 245 (1934)), “a public corporation,” “a governmental agency,” and “a public entity” (Regents v. Superior Court, 3 Cal.3d 529 (1970)). No single statutory term fits — because none of them was built for an institution created directly by the People.

I. What Is a Constitutional Corporation?

In 1868, the California Legislature passed the Organic Act establishing the University of California and creating its Board of Regents. The Organic Act explicitly declared that no Regent “shall be deemed a public officer” and that the appointment “shall be held and deemed to be exclusively a private trust.” In other words: private trustees running a public institution with public money — and the law says they are not public officers.

Controversy over legislative control of the University led to a decisive battle at the 1879 Constitutional Convention. The result was Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution, which established the University as a constitutionally created public trust with “full powers of organization and government, subject only to such legislative control as may be necessary to insure compliance with the terms of the endowment of the University and the security of its funds.”

Read that language again. Full powers of organization and government. Not partial. Not advisory. Not subject to legislative approval. Full.

What the Courts Have Said:
•  The U.S. Supreme Court ruled in Hamilton v. Regents of the University of California (1934) 293 U.S. 245 that the Regents’ orders carry the force of state statutes. Not rules. Not regulations. Statutes.
•  The California Attorney General’s 1957 Opinion concluded that the Regents “constitute a branch of the state government equal and coordinate with the legislative, the judiciary, and the executive branches.” A fourth branch. Created not by legislators but by the People directly.
•  In People v. Lofchie (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 240, 262, the Court of Appeal held that the University’s constitutional autonomy is “greater than that of charter cities.”

The Regents own the University — not metaphorically. They hold legal title to all UC property. They make law. They create police departments. They set wages. They govern 246,000 employees across ten campuses, five medical centers, and three national laboratories, with a combined annual budget exceeding $51 billion. They are simultaneously the owner, the government, and the law — what legal historians call proprietary government — a concept most scholars believed had been extinct in America since the colonial era.

The only authority that sits above the Regents is the California Constitution itself — changeable only by the People — and the United States Constitution. Everything else — every statute, every regulation, every state agency — sits below them.

No other employer in California holds these exemptions. Not the State itself. Not the CSU system. Not county governments. Not charter cities. The California Professional Firefighters stated in their support letter for ACA 6 that UC is “the only employer in the state” exempt from basic labor standards such as minimum wage, overtime pay, equal pay, sick leave, and more.

ACA 6, authored by Assemblymember Matt Haney, was a proposed constitutional amendment that would have required the UC Regents to conform to the same labor, employment, and occupational safety standards as other public agencies. It failed in the Senate Committee on Elections and Constitutional Amendments. The only path to fixing this — a constitutional amendment — could not survive a committee vote.

The University of California is not the only constitutionally autonomous university in America. Three states have the strongest judicial recognition of university constitutional autonomy:

California’s Regents can ignore California’s. The Gomez decision is unique in American higher education law. No other university in the country has obtained a court ruling exempting it from state minimum wage, overtime, meal breaks, paid sick leave, and PAGA — simultaneously.

IV. What Happens When a Peace Officer Works for a Private Government?

Under Education Code § 92600, the Regents are “authorized and empowered to appoint one or more persons to be members of the University of California police department as such police department is constituted on September 19, 1947, or may thereafter be constituted.” That last phrase — or may thereafter be constituted — grants open-ended authority. The Regents can constitute the department however they see fit, whenever they see fit.

Under Penal Code § 830.2(b), UC police officers are classified as full peace officers — the same classification as CHP officers. They carry the same badge, the same gun, the same POST certification, the same authority to use deadly force, and the same personal liability under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. On paper, a UC police officer is identical to any municipal cop in California.

On paper. The reality is a different universe:

The Paradox: UC police officers hold Penal Code § 830.2 authority — full peace officer status with statewide jurisdiction. But they work inside an institution that is constitutionally exempt from the labor protections that every other peace officer in California receives. All of the responsibility. None of the safety net. That is the structural trap. And Chapter 1 documented what happens to the officer who tries to report what he finds inside it.

V. The Legal Hierarchy — How a Statute Cannot Command the Constitution That Created the Regents

The hierarchy of law in California is:

Level Authority Examples
1United States ConstitutionSupreme law of the land
2State ConstitutionArt. IX, §9 — The Regents sit here
3StatutesPenal Code §§ 830.2, 832, 13510; Education Code § 92600 — POST sits here
4RegulationsCreated by agencies under statutory authority
5PoliciesInternal rules of organizations

The Regents sit at Level 2. They derive their power directly from the California Constitution. POST, the Penal Code, the Education Code — all of those sit at Level 3. They are legislative creations. Statutes. They flow down from the Constitution through the Legislature.

Statutes cannot command a constitutional entity. That is the entire principle. The Legislature cannot tell the Regents how to organize their government any more than the Legislature can tell the Judiciary how to run its courts. The Regents do not participate in POST because Penal Code § 13510 requires them to. They participate because they choose to.

Penal Code § 832 is a statutory framework that flows down from the Constitution to the Legislature to the agencies. The Regents operate at the constitutional level. They do not receive authority from § 832 or § 830.2. Those statutes merely recognize what the Regents already have the constitutional power to create.

The Untested Question: The Regents could train their officers however they want. Badge them however they want. Arm them however they want. Discipline them however they want. Because “full powers of organization and government” means exactly that. If the Regents decided tomorrow to create their own training standards and walk away from POST entirely, who stops them? The Legislature? Cannot override the Constitution. POST? A statutory body with voluntary participation trying to regulate a constitutional one. The courts? They have spent 147 years siding with the Regents on internal affairs. The fact that the Regents have not walked away from POST is not because they cannot. It is because they have not needed to. POST has never given them a reason to — because POST has never actually enforced anything against them. Law — without enforcement.

Government Code § 8547.10 — the Reporting of Improper Governmental Activities Act — demonstrates the circularity. As documented in Chapter 1, Badge #341 filed a formal complaint under this statute in July 1994. The statute required the University to reach a decision regarding the complaint “within the time limits established by the Regents.” The statute delegated the investigation back to the Regents themselves, on the Regents’ own timeline. The fox investigates the henhouse on the fox’s schedule. The documented record does not reflect that the University ever reached the required decision or notified him of any investigation.

VI. Only Three Authorities Can Reach the Regents — Everyone Else Hits a Wall

If the Regents sit at Level 2 of the legal hierarchy, then the only authorities that can reach them are those at Level 2 or above. The complete list of entities with legal authority over the UC police is exactly three:

Door 1 — The Regents Themselves (Level 2). The Regents can reform their own police departments at any time. They have “full powers of organization and government.” They do not need legislation, voter approval, or outside intervention. They simply need to decide to act. The entire documented record — from 1989 to 2026 — establishes that they have not.

Door 2 — The People of California, Through a Constitutional Amendment (Level 2). Article XVIII of the California Constitution allows amendments proposed by two-thirds of the Legislature and ratified by a majority of voters. ACA 6 was precisely this mechanism — a proposed constitutional amendment to require UC to follow the same labor standards as every other public employer. It failed in committee. The only structural remedy available through the democratic process could not survive a committee vote.

Door 3 — The Federal Government (Level 1). The U.S. Constitution sits above the state constitution. Three federal mechanisms can reach the Regents regardless of Article IX, §9:

•  The U.S. Department of Justice under 34 U.S.C. § 12601 — pattern-or-practice investigations of law enforcement agencies.
•  The U.S. Department of Education under the Clery Act (20 U.S.C. § 1092(f)) — campus safety and reporting requirements, with federal funding as the enforcement lever.
•  The federal courts under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 and the Fourteenth Amendment — individual civil rights claims against officers and institutions acting under color of state law.

That is the complete list. Not POST. Not the Legislature. Not the Governor. Not the District Attorney. Not the California Department of Justice. Not Cal/OSHA. Not the Bureau of State Audits. Not the Little Hoover Commission. Every one of those entities is a statutory creation at Level 3 or below. They can investigate. They can report. They can recommend. But they cannot compel the Regents to do anything, because statutes cannot command upward against a constitutional entity. Chapter 1 documented an officer who filed with every one of those agencies. Not a single one took enforcement action against the University of California. Now you know why.

VII. In Defense of Officers Trapped Inside the UC Corporation

This chapter has documented a constitutional fortress — an institution with lawmaking power, tax immunity, labor law immunity, self-created police departments, and no external accountability mechanism with actual enforcement authority.

This chapter is not an attack on the officers who serve inside that system. It is a defense of them.

The same structural wall that prevents outside agencies from holding UC accountable also traps the people inside. An officer who discovers misconduct — ticket-fixing, fabricated investigations, racism in the command staff, theft of university property — has nowhere to go. He can file a grievance. The grievance goes to university administrators. He can report to the Chancellor. The Chancellor reports to the Regents. He can complain to POST. POST is a statutory body trying to regulate a constitutional entity. He can file with the DA, the FBI, the Bureau of State Audits, the California DOJ. Not one of them can compel the Regents to act.

That is not a theoretical observation. That is the documented record of Officer 341’s thirty-seven-year experience, laid out in the preceding chapter with grievance numbers, complaint dates, agency contacts, and institutional responses. Every channel was used. Every channel is documented. And not a single external agency took enforcement action against the University of California.

The commanders who carried out the retaliation documented in Chapter 1 were themselves subject to the same institutional pressures that made whistleblowing career suicide. The lieutenant who was demoted for sexual and racial harassment operated inside a system where the chief above him sold police vehicles for cash and the institution above the chief was constitutionally immune from the agencies that might have intervened. The captain who concluded the whistleblower was lying — before a tape recording proved otherwise — operated inside a system where contradicting the command staff meant becoming the next target. An officer who objected to what command staff was doing had two choices: comply or become the next target. Many complied.

Officer 341 did not. He won every grievance he filed. The University’s response to losing each proceeding was escalation rather than compliance. He reported command staff misconduct to the UC President, to the Chancellor, to the DA, to the FBI, to the California DOJ, to the Bureau of State Audits, to the press. The University investigated and terminated the Chief of Police. Then they turned the machinery on the officer who reported it.

Thirty-seven years later, the same officer filed POST complaints against the next generation of UCLA PD command staff. The institution’s response has been identical. The same playbook. The same institution. The same wall.

The Point: This is not a chapter about what the Regents should do. It is a chapter about what the Regents can do — and why no one can stop them. The constitutional architecture documented here is the reason every remedial effort in Chapter 1 failed. It is the reason ACA 6 failed. It is the reason POST has never enforced anything against a UC police department. And it is the reason this report exists — because the only weapon left is the public record.

The Regents’ constitutional armor has one weakness: it only works in the dark. The moment the public understands that one institution in California operates as a fourth branch of government, exempt from labor law, exempt from tax law, exempt from zoning, with its own police force whose policies carry the force of state statutes, governed by appointed non-public officers serving twelve-year terms, accountable to literally no one — that is when a constitutional amendment stops dying in committee.

Nemo iudex in causa sua — no one shall be judge in his own cause. The principle proves the disease. Article IX, §9 proves the cure is structurally impossible through ordinary legislation. The only three doors of reform that remain are the Regents themselves, the People of California through a constitutional amendment, and the federal government.

This report is knocking on all three doors.
