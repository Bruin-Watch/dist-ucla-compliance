Note to Investigators, Attorneys, and Journalists

This campus dossier contains enforcement-level detail not summarized in the main chapters of this series. It documents the complete incident history, POST compliance status, financial exposure, and leadership record for the UC Riverside Police Department. These documents are intended for POST investigators, attorneys, civil rights organizations, journalists, and other parties requiring granular evidentiary detail for enforcement, litigation, or investigative purposes. All source citations are independently verifiable. Readers are encouraged to draw their own conclusions from the documented record.

Chronological Incident Log — All Documented Incidents Relevant to POST Oversight

All incidents are presented in complete chronological order. Pre-mandatory reporting era incidents are included to document patterns of conduct predating current mandates. Applicable laws and POST reporting requirements are identified for each incident.

Pre-Mandatory Reporting Era  April 30, 1993 — Racial Profiling Detention of Graduate Student Maurice Webb
FieldDetail
DescriptionTwo UCRPD officers stopped graduate student Maurice Webb, ordered him to drop his possessions, told him he “fit the description” of a suspect, and attempted to photograph him. The UC Riverside Highlander published an investigation on May 18, 1993. Managing Editor Edwin Ramoran connected the incident to parallel struggles at UCLA and UC Irvine, framing it as a systemic institutional pattern across the UC system. The original article predates online archives and exists in physical or microfilm form; UCR Library completed digitization of the full Highlander archive (since 1954) to the Calisphere database circa 2022.
Applicable Law / ReportingRIPA/AB 953 not enacted until 2015; UC PD data collection did not begin until January 2022. However, POST training standards on bias-based policing, 14th Amendment equal protection, UC Police Policies and Administrative Procedures, and PC §13519.4 (racial profiling prohibition) were applicable at the time.
Compliance StatusPRE-MANDATORY — NO REPORTING MECHANISM EXISTED Included to establish documented pattern of conduct predating current mandates.
Source(s)UC Riverside Highlander, May 18, 1993 (pre-digital; archived in Calisphere); Inside UCR, April 25, 2022 — insideucr.ucr.edu
Pre-Mandatory Reporting Era  January 19, 2012 — Pepper Balls Deployed at UC Regents Meeting Protest
FieldDetail
DescriptionApproximately 1,000 students protested tuition increases at a UC Board of Regents meeting held at UC Riverside. Protesters initiated a sit-in that displaced the Regents from their meeting room, then surrounded three senior UC administrators — Provost Lawrence Pitts, Senior VP Jack Stobo, and VP of Investments Marie Berggren — who were trapped in parking enforcement vehicles for over an hour. Approximately 100 UC police officers were reinforced by 100 additional officers from the Riverside County Sheriff’s Department and Riverside PD. UCRPD officers fired pepper balls at protesters. Student Anthony Lascano, 19, displayed wounds on both thighs and his right knee from the projectiles. Chief Mike Lane confirmed the use of pepper balls, stating they were deployed “to protect a fellow officer from getting seriously injured.” Three arrests were made, including two booked on felony assault-on-officer charges. Nine UC officers sustained minor injuries. The UCR Police Accountability Board subsequently confirmed this was the last time UCRPD used impact rounds from a launcher in any non-training environment.
Applicable Law / ReportingAB 48 not effective until January 1, 2022. However, PC §13652 (pre-AB 48 annual use-of-force reporting requirements) and POST use-of-force reporting guidelines were applicable. Mutual aid documentation requirements also applied across the two responding agencies.
Compliance StatusPRE-MANDATORY — AB 48 DID NOT YET EXIST No public record of POST or DOJ reporting located under then-applicable PC §13652 annual use-of-force reporting. Included to document pattern of crowd-control weapon use without confirmed reporting.
Source(s)The Press-Enterprise, January 19, 2012 — pressenterprise.com; Davis Vanguard, January 2012 — davisvanguard.org; UCR PAB — diversity.ucr.edu
Pre-Mandatory Reporting Era  April 4, 2013 — Systematic Racial Profiling of Black Students Following Armed Robberies
FieldDetail
DescriptionFollowing 13 campus-area armed robberies since January 2013, UCRPD increased patrols and hired private security. At a Chancellor’s Town Hall on April 4, 2013, African-American students reported systematic stops by campus police. Graduate student Aaron Jones, 27, described being approached by an officer while sitting in a car outside student apartments and asked “why we were there and if we were students.” Senior Philip Carroll-Johnson, 24, reported being stopped three times in four days, stating: “I’ve never experienced anything like this” in four years at UCR. Carroll-Johnson objected to university email alerts describing suspects as “two black men between 5’7” and 5’9” tall, wearing blue jeans and hoodies” — descriptions broad enough to encompass many African-American students. Graduate students Jerry Robertson and James Garrett also reported frequent stops without cause. Chief Mike Lane acknowledged all suspects in the robberies were African-American males but maintained that department policy barred racial profiling. This incident became the most frequently cited example of UCRPD racial profiling in subsequent reports, including the UAW 2865 “Policing in the UC” report (February 2021).
Applicable Law / ReportingRIPA/AB 953 enacted 2015 but UC PD data collection did not begin until January 2022. PC §13519.4 (racial profiling prohibition, enacted 2000) was applicable. POST training standards on bias-based policing and 14th Amendment equal protection were applicable.
Compliance StatusPRE-MANDATORY — RIPA DATA COLLECTION NOT YET REQUIRED No reporting mechanism for stop data existed at the time. Included to document documented pattern of conduct predating current mandates.
Source(s)The Press-Enterprise, April 4, 2013 — pressenterprise.com; UAW 2865, February 2021 (p.14, fn.59) — uaw2865.org
July 22–23, 2018 — Fatal Officer-Involved Shooting of Juan Garcia; SB 1421 Record Withheld
FieldDetail
DescriptionAt approximately 1:00 a.m. on July 22, 2018, multiple 911 calls reported shots fired in the 600 block of West Blaine Street, just outside UCR’s northern boundary. UCRPD patrol units arrived first and spotted Juan Garcia, 38, walking northbound on Watkins Drive. When officers attempted contact, Garcia “produced a firearm, and an officer-involved shooting occurred,” per Riverside PD spokesperson Officer Ryan Railsback. Garcia was found with two pistols and an improvised firearm. He was hospitalized in critical condition at Riverside Community Hospital and died at approximately 2:20 p.m. on July 23. No officers or bystanders were injured. Riverside Police Department investigated the shooting.

NO PUBLICLY AVAILABLE DISTRICT ATTORNEY RULING ON THIS SHOOTING WAS LOCATED IN AVAILABLE RECORDS — a documented transparency gap, as of the date of research for this chapter. This incident appears to be the single SB 1421 record that UCRPD acknowledged possessing but declined to release to Voice of San Diego in May 2020, citing the investigation as “still pending” — approximately two years after the shooting occurred.
Applicable Law / ReportingGovernment Code §12525.2 (DOJ reporting of officer-involved shootings — was already required at time of incident, July 2018); SB 1421 (effective January 1, 2019 — applied retroactively to existing records, requiring release upon taking effect); PC §832.7 (peace officer personnel records — OIS disclosure); AB 71 / PC §13012 (URSUS — Use of Force Incident Reporting System). NOTE: While SB 1421 took effect after the shooting, Government Code §12525.2 required DOJ notification at the time of the incident.
Compliance StatusNOT COMPLIANT — SB 1421 RECORD WITHHELD UCRPD acknowledged holding one SB 1421 record (identified as likely this incident) but refused to release it to Voice of San Diego in May 2020, citing a pending investigation — two years after the shooting. No DA ruling located in available public records as of the date of research for this chapter. Across the UC system, only two case files were released despite more than 200 recorded use-of-force incidents.
Source(s)MyNewsLA.com, July 23, 2018 — mynewsla.com; The Press-Enterprise, July 2018 — pressenterprise.com; Voice of San Diego, May 12, 2020 — voiceofsandiego.org; UAW 2865, February 2021 — uaw2865.org

2024 (Specific Date Not Publicly Disclosed) — Unreported Less-Lethal Force Deployment During Berkeley Mutual Aid; Lt. Day On-Record Admission of AB 48 Non-Compliance
FieldDetail
DescriptionA LAist investigation published September 12, 2025 revealed that a UCRPD officer deployed less-lethal projectiles “at a person who was assaulting officers” while providing mutual aid to UC Berkeley police during 2024 protests. The LAist article is framed entirely within the context of AB 48, which governs the use of less-lethal kinetic energy projectiles and chemical agents during protests and demonstrations — not traditional firearms discharges. A companion LAist article (Source 32) states explicitly: “UC Riverside is one of the nine UC campus police forces without public-facing reports on their use of chemical agents and less-lethal projectiles.” UCRPD Lieutenant Jason Day confirmed the incident to LAist and acknowledged that the department HAD NOT REPORTED the less-lethal force deployment to the California Department of Justice — a step mandated by AB 48, which requires reporting of crowd-control weapon use within 60 days of deployment. Lt. Day cited department capacity issues as the reason for non-reporting, stating: “A lot of times things are just going to have to get set aside.” Day also stated: “There’s more homework on my part that needs to be done.” UCRPD had requested more than $10,000 worth of less-lethal munitions in September 2024, including 40mm less-lethal projectile launchers, which are governed by AB 48 and AB 481. UCRPD was among nine of ten UC campus police departments found non-compliant with AB 48 by the LAist investigation.
Applicable Law / ReportingAB 48 (PC §§13652, 13652.1) — MANDATORY reporting of crowd-control weapon use (kinetic energy projectiles and chemical agents) within 60 days of deployment, extendable to 90 days; requires documentation of crowd size, officer count, type of projectile or agent, rounds deployed, injuries documented, and de-escalation tactics attempted. NOTE: Government Code §12525.2 (DOJ reporting of traditional officer-involved firearms discharges) and AB 71/URSUS reporting apply to incidents involving firearm discharge; the LAist source and Lt. Day’s on-record admission are framed exclusively within AB 48’s less-lethal/crowd-control reporting framework. POST use-of-force reporting requirements also applicable.
Compliance StatusNOT COMPLIANT — CONFIRMED ON RECORD BY LT. DAY Lt. Jason Day confirmed to LAist on the record that UCRPD did not report the less-lethal force deployment to the California DOJ, as required by AB 48. Day cited “capacity issues” and stated “A lot of times things are just going to have to get set aside.” This constitutes a documented, on-record acknowledgment by a command-level supervisor that a mandatory state reporting obligation under AB 48 was consciously not fulfilled. This is distinct from an absence of data: it is an articulated, on-record explanation for deliberate non-reporting.
Source(s)LAist, September 12, 2025 — laist.com/news/criminal-justice/uc-campus-police-protest-reporting-law; LAist — AB 48 Enforcement Gap — laist.com/news/politics/california-weapons-protests-reporting-requirements

Among the city’s documented allegations: “The UCR Police Department has been partially defunded, despite an ‘anticipated increase in crime and livability issues’ on campus due to growth.” The Police Accountability Board had separately documented a 50% year-to-date increase in calls for service with no corresponding staffing increase.

Source(s)City of Riverside vs. UC Regents, Riverside Superior Court, August 23, 2024 (court filing; no public URL identified)
Key Finding — On-Record Admission of Deliberate AB 48 Reporting Non-Compliance: UCRPD Lieutenant Jason Day’s statement to LAist — that a UCRPD less-lethal force deployment during Berkeley mutual aid was not reported to the California DOJ because “things are just going to have to get set aside” — constitutes a documented, on-record acknowledgment by a command-level supervisor that mandatory state reporting requirements under AB 48 were consciously not fulfilled. The LAist investigation and Lt. Day’s admission are framed within AB 48’s crowd-control weapon reporting framework (governing kinetic energy projectiles and chemical agents), not within the traditional officer-involved shooting (firearm discharge) reporting framework of Government Code §12525.2. This admission is distinct from other documented UC non-compliance in that it articulates awareness of the requirement and a deliberate choice not to fulfill it. The City of Riverside’s separate lawsuit citation of “partial defunding” provides documented institutional context for the capacity claim advanced by Lt. Day.
