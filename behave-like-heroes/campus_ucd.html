Note to Investigators, Attorneys, and Journalists

This campus dossier contains enforcement-level detail not summarized in the main chapters of this series. It documents the complete incident history, POST compliance status, financial exposure, and leadership record for the UC Davis Police Department. These documents are intended for POST investigators, attorneys, civil rights organizations, journalists, and other parties requiring granular evidentiary detail for enforcement, litigation, or investigative purposes. All source citations are independently verifiable. Readers are encouraged to draw their own conclusions from the documented record.

Section A — Department Overview
Agency NameUniversity of California Davis Police Department (UCDPD)
EstablishedEstablished on the Davis campus in 1946. UC Regents formally constituted the University of California Police Department system-wide in September 1947.
Legal AuthorityCalifornia Penal Code §830.2(b) (sworn peace officers); California Education Code §92600 (primary jurisdiction over UC property).
POST StatusPOST-participating agency. All officers hold POST certification. Officers subject to SB 2 certification review and decertification authority (effective January 1, 2023).
Sworn OfficersApproximately 54 sworn officers (as documented during Chief Spicuzza’s tenure). Current staffing figure not independently verified as of March 2026.
Annual BudgetUC-wide police spending doubled from $75.3 million (2009–10) to $148.5 million (2019–20). Davis-specific budget figures not independently confirmed.
Campus PopulationUC Davis reported total Fall 2025 enrollment of 40,617 — comprising undergraduate, graduate, and professional students as well as 1,208 veterinary and medical residents and interns — across the Davis campus and UC Davis Health in Sacramento. Faculty and staff headcount are additional to this enrolled-student figure. Source: UC Davis News — Enrollment statistics released January 8, 2026 by UC Office of the President.
JurisdictionUCDPD's primary jurisdiction is UC-owned, -operated, and -controlled property under California Education Code §92600. Officers coordinate with Davis PD (City of Davis, population ~66,850), Yolo County Sheriff, and Sacramento PD across their respective geographic areas of operation where UC Davis facilities are located. The specific terms of any formal interagency agreements governing concurrent jurisdiction are not independently confirmed in available public records.
Current ChiefJoseph A. “Joe” Farrow (August 2017–present). Former California Highway Patrol Commissioner (February 2008–July 2017); first Japanese-American CHP Commissioner. Service confirmed as of January 27, 2026 reaccreditation announcement.
IACLEA AccreditationACCREDITED  Initial accreditation achieved 2021. Reaccreditation confirmed January 27, 2026 for four-year term. Among six UC campuses confirmed IACLEA-accredited as of the UC Board of Regents January 2025 report (Berkeley, Irvine, San Diego, UCSF, and Santa Barbara identified as the other five). Accreditation status of individual campuses is subject to change; the January 2025 Regents report is the most recent independently verified systemwide count. Source: UC Board of Regents, January 2025.
Oversight BodyPolice Accountability Board (PAB), established May 2014 — described as “the first civilian oversight board established at a major research university.” 14 members (4 undergraduates, 2 graduate students, 2 faculty, 2 staff, 4 UC Davis Health). Critical limitation: Chief retains “full and final authority” over complaint disposition.
Union RepresentationFederated University Police Officers’ Association (FUPOA).
Notable DistinctionUC Davis pioneered the UC system’s civilian police oversight model. The PAB was adopted system-wide — all 10 UC campuses now have PABs. UC Davis also championed AB 960 (allowing DACA recipients to become California peace officers).
Section B — Chronological Incident Log

All documented incidents relevant to POST oversight, presented in chronological order. Incidents predating mandatory reporting laws are labeled accordingly and included to document pattern of conduct. No incident is abbreviated or omitted.

Pre-Mandatory Reporting Era
December 14, 2004 — Fatal Officer-Involved Shooting — Martin Louie Castro Soriano

First and only fatal officer-involved shooting in UC Davis campus history. Soriano, 26, of Antioch, was high on methamphetamine and fired at officers outside Regan Hall. Officers returned fire, killing Soriano. Officers were cleared of wrongdoing by the Yolo County District Attorney, the Yolo County Critical Incident Response Team, and internal affairs. This occurred under Chief Calvin Handy’s command.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: Predates SB 1421 (2019), AB 48 (2022). OIS reporting to DOJ under then-applicable statutes. POST use-of-force standards applicable.

Reported to POST/DOJ? PRE-MANDATORY — Yolo County DA cleared officers. UC Davis provided records from this incident under SB 1421 to Voice of San Diego in 2020 — one of only two OIS records released by any UC campus. Included to document pattern.

Sources: UC Davis News; UC Davis News (student status); LocalWiki — UC Davis PD
Pre-Mandatory Reporting Era
April 2004 (Picnic Day Eve) — Sterling University Apartments Riot — Tear Gas Deployed

An estimated 500–1,000 people gathered at Sterling University Vista Apartments in East Davis as multiple private parties merged. Beer bottles were thrown at police; individuals jumped on vehicles; “swarm robberies” occurred. Officers donned riot gear. Tear gas was fired into the complex along with pepper spray. Over 30 officers from CHP, Yolo County Sheriff, and UC Davis PD responded.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: Predates AB 48 (2022). Tear gas and pepper spray deployment in a crowd-management setting would today trigger mandatory AB 48 reporting. Mutual aid documentation requirements applicable.

Reported to POST/DOJ? PRE-MANDATORY — Pre-AB 48. No current reporting obligation. Included to document recurring Picnic Day crowd-control weapon use history and establish pattern of conduct for the department.

Sources: UC Davis News — Students bring special perspective in team effort to quell rowdy parties (confirms approximately 1,000 attendees, swarm robberies, pepper spray deployment, and 30+ officers from multiple agencies); LocalWiki — Sterling University Vista Apartments (documents tear gas deployment and riot gear response)
2005 (Filed) / January–April 2008 (Settled) — Calvin Chang Discrimination Lawsuit — $240,000 Settlement

Calvin Chang — the first openly gay and Asian-American officer on UC Davis police force, hired in 2002 — filed a discrimination lawsuit against the UC Board of Regents alleging racial and sexual orientation discrimination. A supervisor (identified as including Lt. John Pike) allegedly used a homophobic slur. A death threat was allegedly made during his service. Settlement: $240,000, with $66,000 allocated to Chang’s attorney. The settlement required Chang to resign as a condition of resolution.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST standards on workplace discrimination and officer conduct; Title VII of the Civil Rights Act; California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA), Gov. Code §12940. Sustained discrimination findings may implicate SB 2 decertification review for involved supervisors.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — No public record of POST reporting for discrimination findings. The settlement required Chang’s resignation as a condition of resolution. As a result, no sustained internal finding against named officers appears in available public records — a fact that may be relevant to any POST review of whether reporting obligations under applicable standards were triggered by the underlying conduct.

Sources: The Aggie, Feb. 15, 2011; Davis Enterprise
February 2009 (Filed) — Calvin Chang Second Lawsuit — $3 Million Sought; Five Claims Survive to Trial

Calvin Chang filed a second lawsuit against the UC Regents and Chief Spicuzza alleging breach of the 2008 settlement, ongoing discrimination, and retaliation. The university allegedly falsely reported that he resigned due to misconduct and added negative documents to his personnel file. Chief Spicuzza allegedly attempted to evict him from university housing. UC Davis filed over 100 motions to dismiss; approximately 90% of claims were dismissed by 2012, but five discrimination causes of action survived and proceeded to trial.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST standards on officer retaliation; FEHA; Brady/Giglio implications if dishonesty findings were sustained against named officers during this litigation.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — Case proceeded to trial on surviving claims. Final disposition not identified in available public records.

Source: The Aggie, Feb. 15, 2011
March 2009 (Filed) — Chithien Le Discrimination Lawsuit — Second Discrimination Filing in Same Department

Chithien Le, 28, another Asian-American officer, filed suit alleging sexual harassment, racial discrimination, and hostile work environment. Officers allegedly logged onto Le’s computer and changed his desktop wallpaper to gay pornography. Officers Rob Stoleo, Shawn Bowers, and Danny Sheffield were specifically named. Le was called “the next Calvin Chang” as a slur. Despite being nominated for officer of the year after his first year, Le received a termination notice in February 2008.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST standards on officer conduct and workplace harassment; Title VII; FEHA. A pattern of discrimination complaints from multiple officers within the same department and timeframe may constitute a systemic issue warranting POST review.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — Final disposition not identified in available public records. Two discrimination lawsuits from separate officers within the same department and overlapping time period establish a documented pattern.

Source: The Aggie, March 10, 2009
October 2009 (Disclosed) — Clery Act Over-Reporting Scandal — 140% Over-Reporting of Sexual Offenses — Unprecedented in National History

UC Davis disclosed that it had over-reported forcible sexual offenses by 140% for three consecutive years (2005–2007). Reported totals were 48, 68, and 69 offenses respectively. The correct figures were 21, 23, and 33 — a total overcount of 108 incidents across the three-year period. The discrepancy was attributed to Jennifer Beeman, director of the Campus Violence Prevention Program (16 years of service), who was placed on leave in December 2008 for improperly charged travel expenses and departed in June 2009.

S. Daniel Carter of Security on Campus stated: “completely unprecedented — we have never seen anything like that before.” Each violation carried a potential fine of $27,500; UC Davis was therefore eligible for up to $2.97 million in federal penalties. Ironically, UC Davis had received the inaugural Jeanne Clery National Safe Campus Award in 1996. UC Davis self-reported to both the U.S. Department of Education and the Department of Justice. The subsequent review was overseen by Dolores Stafford (past IACLEA president). No evidence of an actual fine being imposed was located in available public records.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: Clery Act (20 U.S.C. §1092(f)); 34 C.F.R. §668.46. Data integrity requirements. This is a documented compliance failure unique in national Clery Act history, involving over-reporting rather than the typical pattern of under-reporting.

Reported to POST/DOJ? PARTIALLY COMPLIANT — UC Davis self-reported to both U.S. Department of Education and Department of Justice. No evidence a fine was actually imposed despite eligibility for up to $2.97 million in penalties.

Sources: Campus Safety Magazine, October 2009; UC Davis News — Clery Statistics
April 17, 2010 — Picnic Day Crisis — 1 Death, 38 Arrests, 60 Citations, 516–545 Calls for Service

The 96th annual Picnic Day turned violent by evening: street fights, assaults on officers, reports of women being groped, reports of gunfire, and widespread property damage. Scott Heinig, a recent UC Davis baseball graduate, died from a head injury sustained at a private house party. Davis PD recorded 38 arrests (30 misdemeanor, 6 felony), 60 citations, and 516–545 calls for service compared to approximately 100 on a typical weekend. Police were so overwhelmed that they could not respond to many calls during the peak period. The aftermath nearly resulted in permanent cancellation of Picnic Day.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST use-of-force reporting (if force used during arrests); Clery Act (campus crime statistics, timely warnings); mutual aid documentation requirements.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — Heinig’s death occurred at a private off-campus house party. Available public records do not establish that the event was located on Clery-reportable geography or that it was institutionally affiliated in a manner that would trigger a Clery Act reporting obligation. Whether force used during the 38 arrests was individually reported to POST is not documented in available records. Massive mutual aid mobilization for the subsequent 2011 Picnic Day (64 arrests, 269+ citations) followed as a documented institutional response.

November 18, 2011 — Pepper Spray Incident — International Outrage — $2.7 Million+ Total Documented Costs

On November 18, 2011, during Occupy UC Davis protests on the campus quad, Lt. John Pike and Officer Alexander Lee pepper-sprayed seated, nonviolent student demonstrators at close range with MK-9 pepper spray. Chief Spicuzza initially told the Davis Enterprise she was “very proud” of officers and falsely claimed that officers had been “trapped” by protesters — a characterization subsequently contradicted by video evidence and independent review findings.

Total Documented Costs: approximately $2.7 million+, broken down as follows:

  • Kroll investigation (led by former NYPD/LAPD Commissioner William Bratton): $445,879
  • Internal affairs investigations (Van Dermyden Allison Law Corp + Norman A. Traub Associates): $230,256
  • UC systemwide Edley-Robinson report: $320,000
  • UC Berkeley officials’ salaries/fees for systemwide review: $88,686
  • Crisis management consulting: $119,714
  • Baker v. Katehi class action settlement: $1,000,000 ($30,000 each to 21 named plaintiffs; up to $100,000 additional claimants; $250,000 attorney fees)
  • Pike workers’ compensation award: $38,056
  • Pike paid leave salary (~8 months on $121,680 annual salary): approximately $81,120
  • Spicuzza paid leave salary (~5 months on $140,417 annual salary): approximately $61,500
  • Officer Alexander Lee paid leave: approximately $38,040
  • Reputation management — Nevins & Associates of Maryland ($15,000/month, six-month contract beginning January 2013, contracted to “eradicate” Google references to the incident) plus additional NYC-based consultant: $175,000+

Personnel Chronology: Chief Spicuzza was placed on paid leave on November 21, 2011; announced her retirement on April 18, 2012 (retirement forced, preserving pension); retired with full benefits before the internal investigation was complete, having drawn approximately $61,500 in paid leave salary during that period. Lt. Pike was fired by Chief Carmichael on July 31, 2012, overruling two separate internal investigations that had recommended lesser discipline. On September 19, 2012, the Yolo County District Attorney declined to prosecute Pike. On October 16, 2013, Pike was awarded $38,056 in workers’ compensation for “psychiatric injuries” resulting from threats received after the incident — approximately $8,000 more than the $30,000 each pepper-sprayed student received in the class action settlement. Officer Alexander Lee departed by July 2012; the circumstances of his departure were never officially confirmed by the department.

Independent Review Findings (Reynoso Task Force, April 11, 2012): The independent task force, chaired by former California Supreme Court Justice Cruz Reynoso and appointed by UC President Mark G. Yudof, found: (1) the pepper spray deployment “should and could have been prevented”; (2) Lt. Pike’s use of MK-9 pepper spray on seated, non-resisting students was “objectively unreasonable”; (3) officers used an unauthorized stream-delivery method rather than the policy-approved fog variant; (4) Pike had no clearly defined role in the operations plan yet was responsible for the key decision about the weapon used; (5) Chief Spicuzza’s actions were “critically flawed”; and (6) the department was “very dysfunctional.”

Internal Investigation Contradiction: A 76-page internal affairs report dated March 1, 2012, determined that Pike’s use of pepper spray was “reasonable under the circumstances” — a finding that directly contradicted the Reynoso Task Force’s conclusion of “objectively unreasonable” force. This produced two simultaneous and irreconcilable official findings from the same events.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST use-of-force standards; PC §13652 (annual use-of-force reporting); excessive force standard (4th Amendment, Graham v. Connor). Reynoso Task Force found Spicuzza’s actions “critically flawed” and department “very dysfunctional.” Lt. Pike’s termination for excessive force is the type of personnel action that, under applicable POST standards, would require reporting to the Commission. Whether that reporting occurred has not been independently verified in available public records.

Reported to POST/DOJ? PARTIALLY DOCUMENTED — Reynoso Task Force report published April 11, 2012. Kroll investigation completed. Internal affairs completed. DA investigation completed. Whether Pike’s termination for excessive force was formally reported to POST as required is not independently verified in available records. Spicuzza’s retirement before investigation completion may have prevented sustained findings from reaching POST.

Key Finding — Contradictory Official Findings: The internal affairs investigation found Lt. Pike’s actions “reasonable under the circumstances,” while the independent Reynoso Task Force found the same actions “objectively unreasonable.” Chief Spicuzza retired with full benefits before investigation completion. Whether the findings that ultimately supported termination were reported to POST in the manner required by law is not established by available public records.
Sources: Reynoso Task Force Report — Official PDF (April 11, 2012) — UC Davis Demonstration Reviews; UC Davis News — Use of pepper spray not justified, concludes Reynoso Task Force; Wikipedia — UC Davis Pepper Spray Incident; CBS News — Spicuzza resignation; CBS Sacramento — Pike workers’ comp; HuffPost — Pepper spray costs (Prof. Ostertag, UC Davis, public records analysis); Spokesman-Review — $1M settlement; CS Monitor — Reputation scrubbing

May 2014 — Police Accountability Board Established — First at a Major Research University

Chief Carmichael established the Police Accountability Board (PAB) as a signature post-pepper-spray reform following recommendations of the Reynoso Task Force. The PAB was described as “the first civilian oversight board established at a major research university.” Its first public session was held in October 2014. The PAB structure was subsequently adopted system-wide by UC, and all 10 UC campuses now have PABs.

Documented Limitation: The PAB’s bylaws explicitly provide that the Chief of Police retains “full and final authority” over complaint disposition. The PAB’s sustain rate on investigated allegations through 2021 was documented at 15% — a figure that has been cited in academic and journalistic reviews of the board’s effectiveness.

Sources: The Aggie, Oct. 30, 2014; CBS Sacramento — Pepper Spray Saga / PAB; PAB website; PAB bylaws
November 18, 2014 — Tuition Protest on Pepper Spray Anniversary — Deliberate Police Absence

On the third anniversary of the pepper spray incident, several hundred students (reported range: 200–600) rallied on the exact Quad location where the spraying occurred and marched to Mrak Hall. Chancellor Katehi stood on the front steps and engaged directly with protesters. Approximately 20 students spent the night inside Mrak Hall. FOX40 reported: “During Tuesday’s rally and march, no campus police officers could be seen.” Zero arrests were made. Zero force was used.

On November 19, students briefly reoccupied Mrak Hall. On November 24, approximately 300 students walked out, occupied a Bank of America branch on campus, and marched through Olsen Hall — again with zero police confrontation.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: First Amendment assembly rights. The deliberate non-enforcement strategy represents an institutional response to the 2011 crisis. POST training standards on protest response de-escalation.

Reported to POST/DOJ? N/A — No force used, no arrests made. No reporting obligation triggered. Included to document the post-pepper-spray institutional transformation in protest response methodology.

Source: FOX40, November 18, 2014

May 6 – June 20, 2024 (45 Days) — Gaza Solidarity Encampment — Zero Arrests, Zero Force Used

The UC Davis Gaza solidarity encampment, organized by Davis Popular University for the Liberation of Palestine (UCD PULP) on the Memorial Union Quad, became one of the longest-lasting pro-Palestinian encampments in the national wave of 2024 campus protests — lasting 45 days, more than double the duration of UC Berkeley’s 22-day encampment. The encampment grew from approximately 20 tents at its inception to dozens of tents with approximately 200 participants at peak activity. The five stated demands were: (1) divestment, (2) Koret program cancellation, (3) Palestine studies investment, (4) full amnesty for participants, and (5) Chancellor May’s resignation from the Leidos corporate board.

Police made zero arrests during the entire 45-day period. No use of force was employed. No mutual aid agencies were summoned. Police maintained communication through Student Affairs. The encampment ended voluntarily on June 20, 2024. In his June 20 statement, Chancellor May wrote that “students remained peaceful throughout the encampment and de-escalated challenging situations.” However, organizers (PULP) accused UC Office of the President of blocking any formal written agreement.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: AB 48 — no crowd-control weapons were used, so no AB 48 reporting was triggered. First Amendment assembly rights. Clery Act (campus safety reporting). This encampment was a distinct event from the May 24, 2024 weapons discharge incident (documented separately below).

Reported to POST/DOJ? N/A — No force used, no arrests made. No reporting obligation triggered. UC Davis’s zero-arrest outcome stands in documented contrast to other UC campus responses: UCLA (~210 arrests), UCSD (64–65 arrests), UCSC (~80 arrests), UCI (47 arrests).

Sources: Wikipedia — 2024 UC Davis Encampment; UC Davis News — 2024 Campus Climate Updates; Chancellor May Statement, June 20, 2024
May 24, 2024 (~1:40 a.m.) — Officer Unintentionally Discharges Service Weapon During Arrest — Body Camera Footage Exists

During the encampment period, a UC Davis police officer unintentionally discharged his service weapon during the arrest of three vandalism suspects who had been spray-painting buildings and campus “Egghead” sculptures with pro-Palestinian messages. The three suspects identified in records are: Lysandra Dasilva, 31; Nathan Orr, 30; and Cheyenne Xiong, 20, a UC Davis student. No one was injured as a result of the discharge.

Defense attorneys subsequently introduced body camera footage into criminal proceedings, describing it as showing the officer firing at one fleeing suspect — and characterizing the event as “a 20-year-old junior at UC Davis being hunted down and shot at by campus police” for alleged spray-painting. This characterization represents the advocacy position of defense counsel as presented in criminal proceedings and does not constitute a judicial finding of fact. The Yolo County District Attorney filed felony charges; a judge later dismissed the grand jury indictment; the DA continued pursuing charges into late 2025. Chief Farrow referred the discharge incident to the Police Accountability Board.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: POST reporting: firearm discharge by an officer is a POST-reportable event. PC §13652 (use-of-force reporting). AB 748 (body camera footage disclosure requirements — footage confirmed to exist per defense attorneys). OIS reporting protocols (though no person was struck). Yolo County DA involvement is documented.

Reported to POST/DOJ? PARTIALLY DOCUMENTED — Chief Farrow’s referral to PAB is documented. Whether the weapons discharge was formally reported to POST as required by law is not independently verified. Body camera footage exists but its disclosure status under AB 748 timelines is unknown from available records.

Sources: ABC10 — UC Davis officer gun discharge; Davis Enterprise — Officer’s gun goes off; Yolo County DA — Felony charges; Davis Vanguard — Egghead case, Sept. 2025

April 3, 2025 — Turning Point USA Confrontation — Violence, No Arrests at Scene; Felony Charges Filed November 2025

Approximately 40 masked protesters, believed to be affiliated with Cops Off Campus, confronted a Turning Point USA “Prove Me Wrong” tabling event featuring conservative commentator Brandon Tatum. Protesters pulled down the tent, destroyed camera equipment, and assaulted attendees. Beth Bourne (a UC Davis employee) was struck with an umbrella and punched. Timothy Blank was struck in the face twice.

UC Davis police formed a perimeter but made no arrests at the scene, taking only two assault reports. Chancellor May publicly condemned the violence as “appalling.” Felony charges were subsequently filed in November 2025: Joanna Clara Sodke, 22, was charged with felony conspiracy to commit a riot and misdemeanor battery (arraigned November 14, 2025, $10,000 bail). Chloe Love Peterson, 22, was charged with felony conspiracy and misdemeanor battery. Critics noted the ironic contrast between the 2011 department’s excessive force during peaceful protest and the 2025 department’s failure to arrest during active assault.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: Clery Act (timely warning for violent crime occurring on campus); POST use-of-force standards; PC §404/404.6 (riot); PC §242 (battery). The question of whether the failure to make arrests at the scene of violent crime in progress constitutes a POST-reviewable policy failure is not addressed in available public records.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — No public record of POST review for failure to arrest during active assault. Charges were filed 7+ months after the incident. Whether a Clery timely warning was issued for the assault is not independently verified in available records.

Sources: CBS Sacramento — TPUSA charges, November 2025; ABC10 — TPUSA confrontation; Yolo County DA — TPUSA felony charges
April 12, 2025 — Picnic Day Shooting — 3 Shot, Children Exposed, AR-15-Style Pistol, Federal Charges

A shooting occurred at Community Park during a gathering hosted by UC Davis fraternity Phi Beta Sigma featuring rapper Haiti Babii. Three people were shot with non-life-threatening injuries. Children and Little League families were present and exposed to gunfire. Suspect Joseph Allen Davis, 19, was arrested on May 23, 2025, on federal charges. He had fired an “AR-15-style pistol.” 57 officers responded; 321 calls for service were recorded in a 24-hour period. Davis City Council unanimously formed a Picnic Day safety subcommittee. Major 2026 policy changes followed: a full prohibition on special event permits and doubled police staffing for the event.

Applicable Law / Reporting Requirement: Clery Act (timely warning/emergency notification for campus-adjacent shooting); POST reporting requirements for firearm incident response; federal firearms charges indicate ATF/FBI coordination and documentation.

Reported to POST/DOJ? UNKNOWN — Whether UC Davis PD issued a proper Clery timely warning and filed all required POST reports is not independently verified in available records. Federal charges indicate documented ATF/FBI coordination.

Source: Davis Enterprise — Picnic Day shooting, April 2025

AB 48
(PC §§13652, 13652.1)
Effective Jan 1, 2022		 Requires every law enforcement agency to document and publicly report any use of kinetic energy projectiles, chemical agents, or other crowd-control weapons during protest/crowd management. Reports filed with California DOJ. NON-COMPLIANT UCDPD was not listed on the California DOJ’s statewide AB 48 reporting webpage as of the September 12, 2025 LAist investigation. 9 of 10 UC campus police departments failed to comply; only UCLA complied. Only 32 of 624 total California agencies appeared on the DOJ’s AB 48 website. UC Davis does maintain compliance with the separate AB 481 (military equipment) requirement. LAist, Sept. 12, 2025
Clery Act
(20 U.S.C. §1092(f))
Effective 1990		 Requires disclosure of campus crime statistics, daily crime logs, timely warnings, emergency notifications, and annual security reports. HISTORICALLY NON-COMPLIANT October 2009: UC Davis disclosed over-reporting of forcible sexual offenses by 140% for three consecutive years (2005–2007). Overcount: 108 incidents. Eligible for up to $2.97 million in federal penalties. No fine imposed. UC Davis had received inaugural Jeanne Clery National Safe Campus Award in 1996. Current compliance status not independently audited for the most recent reporting period. Campus Safety Magazine, Oct. 2009
SB 1421
(PC §832.7)
Effective Jan 1, 2019		 Requires release of records relating to OIS, use of force causing great bodily injury/death, sustained findings of sexual assault, and sustained findings of dishonesty by officers. PARTIALLY COMPLIANT — ABOVE UC AVERAGE (as of May 2020) Voice of San Diego (May 12, 2020) found UC Davis was one of only three UC campuses (with UC Merced and UC Santa Barbara) that provided any records under SB 1421 as of that date. UC Davis provided records from the 2004 Soriano OIS — one of only two OIS records released by any UC campus. Six campuses (UCSD, UCB, UCLA, UCI, UCSC, UCSF) provided no records. UC system was aware of 200+ use-of-force incidents but released only two case files total. This compliance finding is based on May 2020 reporting; UC Davis’s current SB 1421 compliance posture was not independently re-audited for this March 2026 report. Voice of San Diego, May 12, 2020
AB 481
(Gov. Code §7070 et seq.)
Effective Jan 1, 2022		 Requires governing body approval for acquisition/use of military equipment and annual use reports. COMPLIANT UCDPD publishes AB 481 military equipment reports and hosts a dedicated AB 481 compliance page on the department website. UC Davis PD — AB 481 page
AB 748
(PC §832.18)
Effective July 1, 2019		 Requires release of body camera footage within 45 days for critical incidents (OIS, in-custody death, use of force causing great bodily injury). UNKNOWN — FOOTAGE EXISTS, DISCLOSURE RECORD UNCLEAR Defense attorneys in the May 24, 2024 weapons discharge case introduced body camera footage showing the officer firing at a fleeing suspect. This confirms body cameras were deployed and footage exists. Whether footage was proactively released under AB 748 timelines, or surfaced only through criminal defense discovery, is not documented in available records. Additionally, AB 748 applies to specified critical incidents (OIS, in-custody death, use of force causing great bodily injury); whether this discharge — in which no person was struck — meets the statutory trigger is itself an unresolved legal question not addressed in available public records. Davis Vanguard, Sept. 2025
Brady / Giglio
Disclosure Obligations		 Prosecutors must disclose material impeachment evidence about law enforcement witnesses. Agencies must maintain and disclose Brady lists of officers with sustained findings affecting credibility. UNKNOWN — 2011 PERSONNEL ACTIONS RAISE QUESTIONS Lt. John Pike was fired for excessive force (July 2012). Two internal investigations found his conduct “reasonable” before being overruled. Whether Pike’s termination and the underlying findings were disclosed to the Yolo County DA’s Brady list is not documented. The Chang and Le discrimination lawsuits documented workplace harassment including alleged use of slurs by supervisory officers — sustained findings in these matters could implicate Brady/Giglio obligations. Davis Enterprise; Davis Vanguard; The Aggie
RIPA / AB 953
Effective Jan 2022 (for UC PDs)		 Requires collection and reporting of data on all stops, including perceived demographic characteristics. RIPA REPORTING ACTIVE — PUBLIC STOP-DATA CONFIRMED UC Davis PD began collecting RIPA data in January 2022 with reporting due April 2023. The department publishes a Transparency Dashboard (established 2021) for public access to police data. This compliance finding is based on publicly observable reporting activity; an independent audit of data accuracy or completeness was not conducted for this report. UC Davis PD — UC Davis Difference
SB 2 / Kenneth Ross Jr.
Decertification Act
Effective Jan 1, 2023		 Establishes POST authority to investigate and decertify peace officers for serious misconduct. NO DECERTIFICATION ACTIONS IDENTIFIED — HISTORICAL CASES MAY WARRANT REVIEW No POST decertification actions found against UCDPD officers. Pike’s 2012 termination for excessive force and the documented pattern of discrimination (Chang/Le lawsuits) predate SB 2 but establish a historical record. Whether officers involved in 2011 events who remained in law enforcement were subject to SB 2 review after January 2023 is not documented in available records.
