Opening Statement Law Enforcement Without Enforcement

The Core Problem

  • 1
    If you rack up too many tickets, the DMV suspends your driver’s license – you are legally done driving until you earn it back.
  • 2
    But the police departments that write those tickets and respond to those calls have no equivalent DMV for themselves. There is no license to operate a police department that can be suspended or revoked when the department breaks the rules.

POST Licenses Individual Cops — It Does Not License Departments

  • 3
    California’s Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST) certifies individual peace officers and can decertify them for “serious misconduct” under SB 2, even without a criminal conviction.
  • 4
    POST keeps a list of certified officers and a list of participating agencies. It does not issue a charter that makes a city, county, or campus “a police department,” and it does not have the power to take that charter away.
⚠  For Agencies, POST Is Voluntary — And Walking Away Has Almost No Consequence
  • A city, county, or UC campus chooses to be a POST-participating agency. The governing statute, Cal. Penal Code § 13510.1, describes agencies that “participate” in the POST Program — not agencies required to hold a POST-issued operating license. If a department walks away from POST, or if POST suspends the agency from the program, the department still keeps its badges, guns, and arrest powers under local authority.
  • Losing POST participation can mean losing training money or access to POST resources. It does NOT mean the department stops being a police department or has to shut down.
  • POST’s own spokesperson confirmed this gap on the record. In response to questions about AB 48 reporting, POST spokesperson Meagan Poulos told LAist: “The way the law is written, there is no stated enforcement mechanism. The individual agencies are responsible.” The California Department of Justice separately confirmed that AB 48 “does not include requirements for DOJ to review, audit, or enforce law enforcement agency requirements.” The bill’s author, former Assemblymember Cristina Garcia, asked in frustration: “If we don’t have enforcement, do we really have a law?”
  • In other professions, the licensing board can say, “You are no longer allowed to practice at all.” In California policing, there is no statewide body with that power over an entire department.

SB 2 Was Supposed to Be the Fix — But Only for Individuals

  • 5
    SB 2 (the Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act of 2021) finally gave California a system for decertifying individual officers who commit serious misconduct, after decades as one of the few states without general decertification authority.
  • 6
    Penal Code § 13510.9 says agencies must report allegations of serious misconduct to POST within 10 days — not just sustained findings, but any allegation that could lead to decertification.
  • 7
    POST has confirmed there are no statutory exceptions: departments must report even allegations they think are frivolous or intend to clear.

In Practice, SB 2 Gave Bad Leadership a Simple Five-Step Playbook

  • Step 1
    A serious allegation comes in. Excessive force, retaliation, falsifying reports, civil-rights violations, or abuse of power — sometimes against a street-level cop, sometimes against a captain or the chief.
  • Step 2
    The law triggers a 10-day duty to report. The moment that allegation exists, Penal Code § 13510.9 says the department must report it to POST within 10 days, even if the chief thinks it is bogus or plans to exonerate the officer.
  • Step 3
    The department keeps it in-house. Instead of reporting, the allegation gets re-labeled, narrowed, or dumped into an “other” category so it never hits POST’s system. This is not hypothetical — UC’s own systemwide ECAS audit found that 25% of UC police complaint allegations were classified as “other,” meaning the departments could not or did not even categorize the misconduct they were sitting on.
  • Step 4
    The reporter becomes the problem. Careers stall, disability pay disappears, records go missing or are contradicted, and the officer or employee who documented command-level misconduct finds himself fighting for his income, his medical care, and his reputation — while the subject of the complaint keeps their badge and authority.
  • Step 5
    Nothing happens to the agency. SB 2 created no automatic fine, no funding cutoff, and no “department license” for POST to pull when a department fails to report. At most, POST can audit or question the agency’s voluntary participation status — but the department itself keeps right on operating.

UC Police Have CHP-Level Powers With Less External Control

  • 8
    UC police officers are Penal Code § 830.2(b) officers, with statewide arrest authority equivalent to the California Highway Patrol.
  • 9
    But they do not answer to a city council or county board that can be voted out. They answer to the UC Regents and campus chancellors — appointed officials backed by constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution, which limits ordinary legislative control over UC.
  • 10
    The result is a system where departments have the same powers as municipal agencies, fewer democratic checks, and no state-level license that can be revoked when they break the rules.

The UC System Shows What That Gap Looks Like in the Real World

  • Laws
    Ten UC police departments are all subject to “mandatory” laws: SB 1421 and SB 16 (personnel-records transparency); AB 48 and AB 481 (crowd-control and military-equipment reporting); AB 748 (body-camera video disclosures); the Clery Act, 20 U.S.C. § 1092(f) (campus crime statistics); RIPA, Gov. Code § 12525.5 (racial and identity profiling data); and state use-of-force reporting requirements.
  • Record
    Multiple campuses have ignored or delayed those mandates — withholding SB 1421 records, failing to file AB 48 crowd-control reports, under-reporting or misclassifying Clery Act crime data — yet none of those departments has ever lost its ability to operate as a police department, because no agency-level license exists.

To Be Fair — Other Shutdown Mechanisms Exist. However, None of Them Are POST.

  • 11
    A city council can vote to disband its own police department, as Camden, New Jersey did in 2013 — and rebuild from scratch with different accountability structures.
  • 12
    County boards of supervisors can defund, restructure, or abolish sheriff’s office patrol functions.
  • 13
    Voters themselves can pass ballot measures to restructure, disband, or defund a local police department.
  • 14
    Courts can force structural reform through federal consent decrees — as happened with the LAPD after Rampart — when a pattern-or-practice of civil-rights violations is proven under 34 U.S.C. § 12601.
  • 15
    State legislatures can pass laws stripping a department of its authorization to operate — though California has never done so.
  • 16
    Every one of those mechanisms requires a political majority, a federal lawsuit, or a constitutional crisis. None of them are POST. All of them are slow, expensive, politically brutal — and completely out of reach for a single whistleblower, a single campus community, or a single POST audit.

At UC Riverside, a Commander Said the Quiet Part Out Loud

A UCR police lieutenant admitted on the record that a required AB 48 report — after firing less-lethal projectiles during mutual aid at UC Berkeley — was never filed. His explanation: “things are just going to have to get set aside” because of capacity.

Even with that open admission that the department knowingly ignored a state reporting law, there was no structural way for POST to sanction UCR as a department. At most, its voluntary status as a POST-participating agency was at risk. Its legal status as a police department was not.

At UCLA, the Cost of Telling the Truth Fell on the Person Who Reported It

Long before SB 2 or today’s transparency laws existed, a UCLA officer documented corruption and command-level misconduct.

Even under the Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights and the whistleblower protections that did exist at the time, he was not treated as someone the system was built to protect: years without disability income, loss of his home, battles for medical coverage during cancer treatment, and a paper trail of contradictions in the university’s own records — while the people he reported kept their badges and their authority.

Decades later, SB 2 finally created a way to decertify individual officers for serious misconduct. It still did not create a way to decertify the department leadership that retaliated against him, or to pull UCLA PD’s “license” to operate — because no such license exists.

His case is not an anomaly. It is the template. It is what happens when the system is working exactly as it was designed: the individual who reports pays the price, and the institution that retaliated keeps its authority.

Bottom Line

  • 17
    POST was built to uphold the Law Enforcement Code of Ethics — and it already has jurisdiction over the people violating it.
  • 18
    Most of the people running police departments — the chief, the captains, the lieutenants, the commanders who decide what gets reported and what gets buried — are themselves POST-certified peace officers.
  • 19
    That means POST already has direct personal jurisdiction over every command officer who signed off on a late report, buried a complaint in “other,” or retaliated against a whistleblower. POST does not need a new law or a new agency. The certification lever already exists.
  • 20
    So the real question — the one POST has never been asked to answer on the record — is this: When a department comprised of individually POST-certified commanders repeatedly violates mandatory reporting requirements, ignores the Code of Ethics they swore to uphold, and retaliates against the officers who tried to report up the chain — why isn’t POST going after them personally?
  • 21
    That is the gap. Not a missing law. Not a missing agency. A missing decision — by the body that already holds the power — to use the tool it already has.
POST Allows Officers to Break the Law

Not by design. Not in writing. But in practice — by structure — that is exactly what happens.

POST certifies individual officers and can decertify them for serious misconduct. Decertification only works if the misconduct reaches POST. The only people required to report that misconduct to POST are the same command officers who committed it, covered it up, or ordered it buried.

There is no independent intake. There is no mandatory external audit triggered by a complaint. There is no automatic referral when a department fails to file a required report. There is no penalty when a certified commander dumps 25% of misconduct allegations into an “other” category so they disappear from the system.

POST built a decertification machine and then handed the power to feed it — or starve it — entirely to the people the machine was designed to hold accountable.

When those people choose not to report, POST does nothing.

When a lieutenant openly admits on the record that a required state report was never filed, POST does nothing to the department.

When a whistleblower loses his income, his home, and his health coverage for reporting command-level misconduct up the chain, POST does not open a case against the commanders who retaliated.

The certified officers at the top of the chain break mandatory reporting laws. They retaliate against the officers who follow them. They do it wearing the same POST certification and swearing the same Law Enforcement Code of Ethics as the officer they destroyed.

And POST does nothing.

So yes. In practice, POST allows officers to break the law — as long as the officers breaking it are the ones running the department.

“Participating in the POST Program is voluntary.” — Cal. Code Regs. Title 11, § 1010. The program is voluntary. The oath those commanders swore to uphold — to behave like heroes — was not. POST can still enforce it.

The Five Facts

What The Documented Record Establishes

Each of the following facts is independently verifiable from cited public sources. Readers are encouraged to verify each one.

1

9 out of 10 — LAist, September 2025

Nine out of ten University of California campus police departments failed to comply with California state law governing protest response reporting. Documented by LAist in September 2025 from public records. Not one or two departments. Nine out of ten.

2

UC’s Own Auditors Found The Same Problems Three Times

The UC Office of the President’s own internal audit service (ECAS) conducted a systemwide audit in 2023 and found simultaneously across all ten departments: no systemwide use of force policy; 25% of complaints categorized as “other” — meaning departments could not classify their own misconduct complaints; at least one campus had no policy for investigating its own Chief of Police. These are the same categories the 2019 Presidential Task Force had already recommended fixing.

3

The LAPD Comparison — 37 Years, No Consent Decree

The documented 37-year University of California police record (December 1989, when Badge #341 joined UCLA PD, through 2026) contains evidence in every category that triggered the federal LAPD consent decree: excessive force, racial profiling, evidence issues, whistleblower retaliation, internal review failures, reporting non-compliance, and leadership instability. The LAPD consent decree was triggered. No federal pattern-or-practice investigation has been initiated against any UC police department.

4

No Elected Officials. No Voters. No Recourse.

The University of California’s ten police departments are governed by the Board of Regents — appointed officials with constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution, interpreted by the California Supreme Court in Regents of the University of California v. Superior Court (1999) to limit the Legislature’s ability to impose governance requirements on UC. There is no city council. There are no elected officials. There are no voters who can remove them.

5

Three Reform Attempts. Same Findings.

2019: Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing — found fundamental problems, issued recommendations. 2021: UC Community Safety Plan — implemented in response to Task Force findings. 2023: ECAS Systemwide Audit — found the same fundamental problems the 2019 Task Force identified. No external enforcement mechanism compelled compliance with any of the three sets of recommendations.
