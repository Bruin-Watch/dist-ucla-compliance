I. POST Authority and Certification

The Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST) is the state agency responsible for setting minimum selection and training standards for California law enforcement. UC police officers are subject to POST jurisdiction as specified in Penal Code § 830.2(b).

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Penal Code § 830.2(b) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Defines UC police officers as peace officers whose authority extends to any place in the state. Officers appointed pursuant to Education Code § 92600 are included. This is the statutory basis for UC police officers' sworn status and, critically, their subjection to POST oversight, certification requirements, and the decertification provisions of SB 2.
Cal. Penal Code § 13510 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes POST's rulemaking authority to adopt and amend minimum standards for physical, mental, and moral fitness governing recruitment of peace officers. Requires POST to adopt rules for training, education, and certification. Applies to UC police officers through their classification under § 830.2(b).
Cal. Penal Code § 13510.1 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Requires every peace officer described in §§ 830.1, 830.2 (except subdivision (d)), 830.3, 830.32, and 830.33 to possess a valid POST certificate. As amended by SB 2, subdivision (g)(1) provides that agencies may only employ officers who possess current, valid certification. UC police agencies are POST-participating agencies and their officers must maintain current certification.
Cal. Penal Code § 13510.5 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Requires POST to develop and implement training standards for peace officers, including courses on use of force, crisis intervention, racial and cultural diversity, and community policing. Training courses must address officer accountability and de-escalation techniques. UC police departments must ensure their officers complete all POST-mandated training.
Cal. Penal Code § 13503(f) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Grants POST the authority to conduct audits of law enforcement agencies to verify compliance with POST standards and requirements. As amended by SB 2, POST's audit authority extends to verifying that agencies are complying with mandatory reporting requirements under § 13510.9 and that officers possess current, valid certification.
Cal. Ed. Code § 92600 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Authorizes the UC Regents to establish a police department and appoint peace officers to protect university properties and persons. UC police officers appointed under this section derive their peace officer status from Penal Code § 830.2(b). The Regents' constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution does not exempt UC police from POST jurisdiction or Penal Code requirements.
Source Note: POST's list of participating law enforcement agencies, including all 10 UC campus police departments, is maintained at post.ca.gov/le-agencies. POST's Guide to Peace Officer Decertification Proceedings confirms that officers described in Penal Code § 830.2 are subject to SB 2 oversight. See post.ca.gov — Guide to Decertification.

II. SB 2 — Decertification (The Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act of 2021)

Senate Bill 2 (the Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act of 2021) established California’s first statewide system for revoking or suspending a peace officer’s certification for serious misconduct. For a detailed analysis of SB 2’s application to UC police departments, see Chapter 02, Sections IV–VI. The statute table below provides direct links to the operative code sections.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Penal Code § 13510.8 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes the grounds and procedures for decertification. Subdivision (a)(1) provides that POST shall suspend or revoke a peace officer's certification for "serious misconduct." Subdivision (b) defines "serious misconduct" to include, at minimum, nine categories: (1) dishonesty; (2) abuse of power; (3) physical abuse; (4) sexual assault; (5) demonstrating bias; (6) acts that violate the law; (7) participation in a law enforcement gang; (8) failure to cooperate with an investigation; and (9) failure to intercede. Subdivision (c)(1) requires agencies to complete investigations into misconduct regardless of the officer's employment status — even if the officer has resigned. Subdivision (g)(1) provides authority for POST to act on misconduct that occurred prior to January 1, 2022, if the employing agency makes a final determination after that date.
Cal. Penal Code § 13510.85 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes the due process procedures for decertification proceedings. When POSAD finds reasonable grounds for decertification, it must notify the officer in writing of its determination, reasons, and the officer's right to appeal. The officer has 30 days to request review. If no timely request is filed, POST suspends or revokes certification per the Division's recommendation. Appeals are heard by the Peace Officer Standards Accountability Board, which makes recommendations to the Commission.
Cal. Penal Code § 13510.9 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes mandatory reporting requirements for law enforcement agencies. Agencies must report to POST within 10 days when any of the following occurs: (a) an allegation of serious misconduct; (b) findings by civilian oversight entities; (c) final disposition of an investigation finding misconduct; (d) a civil judgment, court finding, or settlement based on officer conduct. POST FAQ confirms there are "no statutory exceptions for frivolous, unfounded, or exonerated allegations" — all must be reported. Retroactive reporting for events between January 1, 2020 and January 1, 2023 was required beginning July 1, 2023.
Cal. Penal Code § 13509.5 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes the Peace Officer Standards Accountability Division (POSAD) within POST. POSAD is responsible for accepting and reviewing public complaints regarding peace officer conduct, reviewing agency internal affairs investigation findings, and conducting additional investigations as needed. Any member of the public may file a complaint with POSAD regarding officer conduct that may constitute serious misconduct.
Cal. Penal Code § 13509.6 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Establishes the Peace Officer Standards Accountability Advisory Board. The Board consists of nine members: two current or former peace officers and seven members of the public. The Board reviews POSAD's findings and recommendations, conducts public hearings on decertification cases, and makes written recommendations to the POST Commission by majority vote.
Cal. Civil Code § 52.1 (Bane Act) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov As amended by SB 2, provides a civil cause of action for any person whose constitutional or statutory rights have been interfered with by threats, intimidation, or coercion by a public officer or employee. SB 2 removed prior barriers that had required proof of threats separate from the constitutional violation itself. The Bane Act applies to UC police officers as public employees exercising law enforcement authority.
Key Finding: SB 2 applies to all peace officers described in Penal Code § 830.2, which includes UC police officers. POST’s Guide to Peace Officer Decertification Proceedings explicitly confirms this. As established in Chapter 02, Section VI, the mandatory reporting requirement under § 13510.9 contains no exceptions — agencies must report all allegations of serious misconduct within 10 days, regardless of the outcome of internal investigation. POST’s own 2024 POSAD Annual Report identifies 174 agencies (28%) that have never submitted a single misconduct report.

III. Peace Officer Records and Transparency

California has enacted a series of statutes progressively expanding public access to peace officer personnel records, particularly those involving misconduct, use of force, and dishonesty.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
SB 1421 (2018) — Cal. Penal Code § 832.7(b) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Effective January 1, 2019, SB 1421 made certain categories of peace officer personnel records subject to public disclosure for the first time. Records that must be disclosed include: incidents involving the discharge of a firearm at a person; incidents involving use of force resulting in death or great bodily injury; sustained findings of sexual assault; and sustained findings of dishonesty in the reporting, investigation, or prosecution of a crime or misconduct. The California Supreme Court confirmed in Walnut Creek Police Officers' Assn. v. City of Walnut Creek (2019) that the law applies retroactively to records created before its effective date.
SB 16 (2021) — Cal. Penal Code § 832.7(b) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Effective January 1, 2022, SB 16 expanded the categories of peace officer records subject to mandatory disclosure beyond SB 1421. New categories include: sustained findings of unreasonable or excessive force; sustained findings of failure to intervene against another officer's unreasonable force; and sustained findings of participation in a law enforcement gang. SB 16 also established a minimum 5-year retention requirement for all peace officer personnel records subject to disclosure, and required agencies to reconstruct or disclose records going forward.
Cal. Penal Code § 832.5 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Requires every law enforcement agency in California to establish a procedure for investigating citizen complaints against its officers and to make complaint forms available to the public. Subdivision (b) requires each department to retain a copy of each citizen complaint, and the investigation report, for at least five years. This applies to all UC police departments. The complaint procedure must be publicly accessible.
Cal. Penal Code § 832.8 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Defines "personnel records" broadly as "any file maintained under an individual officer's name" by the employing agency. This includes personal data, medical history, employee advancement or discipline, complaints, and any information relating to the officer's job performance or fitness for duty. Subdivision (b) specifies that personnel records include "workers' compensation records" and "medical releases." This definition is significant in determining the scope of records an agency must preserve and, under certain conditions, disclose.
Source Note: The California Supreme Court's ruling on retroactive application of SB 1421 is reported in Walnut Creek Police Officers' Assn. v. City of Walnut Creek (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 940. SB 16's full legislative text is at leginfo.legislature.ca.gov.

IV. Public Safety Officers Procedural Bill of Rights (POBR)

The POBR, codified at Government Code §§ 3300–3313, establishes procedural protections for all public safety officers in California, including UC police officers. The Legislature has declared POBR to be "a matter of statewide concern" applicable to "all public safety officers … wherever situated within the State of California."

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Gov. Code § 3300 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Declares that the Legislature finds and declares that the rights and protections established in POBR constitute "a matter of statewide concern." The protections apply to all public safety officers "wherever situated within the State of California," including UC police officers. UC's constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 does not override POBR's statewide application.
Cal. Gov. Code § 3304 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Prohibits punitive action or denial of promotion based on the lawful exercise of rights under POBR. Subdivision (a) provides that no officer shall be subjected to punitive action for the exercise of POBR rights. Establishes a one-year statute of limitations for bringing disciplinary action against an officer from the date the agency discovers the alleged misconduct.
Cal. Gov. Code § 3305 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov The anti-shadow-file provision. Prohibits any adverse comment from being entered in a public safety officer's personnel file "or any other file used for any personnel purposes by his employer" without the officer first reading and signing the instrument containing the adverse comment. The phrase "or any other file" prevents agencies from maintaining unofficial, secondary, or reconstructed files to circumvent the officer's right to review adverse entries.
Cal. Gov. Code § 3306.5 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Subdivision (a) grants every public safety officer the right to inspect personnel files "that are used or have been used to determine that officer's qualifications for employment, promotion, additional compensation, or termination or other disciplinary action." Subdivision (b) imposes an affirmative duty on every employer to keep each officer's personnel file, or a true and correct copy, and to make it available within a reasonable period of time after a request.

V. Use of Force Standards

California enacted comprehensive use-of-force reforms in 2019 through two complementary bills: AB 392 (statutory standard) and SB 230 (training and policy requirements).

Statute Full Text Link Summary
AB 392 — Cal. Penal Code § 835a leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Effective January 1, 2020, redefined the standard for use of deadly force in California. Peace officers may use deadly force only when the officer "reasonably believes, based on the totality of the circumstances," that such force is "necessary" to defend against an imminent threat of death or serious bodily injury. Changed the prior standard from "reasonable" to "necessary," requiring officers to use de-escalation techniques, time, distance, and other resources when feasible before resorting to deadly force.
SB 230 — Cal. Penal Code § 7286 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Effective January 1, 2021, requires every law enforcement agency to maintain a use-of-force policy that includes de-escalation techniques, the duty to intercede when witnessing excessive force, reporting requirements for uses of force, and comprehensive training in de-escalation and alternatives to force. Agencies must make their use-of-force policies publicly accessible. Applies to all UC police departments.
AB 953 — Cal. Gov. Code § 12525.5 (RIPA) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov The Racial and Identity Profiling Act (RIPA) of 2015 requires law enforcement agencies to collect and report data on all stops conducted by their officers, including the perceived race, ethnicity, age, and gender of the person stopped; the reason for the stop; actions taken during the stop; and the outcome. Data is reported to the California Department of Justice. UC police departments are required to report RIPA data under the phased implementation schedule.

VI. Federal Civil Rights Statutes

UC police officers exercise state authority under color of law and are therefore subject to federal civil rights statutes. The following federal statutes provide both criminal penalties and civil causes of action for violations of constitutional rights by state actors.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
42 U.S.C. § 1983 law.cornell.edu Provides a civil cause of action against any person who, acting under color of state law, deprives another person of rights secured by the Constitution or federal law. UC police officers act under color of state law when performing their duties. Section 1983 allows individuals to sue officers in their personal capacity for damages, and agencies under Monell v. Department of Social Services (1978) where the violation resulted from an official policy, custom, or practice. Qualified immunity may apply to individual officers but does not apply to the entity itself.
42 U.S.C. § 1985 law.cornell.edu Provides a civil cause of action against two or more persons who conspire to deprive any person of equal protection of the laws or equal privileges and immunities under the laws. Subdivision (3) addresses conspiracies to deprive persons of civil rights based on race, class, or other protected characteristics. Unlike § 1983, a § 1985 claim requires proof of a racial or class-based discriminatory animus motivating the conspiracy.
Title VI — 42 U.S.C. § 2000d law.cornell.edu Prohibits discrimination on the basis of race, color, or national origin in any program or activity receiving federal financial assistance. The University of California receives substantial federal funding. If a UC police department engages in a pattern or practice of discriminatory policing, or if the university fails to address known patterns of discrimination within its police departments, Title VI may provide a basis for administrative enforcement by the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) or the U.S. Department of Justice.
Title IX — 20 U.S.C. § 1681 law.cornell.edu Prohibits sex-based discrimination in any education program or activity receiving federal financial assistance. Although primarily associated with campus sexual assault policies, Title IX may apply to UC police conduct where officers' actions — or failures to act — are alleged to constitute sex-based discrimination in the educational environment. UC's obligation to address gender-based violence on campus includes oversight of its police departments' handling of such cases.
18 U.S.C. § 242 law.cornell.edu Federal criminal statute prohibiting the willful deprivation of constitutional rights under color of law. Provides for criminal penalties including imprisonment for any person acting under color of law who willfully subjects an inhabitant of any state to the deprivation of rights, privileges, or immunities secured by the Constitution or laws of the United States. The U.S. Department of Justice, Civil Rights Division, has jurisdiction to prosecute violations.
34 U.S.C. § 12601 (Pattern-or-Practice) law.cornell.edu Authorizes the U.S. Attorney General to bring a civil action against any governmental authority when there is reasonable cause to believe the authority has engaged in a "pattern or practice" of conduct that deprives persons of constitutional rights. This is the statutory authority under which the DOJ has conducted pattern-or-practice investigations of police departments nationwide and entered into consent decrees requiring institutional reform.

VII. Campus Safety and Reporting (Clery Act)

The Jeanne Clery Disclosure of Campus Security Policy and Campus Crime Statistics Act requires all institutions of higher education participating in federal financial aid programs to disclose information about crime on and near their campuses.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
20 U.S.C. § 1092(f) (Clery Act) law.cornell.edu Requires institutions to compile and publish an Annual Security Report (ASR) containing campus crime statistics for the preceding three years; campus security policies, including those related to sexual assault, dating violence, domestic violence, and stalking; and crime prevention programs. Institutions must issue timely warnings for crimes that represent a serious or continuing threat to the campus community. The U.S. Department of Education enforces Clery Act compliance and may impose fines up to $69,733 per violation (as adjusted for inflation).
34 C.F.R. Part 668.46 ecfr.gov The implementing regulations for the Clery Act. Specifies the categories of crimes that must be reported, the geographic areas covered (on-campus, non-campus, public property), the requirements for timely warnings and emergency notifications, the obligations regarding campus security authorities (CSAs), and the procedures for compiling and distributing the Annual Security Report. UC police departments serve as the primary campus security authorities and primary data sources for Clery reporting at each campus.

VIII. Whistleblower and Anti-Retaliation Protections

Multiple overlapping federal and state statutes protect individuals who report misconduct, fraud, waste, or violations of law by public agencies and their officers.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Gov. Code §§ 8547–8547.12 (Whistleblower Protection Act) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov The California Whistleblower Protection Act protects state employees, including UC employees, who report "improper governmental activity." Section 8547.2 defines "improper governmental activity" to include "any activity by the University of California" that involves "any violation of state or federal law," "significant waste or misuse of state resources," or "gross misconduct, incompetence, or inefficiency." Section 8547.3 prohibits retaliation against employees who make protected disclosures. Section 8547.8 extends protections to UC employees specifically and grants the UC Board of Regents authority to adopt implementing procedures.
Cal. Labor Code § 1102.5 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Prohibits retaliation against any employee who reports a reasonably suspected violation of state or federal law to a government or law enforcement agency, a person with authority over the employee, or another employee with authority to investigate or correct the violation. As interpreted in Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, the burden-shifting framework under Labor Code § 1102.6 applies: once the employee shows the protected activity was a contributing factor in the adverse action, the employer must prove by clear and convincing evidence that it would have taken the same action regardless of the protected activity.
Cal. Labor Code § 132a leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Makes it a misdemeanor for any employer to discriminate against any employee because the employee has filed or made known the intention to file a workers' compensation claim. Provides for reinstatement, lost wages, and increased compensation of up to $10,000 as penalties. The Workers' Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB) has jurisdiction over § 132a claims. Section 132a applies to any adverse action taken because of a workers' compensation claim, including actions taken years after the original injury.
29 U.S.C. § 215(a)(3) (FLSA Anti-Retaliation) law.cornell.edu The Fair Labor Standards Act prohibits any employer from discharging or discriminating against any employee because such employee has filed any complaint or instituted any proceeding under the FLSA. This provision applies to UC police officers who file FLSA complaints regarding overtime compensation or other wage-and-hour violations. FUPOA's federal FLSA case against the Regents (the first of its kind against the UC system) invoked this protection.
Source Note: UC's own whistleblower policy states: "Not to [investigate] would, in and of itself, be an Improper Governmental Activity." UC Agriculture & Natural Resources, Whistleblower FAQ. UC Whistleblower Policy: policy.ucop.edu/doc/1100171/Whistleblower. UC Non-Retaliation Policy: policy.ucop.edu/doc/1100563/WPP.

IX. Public Records and Body Camera Disclosure

California law provides public access to government records through the California Public Records Act and mandates the release of body camera footage in specified circumstances.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Gov. Code § 7920.000 et seq. (CPRA) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov The California Public Records Act, recodified effective January 1, 2023 (formerly Government Code § 6250 et seq.), requires state and local agencies to make public records available for inspection. The Act applies to the University of California, including its police departments, for records that are not exempt under specific statutory provisions. CPRA requests may be used to obtain UC police policies, training records, budget documents, mutual aid agreements, and other non-exempt records.
AB 748 — Cal. Penal Code § 832.18 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Effective July 1, 2019, AB 748 requires law enforcement agencies to release audio and video recordings, including body camera footage, of critical incidents within 45 days of the incident. Critical incidents include officer-involved shootings and use of force resulting in death or great bodily injury. The footage is considered a public record under CPRA. Agencies may delay release only if it would substantially interfere with an active investigation, and must articulate specific reasons for any delay.

X. Employment and Anti-Discrimination Statutes

UC police officers and employees are protected by overlapping federal and state employment and anti-discrimination statutes.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
29 U.S.C. § 201 et seq. (FLSA) law.cornell.edu The Fair Labor Standards Act establishes minimum wage, overtime compensation, and recordkeeping requirements for employers, including public agencies. The Ninth Circuit confirmed in Benshoff v. City of Virginia Beach and related cases that public university police officers are entitled to FLSA overtime protections. The Federated University Police Officers' Association (FUPOA) filed the first FLSA case against the Regents, establishing that UC police officers were entitled to overtime compensation under federal law.
42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq. (ADA) law.cornell.edu The Americans with Disabilities Act prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities in employment, public services, and public accommodations. Title I (employment) and Title II (public services) apply to the University of California. Officers who are injured on duty and receive disability income may be protected under the ADA from adverse employment actions or benefits decisions based on their disability status.
29 U.S.C. § 621 et seq. (ADEA) law.cornell.edu The Age Discrimination in Employment Act prohibits employment discrimination against individuals 40 years of age or older. Applies to the University of California as a public employer. Relevant when policies or practices disproportionately affect older officers or retirees, including benefits determinations, CCW qualification standards, or retiree classification decisions.
Cal. Gov. Code § 12900 et seq. (FEHA) leginfo.legislature.ca.gov The California Fair Employment and Housing Act prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation, marital status, national origin, ancestry, mental and physical disability, medical condition, age (40+), genetic information, military and veteran status, and other protected characteristics. FEHA provides broader protections than federal law in several categories. Claims are filed with the California Civil Rights Department (formerly DFEH). FEHA applies to the University of California and all of its police departments.
18 U.S.C. § 926C (LEOSA) law.cornell.edu The Law Enforcement Officers Safety Act authorizes qualified retired law enforcement officers to carry concealed firearms nationwide, notwithstanding state or local laws. Subdivision (c)(3) establishes two independent qualification paths: subsection (A) requires 10 or more years of aggregate law enforcement service, OR subsection (B) requires the officer to have "separated from service … due to a service-connected disability, as determined by such agency." The two paths are connected by the word "or" — the disability exception at subsection (B) renders the 10-year service requirement at subsection (A) irrelevant for officers who separated due to service-connected disability.

XI. State Audit and Oversight Authority

Multiple state agencies have statutory authority to audit, investigate, and oversee the University of California and its police departments.

Statute Full Text Link Summary
Cal. Gov. Code § 8545.2 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Grants the California State Auditor "full access to all records, accounts, correspondence, property, and other files of any publicly created entity." The State Auditor's authority extends to the University of California and all of its departments, including police departments. This access authority supports both performance audits and investigations of waste, fraud, and abuse.
Cal. Gov. Code § 8546.7 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Provides that every contract involving the expenditure of public funds exceeding $10,000 "shall be subject to the examination and audit of the California State Auditor." The Legislature specifically intended this provision to apply to the Regents of the University of California. UC contracts with law enforcement equipment suppliers, mutual aid partners, and outside legal counsel are all subject to State Auditor examination.
Cal. Gov. Code § 12410 leginfo.legislature.ca.gov Grants the California State Controller authority over "all matters relating to the fiscal affairs of the state" and the disbursement of public funds. The Controller has independent authority to audit claims against the state, examine accounts of state agencies, and issue findings regarding compliance with fiscal requirements. UC payroll, pension disbursements, and benefit payments are subject to Controller oversight.
UC Regents Policy 7503 regents.universityofcalifornia.edu UC's own policy on Compliance with State Audits. Requires that the Chief Compliance and Audit Officer coordinate with the State Auditor. Includes a prohibition on interference with protected disclosures to the State Auditor: employees "shall not … use or attempt to use the official authority or influence of the employee for the purpose of intimidating, threatening, coercing, commanding, or attempting to intimidate, threaten, coerce, or command any person for the purpose of interfering with the right of that person to make a good faith communication to the State Auditor."

XII. Continuing Violation and Tolling Doctrines

The following legal doctrines affect the timeliness of claims arising from UC police misconduct, institutional failures, and employment-related violations. These doctrines are particularly relevant where institutional conduct spans years or decades.

Doctrine / Statute Key Authority Summary
Continuing Violation Doctrine Aryeh v. Canon Business Solutions (2013) 55 Cal.4th 1185 The California Supreme Court held in Aryeh that when an employer's unlawful conduct is ongoing rather than a discrete act, the statute of limitations does not begin to run until the last act in the series of violations. Applied in the employment context, if an employer engages in a continuing course of discriminatory or retaliatory conduct, the entire pattern of conduct — not just the most recent act — may be actionable, provided the last act occurs within the limitations period.
Discovery Rule Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797 Under the discovery rule, a cause of action does not accrue until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the factual basis for the claim. The California Supreme Court held that accrual is postponed when the plaintiff "reasonably and in good faith" did not know of the injury or its wrongful cause. This doctrine is significant where institutional records have been concealed, destroyed, or rendered inaccessible, such that the affected individual could not have known the full scope of the harm.
Equitable Tolling McDonald v. Antelope Valley Community College Dist. (2008) 45 Cal.4th 88 Equitable tolling suspends the statute of limitations while a plaintiff pursues an alternative remedy in a different forum. The California Supreme Court recognized that when an employee has filed a complaint with an administrative agency (such as the EEOC, CRD/DFEH, or WCAB), the statute of limitations on related civil claims may be tolled during the pendency of the administrative proceeding, provided the defendant received adequate notice of the claims.
Equitable Estoppel Lantzy v. Centex Homes (2003) 31 Cal.4th 363 An employer or public agency may be estopped from raising a statute of limitations defense where its own conduct — such as concealing facts, making misrepresentations, or failing to disclose information it was obligated to provide — prevented the plaintiff from timely discovering or pursuing the claim. Applied where an institution's own record-keeping failures, contradictory classifications, or administrative opacity delayed the claimant's ability to understand the full scope of institutional actions.
Key Finding: The continuing violation doctrine, discovery rule, equitable tolling, and equitable estoppel collectively establish that institutional conduct spanning years or decades may remain actionable — particularly where the institution's own record-keeping practices have obscured the nature, scope, or duration of the violations. A pattern of contradictory classifications, destroyed records, and administrative non-responsiveness may support the application of multiple tolling doctrines simultaneously.
