I. Systemwide Workforce Overview

The University of California operates ten separate campus police departments, each led by its own chief of police. There is no single "chief of the UCPD" — each campus department functions as an independent law enforcement agency under the overall governance of the UC Regents and the administrative oversight of the Office of the President's Systemwide Community Safety office.

As of January 2025, the University of California employs more than 292 sworn police officers systemwide across all ten campuses, as reported by UCnet (the UC employee resources portal) in connection with the Federated University Police Officers' Association (FUPOA) bargaining unit description. These officers provide the full range of law enforcement services at all UC campuses, including patrol, investigation, crime prevention, emergency response, mutual aid, and specialized services such as executive protection and event security.

In addition to sworn officers, each UC police department employs non-sworn professional staff including dispatchers, community service officers (CSOs), public safety responders, records clerks, administrative personnel, and, at some campuses, police social workers. The total workforce across all ten departments — sworn and non-sworn combined — significantly exceeds the 292 sworn officer count.

The UC systemwide data dashboards, maintained by the Office of Institutional Research and Academic Planning (IRAP) in coordination with UCOP's Systemwide Community Safety office, publish budget and workforce data for all ten campuses. The dashboards are publicly accessible and present data on operating expenses, personnel counts, and workforce composition. Each campus's data reflects its unique organizational and budget model.

II. Bargaining Unit Structure and FUPOA

UC police officers are represented by the Federated University Police Officers' Association (FUPOA), which serves as the exclusive bargaining representative for the Police Officers (PA) bargaining unit across the UC system. FUPOA was founded in the early 1990s at UCLA, during a period of institutional crisis documented in Chapter 07 of this series (formally incorporated June 16, 2003, per California Secretary of State records), and achieved system-wide recognition through the Higher Education Employer-Employee Relations Act (HEERA), Government Code §§ 3560–3599.

The FUPOA bargaining unit (Unit PA) covers sworn police officers at all ten UC campuses. The current collective bargaining agreement between the Regents of the University of California and FUPOA establishes compensation scales, working conditions, overtime provisions, grievance procedures, and other terms and conditions of employment. FUPOA's first major legal action — a federal Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) case against the Regents — established that UC police officers were entitled to federal overtime protections, representing the first FLSA case ever filed against the UC system (see Chapter 07 of this series for the full history).

Police management personnel — lieutenants, captains, and chiefs — are generally not represented by FUPOA and are covered under separate management and senior professional (MSP) appointment terms.

Source Note: FUPOA bargaining unit information: ucnet.universityofcalifornia.edu. HEERA: Cal. Gov. Code §§ 3560–3599. For the complete history of FUPOA's founding and the FLSA case, see Chapter 07 of this series.

III. Sworn Officer Compensation

UC police officer compensation reflects the specialized nature of campus law enforcement and the high cost of living in California's university communities. As published by the UCLA Police Department (effective June 22, 2025), the total compensation range for a sworn police officer is approximately $100,900 to $170,300 per year. This range includes base salary plus additional pay components: Longevity Pay, Advanced POST Certificate Pay, Specialty Pay (up to two specialty assignments), Systemwide Response Team Pay, Nighttime Shift Differential Pay, Education Pay, Bilingual Pay, and Uniform and Fitness Allowances.

Police Trainees — non-sworn recruits attending a POST-certified basic academy at university expense — are compensated during their training period. Upon successful completion of the academy and promotion to sworn Police Officer, the officer must complete a Field Training Program (FTP) and a probationary period of no less than 12 months.

Lateral candidates — officers transferring from other law enforcement agencies with current POST Basic Certificates — receive initial salary placement based on years of previous law enforcement experience and training. Peace officers from other states must complete the POST Basic Course Waiver (BCW) process before being employed as California peace officers.

Source Note: UCLA PD compensation data (effective June 22, 2025): ucpd.ucla.edu — Police Officer Position. Compensation ranges vary by campus and are subject to collective bargaining.

IV. Non-Sworn Staffing: CSOs, Dispatchers, and Professional Staff

Each UC police department employs a complement of non-sworn staff that extends the department's public safety capabilities. Community Service Officers (CSOs) are non-sworn student employees who perform functions including night escorts, building checks, general citizen assistance, and serving as additional "eyes and ears" for the department. Almost all UC police departments operate CSO programs. Because CSOs are not sworn peace officers, they do not carry firearms or exercise arrest authority.

Each department staffs a Communications Division, which serves as the police dispatch center and the 9-1-1 access point for each UC campus. Dispatchers are non-sworn professional staff who manage emergency and non-emergency calls, coordinate officer response, and maintain communications logs.

Several UC campuses have added Police Social Workers to their public safety staffing models in recent years, reflecting the UC Community Safety Plan's emphasis on expanding the definition of campus safety beyond traditional law enforcement. These social workers are embedded within the police department and respond to calls involving mental health crises, substance abuse, homelessness, and other situations where a clinical response may be more appropriate than a law enforcement response.

V. Budget Structure and Funding Sources

UC police department budgets are funded primarily through campus general funds, with additional revenue from parking enforcement, event security fees, reimbursable services (such as mutual aid deployments and security details), and, at some campuses, medical center security contracts. Each campus police department's budget reflects its unique organizational model, campus size, student and employee population, geographic footprint, and the presence or absence of a medical center.

The UC Community Safety Budget and Workforce Dashboard publishes operating expense data for all ten campuses. Budget categories typically include personnel costs (salaries, benefits, overtime), operating expenses (equipment, vehicles, technology, training), and capital expenditures. Personnel costs constitute the largest share of each department's budget, consistent with law enforcement agencies generally.

Each campus's budget and workforce data are reported to UCOP under the systemwide reporting requirements established by the 2021 UC Community Safety Plan. Prior to 2022, budgetary reporting formats varied by campus. The standardization of budget reporting across all ten departments was a key milestone in the Community Safety Plan's transparency goals.

Key Finding: The UC Community Safety Budget and Workforce Dashboard, publicly accessible since 2022, represents the first time UC police department budget and staffing data have been standardized and published in a uniform format across all ten campuses. Prior to this dashboard, each campus reported budget data independently, in varying formats, making systemwide comparison and trend analysis difficult. The interactive Tableau-based dashboards allow the public to view data by campus, by year, and by category.

VI. Growth Trends and Transformation

The UC police system has undergone significant transformation over its seven-decade history. From the original appointment of campus watchmen in the early twentieth century, through the Regents' 1947 establishment of a fully constituted police agency, to the present-day system of ten independent departments with 292+ sworn officers and statewide jurisdiction, the growth trajectory reflects both the expansion of the UC system itself and the increasing complexity of campus public safety.

The UC system has grown from a single campus in Berkeley to ten campuses serving more than 300,000 students and 265,000 faculty and staff, with 2.5 million alumni worldwide. Each campus expansion — from UC San Diego's establishment in 1960 through UC Merced's opening in 2005 — brought with it the creation of a new police department with its own command structure, staffing, and budget.

Recent transformation initiatives, driven by the 2021 UC Community Safety Plan, have shifted the staffing model away from exclusively sworn-officer responses toward a "tiered" approach that incorporates non-sworn responders, social workers, and mental health professionals alongside traditional law enforcement. This transformation is ongoing and has resulted in changes to staffing ratios at several campuses.

VII. Recruitment, Retention, and Vacancy Challenges

UC police departments, like law enforcement agencies nationwide, face significant recruitment and retention challenges. The requirement for POST certification, the competitive compensation landscape in California's urban markets, the extended training pipeline (academy plus field training plus probation), and the unique demands of campus policing all affect UC police departments' ability to attract and retain sworn officers.

FUPOA has historically advocated for competitive compensation, improved working conditions, and staffing levels sufficient to provide adequate public safety coverage across UC campuses. The collective bargaining process between FUPOA and the Regents determines the compensation and benefit packages that directly affect recruitment and retention.

The UC Community Safety Budget and Workforce Dashboard tracks filled and vacant positions across the ten campuses, providing transparency into staffing levels and vacancy rates. Vacancy data is significant because unfilled sworn officer positions result in increased overtime for remaining officers, reduced patrol coverage, and potential impacts on response times and service delivery.

VIII. The UC Community Safety Plan's Impact on Staffing Models

The 2021 UC Community Safety Plan called for a fundamental reimagination of campus safety that extends beyond traditional policing. The Plan established several directives that directly affect police department staffing and budgets. Each campus was directed to establish alternative response programs for calls involving mental health crises, substance abuse, and other situations that do not require a law enforcement response. The Plan directed UCOP to designate a full-time position to coordinate campus safety systemwide, ensure continuous improvement, and monitor implementation.

The Plan also directed each campus to establish independent Police Accountability Boards (PABs) — a new governance function that requires administrative staffing and operational support. The creation of systemwide data dashboards required dedicated data collection, analysis, and reporting staff at both the campus and UCOP levels.

These initiatives represent an expansion of the total resources devoted to campus safety, though not all new resources are allocated to sworn law enforcement positions. The transformation from a purely law enforcement model to a broader community safety model is reflected in the changing composition of campus safety staffing at several campuses, with some departments adding non-sworn positions (social workers, public safety responders, ambassadors) while holding sworn officer counts steady or allowing them to fluctuate with attrition.
