I. POST’s Own Words

The California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training publishes its mission, vision, and values on its official website. They read as follows:

Mission: “To continually enhance the professionalism of California law enforcement in serving its communities.”

Vision: “POST, the leader in innovative partnerships and quality services.”

Values: “Integrity, Professionalism, Innovation, Collaboration.”

Source: California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training, official mission and values — https://post.ca.gov

These are the words of a partner — an institution whose identity is built on collaboration with, and service to, the profession it represents. POST describes itself not as a regulator, not as an enforcer, but as a leader in “innovative partnerships.”

On September 30, 2021, Governor Gavin Newsom signed Senate Bill 2 — the Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act — and assigned this partner, this collaborator, the authority to end a police officer’s career. The Act created California’s first comprehensive statewide system for revoking or suspending a peace officer’s certification for serious misconduct. Prior to SB 2, California was one of only four states without a general decertification authority.

Source: Senate Bill 2 (2021–2022 Session) — Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act — https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202120220SB2

The question this chapter examines is not whether SB 2 was necessary — the Legislature’s findings make that case decisively. The question is whether the body chosen to implement it — a commission dominated by the profession it is now charged with disciplining — can credibly fulfill both its legacy role as a partner and its new role as an enforcer. The legal principle that answers this question is older than the Constitution itself.

II. The Principle: Nemo Iudex in Causa Sua

Nemo iudex in causa sua. No one shall be a judge in their own cause. This is a foundational principle of natural justice recognized across every legal system derived from Roman law. It is not an opinion. It is not a policy preference. It is a structural requirement for legitimacy — the proposition that adjudicatory authority requires independence from the subject of that adjudication. Without that independence, the legitimacy of the outcome is compromised regardless of what the outcome is.

The principle appears explicitly in Sir Edward Coke’s Dr. Bonham’s Case (1610) and was elaborated by Blackstone in his Commentaries on the Laws of England. It is one of the two pillars of natural justice — the other being audi alteram partem (hear the other side). Modern administrative law in the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and every other common law jurisdiction treats the principle as a threshold requirement for valid adjudication. Its application is not limited to courts. It applies to any body that exercises authority to impose consequences on identifiable subjects.

The following subsections illustrate the principle’s application across professional domains — each an example of what happens when the regulator and the regulated are the same people, and each a precedent for the analysis that follows.

Medicine

State medical boards are structured to include physician members — but a physician under investigation by the board is recused from participation in proceedings concerning that investigation. The principle extends further: a board dominated by practicing physicians in a medical specialty has a documented structural incentive to protect its own. Academic literature on medical board performance consistently identifies the tension between the peer-review tradition in medicine — physicians judging physicians — and the independence required for credible discipline. The structural safeguard is independence: those who regulate must be structurally independent of those they regulate, and the adjudicator of any specific case must have no interest in its outcome.

Law — Attorney Discipline

The legal profession’s self-regulatory system illustrates the nemo iudex principle with particular clarity — because it has been analyzed, criticized, and selectively reformed over decades. The American Bar Association’s Model Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 8.3 uses the mandatory “shall inform” when an attorney knows of another attorney’s violation. Washington State’s equivalent rule uses the permissive “should inform” — a choice that materially weakens the reporting obligation. Washington, California, and Georgia were among the jurisdictions that historically rejected mandatory reporting of attorney misconduct. California finally adopted a mandatory lawyer reporting requirement in 2023 (Rule 8.3), but notably does not require reporting of judicial misconduct. The Washington Supreme Court retains “exclusive responsibility” for lawyer discipline: lawyers are disciplined by lawyers, applying standards written by lawyers, through a process controlled by lawyers.

Source: NW Sidebar (Washington State Bar Association): Attorney professional misconduct reporting — https://nwsidebar.wsba.org/2013/09/24/professional-misconduct-lawyer/

The structural point is not that attorneys are corrupt. The structural point is that self-regulation by any profession creates an inherent conflict between the institution’s interest in protecting its members and its obligation to protect the public. Mandatory external reporting requirements exist precisely because that conflict is real and documented.

Religion

The Catholic Church’s decades-long failure to address clergy sexual abuse is the definitive modern example of nemo iudex operating in reverse — the institution investigating its own members, applying its own standards, using its own investigators, and reaching its own conclusions. The structural incentive was to protect the institution, not the victims. Perpetrators were transferred rather than prosecuted. Investigations were conducted by superiors rather than independent authorities. Documentation was concealed within institutional archives rather than shared with law enforcement.

Independent external review was not adopted voluntarily. It was forced by civil litigation, criminal prosecution, legislative reform, and public outrage sustained over decades. The structural lesson is categorical: when an institution investigates misconduct by its own members, the institutional interest in self-preservation systematically competes with the obligation to reach accurate and just conclusions. The conflict does not require bad faith. It operates automatically, as a function of structure.

Corporate Governance

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX, Pub. L. 107-204) exists precisely because corporations cannot audit themselves. Enron’s board approved the transactions that destroyed the company. Arthur Andersen, Enron’s auditor, had a financial relationship with its audit client that compromised its independence. SOX responded by requiring external auditors, independent audit committees composed of non-management directors, mandatory CEO and CFO certification of financial statements, and criminal penalties for obstruction — structural separations that exist because self-regulation failed catastrophically and at enormous public cost.

The principle embedded in SOX is identical to the principle in Dr. Bonham’s Case: the person who benefits from a favorable outcome cannot credibly serve as the auditor of that outcome. Congress did not pass SOX because corporate executives are dishonest. It passed SOX because the structural incentives created by self-auditing are incompatible with independent verification — regardless of the personal integrity of the individuals involved.

Federal Policing — Consent Decrees

34 U.S.C. § 12601 authorizes the United States Department of Justice to investigate and sue law enforcement agencies engaged in a pattern or practice of constitutional violations. The remedy — a federal consent decree with an independent monitor — exists because departments cannot credibly investigate their own systemic failures. The LAPD consent decree, entered in 2001 and not terminated until 2013, required twelve years of external oversight before the federal court found that the department had achieved and was likely to maintain compliance.

The structural premise of 34 U.S.C. § 12601 is explicit: when the institution is the problem, the institution cannot also be the solution. External oversight — a federal court, an independent monitor, and the Department of Justice — is required because the internal accountability mechanisms of the institution have demonstrably failed. The same logic that produced consent decrees for municipal police departments is the logic that governs the question of POST’s composition.

Each of these examples follows the same pattern: when self-regulation meets self-interest, the public always loses. The principle is universal. It applies to doctors, lawyers, priests, and corporations. And it applies — with particular force — to the body California chose to regulate its police officers, and to the constitutionally autonomous institution whose officers it is charged with overseeing.

Key Finding: Ten of the eighteen POST commissioners are current or former sworn law enforcement officers. It did not apply the same principle to the Commission itself — the body that holds final authority over every decertification decision.

IV. The Structural Collapse of Internal Reporting

The inherent conflict of interest identified in Section II is not theoretical. The documented record across the University of California police system demonstrates precisely how the principle operates in practice.

As established in Chapter 02, the 2023 ECAS systemwide audit found that at least one UC campus lacked a policy prohibiting its police department from conducting an investigation of its own Chief of Police — and recommended that such a policy be enacted to address the inherent conflict of interest in a department investigating its own chief. The audit identified the absence of such a policy as a systemic gap, not an anomaly.

As documented in Chapter 01, the structural comparison between UC police departments and municipal agencies reveals this dynamic at the governance level: in the UC system, the chancellor is responsible for overseeing the police chief — but the chancellor is also the chief’s employer, creating an inherent conflict of interest. When the chancellor is implicated in the misconduct being reported, the institutional reporting structure collapses entirely. There is no external authority to whom the complaint can be escalated within the campus governance structure.

Act II documents a thirty-seven-year case study in this structural collapse. When an officer at UCLA PD documented corruption and command-level misconduct in the early 1990s, the institutional reporting chain failed at every level: department supervisors who were the perpetrators, Campus Human Resources which failed to act, the Chancellor who was implicated, and the UC President who did not respond. The officer exhausted every available institutional channel — including the District Attorney’s Office, the FBI, the Bureau of State Audits, and the press — because every internal channel was either structurally compromised or unresponsive. The question that case presents — “Who does a police officer report the Chancellor’s misconduct to?” — is the nemo iudex principle made operational.

Structural Observation: A retired senior LAPD officer with twenty years of experience in internal affairs has observed that his department arrested, terminated, or disciplined officers “every single day” — and the public never heard about most of it. The system works when the institution wants it to work. It fails when the institution has something to protect.

V. The Regents’ Guards: Constitutional Autonomy as Structural Insulation

The nemo iudex principle identified in Section II, and the POST composition documented in Section III, apply to every law enforcement agency in California. But the University of California police system presents an additional structural layer that no municipal department possesses — a layer that makes external oversight not merely difficult, but constitutionally constrained.

Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution, adopted in 1879, established the University of California as a “public trust” administered by the Board of Regents “with full powers of organization and government.” This provision has been interpreted by California courts to grant UC a degree of constitutional autonomy that exceeds even that of charter cities. As the University’s own legal counsel argued before the California Supreme Court: “the University’s constitutional autonomy is greater than that of charter cities.” (People v. Lofchie (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 240, 262.)

The practical consequence of this autonomy is a catalog of exemptions from state and local law that no other employer in California possesses. Courts have consistently held that matters relating to UC’s internal operations are “internal affairs of the university” that do not fall within the areas the Legislature may regulate. The following exemptions have been confirmed by court decisions or constitutional provision:

Labor and Employment Exemptions

State Minimum Wage Laws. The Regents are not subject to California’s minimum wage laws. In Gomez v. Regents of the University of California (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 386, the California Court of Appeal held that the Regents’ timekeeping procedures were “matters of internal affairs of the university that do not come within any of the exceptions to the Regents’ constitutional immunity.”

Prevailing Wage Laws. Courts have held that prevailing wage requirements are internal affairs of UC, exempt from state mandate.

Overtime Pay Laws. The same constitutional immunity applies. The Regents set their own overtime standards.

Meal and Rest Break Requirements. State-mandated meal and rest break standards do not apply to UC employees as a matter of constitutional immunity.

Paid Sick Leave Mandates. UC is exempt from state-mandated paid sick leave requirements, though it maintains internal policies.

Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA). UC employees cannot bring PAGA claims against the Regents. The Court of Appeal in Gomez held that because the underlying labor law claims failed against the Regents, the derivative PAGA claims also failed.

Anti-Outsourcing Restrictions. UC is exempt from state contracting-out rules governing the non-emergency use of personal service contracts. The Regents adopted their own Regents Policy 5402 in 2019, but it is self-enforced — the Legislature has no authority to compel compliance.

Tax and Fiscal Exemptions

Federal Income Tax. The Regents are recognized by the IRS as a tax-exempt organization under Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code and classified as a public charity under Code Section 509(a)(1).

State and Local Property Tax. UC property is exempt from property taxation under Article XIII, Section 3 of the California Constitution. In Regents of University of California v. State Board of Equalization (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 660, the court confirmed that property used by the University of California falls within the constitutional exemption for “state universities.”

Local Business Tax. In City and County of San Francisco v. The Regents of the University of California (2019), the California Supreme Court addressed UC’s immunity from local business taxation, holding that the University’s constitutional status as a state entity constrains the application of municipal tax ordinances.

Regulatory Exemptions

Local Zoning and Building Codes. In Regents of University of California v. Superior Court (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 852, the Court of Appeal held that UCSF’s proposed hospital was exempt from the entirety of San Francisco’s city planning code. The court stated that “the Regents are thus immune from local regulation when operating in their governmental capacity, unless the state expressly waives that immunity through statute or the state constitution.”

Workers’ Compensation. UC operates a self-insured workers’ compensation program rather than participating in the state system. UC’s Proof of Self-Insurance Coverage confirms that “The Regents of the University of California is a governmental entity that has a self-funded retention for liabilities.” The claims administration, fiduciary activity, and program management are handled internally by the Office of Risk Services.

Labor Relations Framework. UC employees are governed by the Higher Education Employer-Employee Relations Act (HEERA) rather than the Meyers-Milias-Brown Act (MMBA) that covers municipal employees. This means UC police officers — represented by FUPOA — operate under a different labor relations framework than every municipal police officer in California, with different rules, different boards, and different grievance procedures.

The Scale: 216,200 Workers

According to the California Senate Committee on Elections and Constitutional Amendments’ analysis of Assembly Constitutional Amendment 6 (ACA 6, Haney, 2023–2024 Session), as of 2022, the University of California employed 73,012 academic employees and 143,188 non-academic employees — a total of 216,200 workers “potentially exempt from various state labor laws due to the UC’s constitutional exemption.” The California Professional Firefighters, in their letter supporting ACA 6, stated that UC is “the only employer in the state that is not subject to basic labor standards such as minimum wage, overtime pay, equal pay, sick leave, and much more.”

ACA 6 proposed a constitutional amendment to require UC to follow the same labor standards as every other public agency in California. It required a constitutional amendment because the Legislature cannot fix this with a regular bill — the Regents’ autonomy is embedded in the Constitution itself. ACA 6 failed in committee. The exemption stands.

The Structural Consequence for Policing

Into this constitutionally insulated institution, the state placed police departments with full peace officer powers under Penal Code § 830.2(b) — statewide arrest authority, firearms, less-lethal weapons, and the authority to use deadly force. These officers hold the same POST certification, the same training requirements, and the same legal authority as officers in any municipal department in California. They are governed by the same SB 2 decertification standards. They are subject to the same Penal Code § 13510.9 mandatory reporting requirements.

But the institution that employs them operates behind a constitutional wall that no municipal department possesses. A municipal police chief answers to a city manager or city council — elected officials who can be voted out. A UC police chief answers to a chancellor who answers to the Regents — appointed officials with constitutional autonomy that the Legislature cannot override without a vote of the People. A municipal department is subject to city zoning, city taxes, state labor law, and state contracting rules. A UC police department operates within an institution that is exempt from all of these.

As established in Chapter 01, Section XI, this constitutional autonomy does not exempt UC police officers from POST, SB 2, AB 48, the Clery Act, or 34 U.S.C. § 12601. But it does insulate the institution that governs those officers from the accountability mechanisms that apply to every municipal employer in California. The laws apply to the officers. The institution that employs, directs, investigates, disciplines, and can silence those officers operates under a different set of rules.

The officers and chiefs caught inside this structure are not the beneficiaries of the Regents’ autonomy — they are its casualties. A UC police chief who wants to hold his command staff accountable must navigate a governance structure that answers to no external authority. A UC officer who blows the whistle on misconduct has no city council to appeal to, no elected mayor to petition, no state labor law to invoke. The constitutional wall that shields the Regents from legislative oversight does not protect the officers. It traps them. Every exemption catalogued above — from minimum wage to PAGA to zoning immunity — is an exemption the Regents enjoy, not the rank and file. The officers carry the same badge, the same authority, and the same personal liability as any municipal officer in California. They simply work for the one employer in the state that has placed itself beyond the reach of the People’s elected representatives. No kings — that was the constitutional promise. Article IX, Section 9 broke it.

Critical Finding: The University of California is the only employer in California that is constitutionally exempt from state minimum wage, overtime, prevailing wage, and other basic labor standards — an exemption affecting 216,200 workers that can only be changed by a vote of the People. Into this uniquely insulated institution, the state placed police departments with full peace officer powers. The result is a law enforcement system that is legally equivalent to any municipal department in its authority to arrest, detain, and use force, but structurally insulated from the governance accountability that constrains every other law enforcement employer in the state. When POST — a commission dominated by the profession it regulates — is asked to oversee officers employed by an institution with constitutional autonomy exceeding that of charter cities, the structural barriers to effective oversight are compounded.

VI. 174 Agencies and the Silence

POST’s own data quantifies the gap between the law’s requirements and the reality of compliance.

California Penal Code § 13510.9 requires every law enforcement agency to report allegations of serious misconduct to POST within ten days. The obligation is triggered by the allegation itself — not by the outcome of any investigation, not by a finding of probable cause, and not by the conclusion of any internal review. The ten-day clock begins when the agency becomes aware of the allegation.

According to POST’s 2024 POSAD Annual Report (page 18), 174 California law enforcement agencies — representing 28% of all agencies subject to the requirement — have never submitted a single Agency Misconduct Report to POST. Of those 174 agencies, 167 had fewer than 50 sworn officers, 4 had between 50 and 99 sworn officers, and 1 had 100 or more sworn officers. The combined sworn officer population represented by these agencies is not negligible: 28% of California’s law enforcement agencies have generated no reportable misconduct — in any year — since the statute took effect.

The same report documents that the average supplemental response time from agencies — the time required to provide additional documentation requested by POST after an initial report — was 241 days. The statute mandates a ten-day initial reporting window. The average supplemental response time is 24 times that figure.

In two years of operation, the Peace Officer Standards Accountability Advisory Board conducted only ten advisory board hearings — hearings in which a full decertification or suspension proceeding was convened and a recommendation made to the Commission. In July 2025, POST’s own Advisory Board specifically identified the UCSF Police Department as a non-reporting agency — an agency that, despite the statutory mandate, had submitted no Agency Misconduct Reports to POST.

Sources: POST 2024 POSAD Annual Report — https://post.ca.gov/Data-Warehouse
UCSF Police Department non-reporting identification — https://citizenportal.ai/articles/6165735/
Key Finding: Nearly one in three California law enforcement agencies has never reported a single allegation of misconduct to POST — despite a statutory mandate requiring reporting within ten days. POST’s response to this documented, large-scale non-compliance has included neither public enforcement action nor loss of certification authority for non-reporting agencies. The partner has not bitten.

VII. The Cultural Disconnect

The evidence documented in this chapter leads to a conclusion that is structural, not personal. POST cannot simultaneously serve as a law enforcement partner and a law enforcement regulator. These roles are mutually exclusive — for the same reason a chief of police cannot credibly investigate his own corruption, a priest cannot investigate abuse within his own parish, and a corporation cannot audit its own financial statements.

There is a term for this in law: inherent conflict of interest. There is a term for it in natural justice: nemo iudex in causa sua. And there is a term for what happens when the conflict is left unresolved: regulatory capture.

SB 2 gave POST teeth. POST’s institutional culture — its self-described mission as a partner and collaborator, its commission dominated by the profession it regulates, its tolerance of 28% non-compliance with mandatory reporting — prevents it from biting.

The Legislature understood this principle when it designed the Advisory Board with a seven-to-two public-to-law-enforcement ratio. It did not apply the same structural safeguard to the Commission that holds the final authority over every decertification decision in California. That asymmetry is not incidental. It is the structural contradiction at the heart of California police oversight.

Critical Finding: The Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training operates under a structural conflict of interest that is incompatible with its enforcement mandate under SB 2. A commission dominated by active law enforcement officers, sheriffs, and police union officials cannot credibly adjudicate the decertification of police officers. The nemo iudex principle — recognized across every legal system derived from Roman law — requires that the adjudicator be structurally independent of those subject to adjudication. POST’s current composition fails this test.

VIII. What This Collection Documents

This chapter establishes the structural framework. The chapters that follow document the evidence.

Act I (Chapters 01–10) documents the regulatory framework governing UC police departments and the systematic gaps in oversight, reporting, and accountability — from consent decree thresholds to POST compliance to use-of-force reporting. Each chapter applies the documented federal standard to the documented UC police record. The recurring structural finding is the same in every chapter: the oversight mechanisms that exist have not been applied, and the mechanisms that would apply them have a structural interest in not applying them.

Act II documents one officer’s thirty-seven-year case study in what happens when every internal reporting channel is structurally compromised — from the first whistleblower complaint in 1992 through the present day. It is not a story of individual corruption. It is a story of structural failure: each institutional actor behaved in the way the structure incentivized, and the result was the systematic suppression of documented misconduct at the command level of a UC police department.

Act III documents the formal audit request and the institutional responses. It records how the documented findings of Acts I and II were presented to oversight bodies and what those bodies did — and did not — do in response.

The Epilogue documents what happened when the courts began to catch up. It is the record of the legal proceedings, rulings, and findings that followed the completion of the administrative record documented in Acts I through III.

The principle established in this chapter — that no one shall be a judge in their own cause — is the thread that connects every chapter, every act, and every documented failure in this collection. It is not an accusation. It is a structural observation. And it is as old as the law itself.
