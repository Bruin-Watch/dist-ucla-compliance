I. Purpose and Methodology

This chapter places the ten UC police departments documented in Parts 1–9 of this series in comparative context with similarly-sized California municipal police agencies and, critically, with the documented standards that have triggered federal pattern-or-practice intervention under 34 U.S.C. § 12601 (formerly 42 U.S.C. § 14141) in municipal police departments across the United States.

The purpose is twofold. First, for the California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST): to identify structural differences that may affect accountability, transparency, and public oversight — factors directly relevant to POST's regulatory mission over all ten UC police departments under Penal Code § 830.2(b). Second, for any federal authority with jurisdiction under 34 U.S.C. § 12601: to document whether the factual record compiled across nine prior parts of this series meets the documented threshold for a pattern-or-practice investigation of one or more UC police departments.

The UC police system comprises ten independent departments with a combined sworn force of approximately 292+ officers (per UCnet's FUPOA bargaining unit description, updated January 22, 2025). Individual departments range from fewer than 15 sworn officers (UC Merced) to approximately 60+ sworn officers (UCLA, UC Berkeley). This size range corresponds to California municipal departments serving communities of approximately 15,000 to 75,000 residents. Comparable California municipal agencies in the 15–80 sworn officer range include departments such as the cities of Davis (~69,000), Santa Cruz (~65,000), Culver City (~40,000), and Beverly Hills (~33,000).

The comparison operates on two levels: structural (examining how the same legal obligations manifest differently based on governance model) and substantive (examining whether the documented factual record of the UC police system over the past 37 years — including excessive force, racial profiling, evidence tampering, whistleblower retaliation, internal review failures, reporting non-compliance, and leadership instability — rises to the level that has triggered federal consent decrees in municipal departments).

Source Note: UC sworn officer count: UCnet — FUPOA Bargaining Unit. California staffing data: Public Policy Institute of California (PPIC), 2024. Population data: U.S. Census Bureau. Federal consent decree authority: 34 U.S.C. § 12601.

II. The Structural Comparison: What Makes UC Police Different

UC police officers hold the same peace officer powers as any municipal officer in California. Under Penal Code § 830.2(b), they are fully sworn peace officers with statewide arrest authority. Under Education Code § 92600, their primary jurisdiction extends to UC property and a one-mile radius. They attend the same POST-certified academies, meet the same certification requirements, and are subject to the same decertification standards under SB 2 (the Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act of 2021). (For a complete analysis of POST’s regulatory authority over UC police departments, including SB 2, POSAD, and mandatory reporting requirements, see Chapter 02.)

Despite this legal equivalence, UC police departments differ from municipal agencies in several structural dimensions:

Key Finding: The most significant structural difference is the governance model. Municipal police chiefs answer to elected officials who are accountable to voters. UC police chiefs answer to appointed administrators who are accountable to the UC Regents — a body with constitutional autonomy that insulates it from ordinary legislative oversight. This creates an accountability chain that is longer, less transparent, and less directly connected to the communities served than the municipal model. Critically, despite holding identical peace officer powers and identical POST obligations, UC police departments have never been the subject of a formal federal pattern-or-practice investigation — a fact that raises the question of whether the Regents' constitutional autonomy has functioned as a structural shield against the type of federal oversight applied to similarly-situated municipal agencies.

III. Staffing and Compensation Comparison

According to the Public Policy Institute of California (PPIC), California had approximately 76,300 sworn law enforcement officers in 2023 — the most recent year for which PPIC has published figures. California maintains approximately 2 officers per 1,000 residents, compared to a national average of approximately 3.5 per 1,000 as reported in PORAC's November 2025 Police Staffing Issue Brief — making California one of the lowest-staffed states in the country on a per-capita basis. The mean officer wage in California in May 2023 was approximately $111,770 — the highest in the nation, roughly 46% above the national mean of $76,550 per the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics.

UC police officer compensation, as published by UCLA PD (effective June 22, 2025), ranges from approximately $100,900 to $170,300 per year in total compensation, including base salary and additional pay components (longevity pay, POST certificate pay, specialty pay, shift differential, education pay, bilingual pay, and allowances). This compensation range is competitive with or exceeds many California municipal agencies, particularly those in lower cost-of-living areas.

California's law enforcement profession has lost more than 3,300 sworn officers and 400 civilian staff since 2020, according to PORAC (Peace Officers Research Association of California). UC police departments, like their municipal counterparts, face the same recruitment and retention challenges documented systemwide.

Key Finding: UC police officers receive compensation comparable to or exceeding many municipal counterparts, are trained to the same POST standards, hold the same peace officer powers, and face the same workforce challenges. The argument that UC police departments should be subject to less rigorous oversight than municipal agencies because of resource constraints or structural differences finds no support in the compensation or training data. Officers receiving $100,000–$170,000 in total compensation from a public employer are subject to the same performance and accountability standards as any municipal officer at any salary level.
Source Note: California staffing and salary data: PPIC, 2024. National salary data: Bureau of Labor Statistics, OES 2023. PORAC study: Police1, Nov. 2025. UCLA compensation: ucpd.ucla.edu.

Chief tenure is a recognized indicator of departmental stability. The Police Executive Research Forum (PERF) documented in its 2021 national survey that police chiefs face significant political and community pressures that can incentivize risk-averse responses, and reported an average chief tenure of approximately 7.3 years among surveyed predecessor chiefs. Frequent turnover disrupts organizational continuity, community relationships, reform implementation, and institutional memory.

UC Davis's five-year interim period (2012–2017) and UCSB's chief resignation amid whistleblower lawsuits (2019) reflect the same pattern. The key variable is whether the chief becomes embroiled in a controversy generating political liability for campus administrators — at which point the chief is removed, but the structural conditions that produced the controversy remain unchanged. This is a documented hallmark of departments that have been placed under consent decrees: leadership turnover as a substitute for structural reform.

V. Accountability and Oversight Structures

California's largest municipal police departments operate under robust civilian oversight structures with investigative authority. The San Francisco Department of Police Accountability (DPA) has subpoena power. The Los Angeles Board of Police Commissioners exercises direct authority over the LAPD Chief. The Oakland Police Commission has the power to terminate the chief. These structures reflect decades of evolution, often catalyzed by specific incidents of misconduct — including, in LAPD's case, the Rampart scandal and the resulting federal consent decree.

UC's Police Accountability Boards (PABs), established campus-by-campus between 2021 and 2023 under the UC Community Safety Plan, are advisory bodies. They review independent investigation reports and submit recommendations to the chief of police and chancellor. They do not have subpoena power. They cannot compel discipline. Their recommendations can be — and have been — overridden by the chief or campus administration.

The 2023 External Comprehensive Assessment of Safety (ECAS) systemwide audit identified five key findings relevant to oversight: (1) no systemwide use-of-force policy existed; (2) complaint handling was inconsistent across campuses; (3) dashboard deficiencies impaired transparency; (4) SB 16 compliance gaps persisted; and (5) no systemwide policy existed for investigating a chief of police — meaning that when a chief is the subject of a complaint, there is no standardized mechanism for independent investigation.

The Bustamante Connection

The 2019 Report of the Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing was chaired by Alexander Bustamante, UC Senior Vice President and Chief Compliance and Audit Officer. The task force was appointed by UC President Janet Napolitano and convened from April through December 2018, meeting five times in person with additional subcommittee sessions. Its report recommended transparency improvements, protest response reforms, and enhanced accountability mechanisms — recommendations that became the basis for the UC Community Safety Plan.

The same Alexander Bustamante received direct correspondence in November 2021 from a former UCLA PD officer — the founding president of FUPOA — documenting a decades-long pattern of institutional misconduct, whistleblower retaliation, and records discrepancies at UCLA PD. This correspondence was sent to Bustamante in his capacity as UC's Chief Compliance and Audit Officer — the same capacity in which he had chaired the task force that recommended policing reforms. The documented record does not reflect any response, investigation, or corrective action resulting from this correspondence.

Critical Finding: The individual who chaired UC's own Presidential Task Force on policing reform — and who held the title of Chief Compliance and Audit Officer for the entire UC system — received documented evidence of the exact categories of institutional misconduct the task force was created to address. The documented record does not reflect any corrective action. This fact is presented without characterization. The reader may assess whether UC's internal reform apparatus functions as a mechanism for institutional change or as an institutional response to public pressure that does not alter underlying conduct.

VI. Reporting Compliance and Transparency

California law imposes uniform reporting requirements on all law enforcement agencies regardless of governance model. AB 48 (less-lethal weapons reporting), AB 953/RIPA (racial profiling data), SB 1421/SB 16 (personnel records disclosure), and Clery Act requirements (campus-specific) apply to UC police with the same force as they apply to any municipal department.

A September 2025 LAist investigation found that nine of ten UC campus police departments failed to comply with AB 48 reporting requirements regarding the use of chemical agents and less-lethal projectiles during protest responses. Only UCLA appeared on the California Department of Justice's statewide AB 48 reporting page. At UC Riverside, a department lieutenant publicly acknowledged that the department's size prevented compliance, stating: "A lot of times things are just going to have to get set aside."

In 2020, when Voice of San Diego requested use-of-force records from UC San Diego — records deemed public under a law California passed the previous year (SB 1421) — the university produced only two case files, omitting records of multiple alleged assaults by officers that had been documented in media reports and internal university reviews. UC stated there was "no UC-wide policy guiding the release of SB 1421 records" and refused to comment on why it maintained a list of hundreds of use-of-force incidents but disclosed only two.

UC Berkeley paid a $2.35 million Clery Act settlement fine in 2020 — the third-largest in Clery Act history at the time of payment, now the fourth-largest following Liberty University's $14 million fine in March 2024 — after a six-year Department of Education review (2014–2020) that found 1,125 misclassified crimes and systemic compliance failures across five years of records. In 2025, the Department of Education initiated a new Clery Act focused review of UC Berkeley following the November 10, 2025 TPUSA protest incident.

Key Finding: Nine of ten UC police departments failed to comply with AB 48. One campus publicly acknowledged inability to comply with state law. One campus produced two use-of-force records while maintaining a list of hundreds. One campus paid what was at the time the third-largest Clery Act fine in federal history (now fourth-largest) for misclassifying 1,125 crimes. This level of documented non-compliance across an entire system of law enforcement agencies — all holding identical peace officer powers to municipal officers — would be unusual in the municipal context, where city councils, city attorneys, and public advocacy organizations provide closer external scrutiny. A municipal department that publicly acknowledged inability to comply with state reporting law would face potential consequences from elected officials. The UC governance structure, with its constitutional autonomy, may create a different incentive structure for compliance.
Source Note: AB 48 compliance: LAist, Sept. 2025. UCSD records disclosure: CalMatters, June 2020; Voice of San Diego, May 2020. UC Berkeley Clery Act: Higher Ed Dive; Department of Education enforcement records.

VIII. The Federal Consent Decree Framework: 34 U.S.C. § 12601

In 1994, Congress passed the Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act, which contained a provision originally codified as 42 U.S.C. § 14141 and now found at 34 U.S.C. § 12601. The provision was a direct response to the 1991 Rodney King beating and the finding by the Christopher Commission that the beating was not an isolated incident but part of a broader pattern of LAPD misconduct. Section 12601 provides:

34 U.S.C. § 12601(a): "It shall be unlawful for any governmental authority, or any agent thereof, or any person acting on behalf of a governmental authority, to engage in a pattern or practice of conduct by law enforcement officers . . . that deprives persons of rights, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the Constitution or laws of the United States."
34 U.S.C. § 12601(b): "Whenever the Attorney General has reasonable cause to believe that a violation of paragraph (1) has occurred, the Attorney General, for or in the name of the United States, may in a civil action obtain appropriate equitable and declaratory relief to eliminate the pattern or practice."

The statute applies to "any governmental authority." UC police departments are governmental law enforcement agencies created by the constitutionally established University of California Board of Regents, exercising peace officer powers under state law. Section 12601 is not limited to municipal agencies.

The Scale of Federal Intervention Since 1994

Since the statute's enactment, the Department of Justice has opened a total of 71 formal pattern-or-practice investigations, with 40 cases ending in negotiated agreements — approximately half as consent decrees and half as memoranda of agreement. The DOJ has historically initiated approximately three investigations per year, with one such investigation typically leading to significant structural reform through a detailed consent decree with independent monitoring.

Consent decrees have been entered against agencies of widely varying sizes, from the Puerto Rico Police Department (~17,000 officers) to the Steubenville, Ohio Police Department (~30 officers). The size of an agency is not a limiting factor. What triggers intervention is the documented pattern of conduct — not the number of officers involved.

Departments placed under consent decrees or memoranda of agreement include: Los Angeles, CA (2001); Pittsburgh, PA (1997); Steubenville, OH (1997); New Jersey State Police (1999); Cincinnati, OH (2002); Detroit, MI (2003); Oakland, CA (2003); Cleveland, OH (2015); Baltimore, MD (2017); Chicago, IL (2019); Ferguson, MO (2016); New Orleans, LA (2012); Seattle, WA (2012); Albuquerque, NM (2014); Newark, NJ (2016); and Louisville, KY (2023), among others.

IX. The LAPD Consent Decree: A Side-by-Side Comparison

The LAPD consent decree remains the most significant federal intervention into a California law enforcement agency. The University of California police system operates within the same state, under the same POST certification requirements, with officers holding identical peace officer powers. The following comparison examines the documented conduct that triggered the LAPD consent decree alongside the documented conduct of the UC police system compiled in Parts 1–9 of this series.

The LAPD Consent Decree: What Triggered It

On November 3, 2000, the United States filed a complaint in U.S. District Court for the Central District of California (Case No. 2:00-cv-11769) under 42 U.S.C. § 14141, alleging that the City of Los Angeles, its Board of Police Commissioners, and its Police Department engaged in a pattern or practice of unconstitutional conduct including: the unconstitutional use of force, including improper officer-involved shootings; improper seizures of persons, including stops not based on reasonable suspicion and arrests without probable cause; and improper searches of persons and property with insufficient cause.

The consent decree was entered into law on June 15, 2001, by U.S. District Court Judge Gary Feess. It was instituted for a minimum of five years and required 191 specific reforms. The decree was extended beyond its original term when Judge Feess concluded the city was not in substantial compliance with material provisions. The consent decree itself was superseded on July 17, 2009, under Chief William Bratton; federal oversight continued through a Transition Agreement until May 2013 — approximately twelve years from the filing of the complaint.

The LAPD consent decree arose from the Rampart Division corruption scandal of the late 1990s, in which officers in the anti-gang CRASH unit admitted or were implicated in: planting evidence, manufacturing evidence, beating suspects, framing suspects, lying in court (perjury), and covering up misconduct. Officer Rafael Perez, who cut a plea deal, implicated more than 70 officers. The City of Los Angeles's own reports identified 171 tainted convictions (156 felony and 15 misdemeanor) arising from the scandal. The city paid approximately $70 million in Rampart-related lawsuit settlements per contemporaneous Los Angeles Times reporting, after earlier estimates of potential exposure reached $125 million.

Critical Finding: In every category of conduct that triggered the LAPD consent decree — excessive force, racial profiling, evidence falsification, whistleblower retaliation, internal review failures, reporting non-compliance, and leadership instability — the documented UC police system record contains comparable or parallel conduct, spanning a longer duration (37 years vs. approximately 10–15 years), across a greater number of individual departments (ten campuses vs. one department), with higher per-officer settlement exposure ($34,932 vs. $13,889). The UC system has also been the subject of its own academic institution's documentation of institutional misconduct (UCLA Luskin Center, March 2022), three separate internal reform efforts (2019 Presidential Task Force, 2021 Community Safety Plan, 2023 ECAS audit), and documented non-compliance with state reporting law across nine of ten departments. No federal consent decree or pattern-or-practice investigation has ever been initiated. This comparison is presented for the reader's independent assessment.

X. When Departments Cannot Self-Govern: Disbandment Precedents

When a police department demonstrates a sustained inability to provide constitutionally adequate policing, several outcomes have occurred in California and nationally. These range from federal consent decrees to outright disbandment and absorption by a larger agency. The following California precedents are documented for comparative purposes.

Compton Police Department — Disbanded September 2000

The Compton Police Department was the municipal law enforcement agency for the city of Compton, California, from 1888 until it was disbanded by the Compton City Council in September 2000. The city council then contracted with the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department for law enforcement services under a $12.3 million contract — the most expensive of the 41 cities the Sheriff's Department served at that time.

The disbandment followed a spate of eight murders in ten days in July 2000. City spokesperson Frank Wheaton stated: "The crime rate has surpassed the Compton Police Department's ability to control." The department had approximately 113 officers at the time of dissolution. Underlying factors included a troubled legacy of racial tensions, public conflict between the department and the city council, questionable officer conduct, and chronic financial difficulties. The 1974 Grand Jury had recommended merging with the Sheriff's Department. In 1978, the City Manager noted the city could save $1 million against a $9 million department budget. The department's chief stated in 1985: "Every two years we go through this sheriff's thing."

Most of the 113 officers received conditional offers to join the Sheriff's Department. Chief Allen retired after 31 years of service, refusing an offer to remain. A 2004 advisory vote on reestablishing the department was defeated 67% to 32%. The city has never reestablished an independent police department.

Maywood Police Department — Disbanded 2010

In 2010, the City of Maywood disbanded virtually all municipal departments and services, including the Maywood Police Department, after the California Joint Powers Insurance Authority (JPIA) cancelled the city's workers' compensation and commercial insurance coverage due to a poor claims history from 2005 to 2010. The department's misconduct and claims record made the city uninsurable. Law enforcement services were contracted to the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department.

MTA Police Department — Disbanded 1997

The Metropolitan Transportation Authority Police Department, responsible for police services for much of the bus and rail public transit in Los Angeles County, was disbanded in 1997. Of the more than 300 officers, 60% were absorbed by LAPD and the remainder by the Sheriff's Department.

The Standard for Disbandment

The documented California precedents establish that police departments have been disbanded when they demonstrate one or more of the following conditions: inability to control crime within their jurisdiction; chronic financial inability to sustain operations; misconduct records that render the agency uninsurable; or governance failures that prevent effective civilian control of the police function. In each case, the governing body — a city council composed of elected officials accountable to voters — made the decision to dissolve the department and contract with a larger agency.

Key Finding: The Compton Police Department was disbanded after a documented pattern of conduct that — while serious — spanned a shorter duration and involved fewer constitutional violation categories than the documented UC police system record compiled in this series. The critical difference: Compton's city council was composed of elected officials who could be voted out of office for failing to act. UC's Board of Regents is composed of appointed officials with constitutional autonomy. The structural incentive to act decisively when a police department fails is fundamentally different. No UC campus police department has ever been disbanded, despite documented patterns of misconduct spanning decades. Whether this reflects effective self-governance or structural insulation from accountability is a question for the reader.

XI. The Structural Immunity Question: Article IX, Section 9

Article IX, Section 9 of the California Constitution provides that the University of California "shall constitute a public trust, to be administered by the existing corporation known as 'The Regents of the University of California,' with full powers of organization and government." This provision has been interpreted by the California Supreme Court to grant UC a degree of constitutional autonomy that limits the Legislature's ability to impose governance requirements on the university.

This constitutional autonomy does not exempt UC police officers from state law. POST certification requirements under Penal Code § 13510.1 apply. SB 2 decertification authority applies. AB 48 reporting requirements apply. SB 1421/SB 16 personnel records disclosure requirements apply. The Clery Act applies. 34 U.S.C. § 12601 applies — it covers "any governmental authority."

However, the constitutional autonomy does affect the day-to-day governance structure within which UC police departments operate. It affects who hires and fires chiefs (appointed administrators, not elected officials). It affects who sets police budgets (Regents and chancellors, not city councils). It affects the structure and authority of oversight bodies (advisory PABs, not commissions with subpoena power). And it affects the political calculus of intervention — a state legislator who attempts to impose governance requirements on UC police faces constitutional autonomy arguments that would not apply to any municipal department.

The practical effect is a police system that is legally equivalent to any municipal department in its peace officer powers and POST obligations, but structurally insulated from the accountability mechanisms that apply to municipal departments of comparable size. This structural insulation has been documented across every dimension examined in this series: governance, oversight, reporting compliance, leadership accountability, and federal investigation.

Critical Finding: The Regents' constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 creates a governance framework in which UC police departments enjoy the full powers of municipal law enforcement — including statewide arrest authority, firearms, less-lethal weapons, and the authority to use force — while operating within an accountability structure that is less directly connected to the communities they serve, less transparent in its operations, and less exposed to external intervention than any comparably-sized municipal agency in California. This is not a theoretical concern. It is documented: nine of ten UC departments failed to comply with state reporting law as of September 2025, and no federal pattern-or-practice investigation has ever been opened, despite a 37-year documented record that parallels or exceeds the conduct that triggered consent decrees in municipal departments. Whether this structural arrangement adequately protects the constitutional rights of the students, faculty, staff, and communities served by UC police is a question that this report presents to POST and to the Department of Justice.

XII. UC's Own Reform Apparatus: Three Attempts, Same Results

The University of California has undertaken three major internal reform efforts targeting its police system in the seven years preceding this report. Each is documented below, along with the documented outcomes.

Attempt 1: The Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing (2019)

In 2018, UC President Janet Napolitano appointed a Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing, chaired by Alexander Bustamante, UC Senior Vice President and Chief Compliance and Audit Officer. The task force convened from April through December 2018, meeting five times in person with additional virtual subcommittee sessions and systemwide outreach. The task force engaged recognized third-party experts in police complaint processes, use of force, implicit bias, police review boards, and community engagement.

The task force's 2019 report examined investigative practices, use-of-force policies, and training. It recommended transparency improvements, enhanced complaint processes, protest response reforms, and accountability mechanisms. The task force reported that complaint numbers were "very low" and that satisfaction surveys showed 93–96% satisfaction rates. The task force also acknowledged requests to disband or disarm UCPD but rejected these, citing campus safety needs.

The task force recommended that each campus police department be "treated as a separate agency" for DOJ reporting purposes and stated that campuses would "meet all DOJ reporting standards by the 2023 deadline." As documented in Section VI above, nine of ten UC departments failed to comply with AB 48 as of September 2025.

Attempt 2: The UC Community Safety Plan (2021)

Published in August 2021 as a "living document," the UC Community Safety Plan established four guiding principles: community-driven safety, holistic tiered response, transparency through data, and independent oversight. The plan set 31 specific actions with milestone dates through December 31, 2023, including: establishing Police Accountability Boards at all campuses; launching systemwide data dashboards; implementing tiered response models; and achieving third-party accreditation of all campus police departments by December 31, 2023.

A November 2022 progress report to the Board of Regents (Regents Item B3) noted that "every UC campus has made progress" but acknowledged challenges including budget constraints, staffing shortages, and the complexity of implementing cultural change. As of February 2026, only two of ten UC police departments (UC Davis and UC Irvine) have achieved IACLEA accreditation — the plan's December 2023 accreditation deadline has passed for the remaining eight departments.

Attempt 3: The ECAS Audit (2023)

The External Comprehensive Assessment of Safety (ECAS) systemwide audit, completed in 2023, found: no systemwide use-of-force policy; inconsistent complaint handling; dashboard deficiencies; SB 16 compliance gaps; and no standardized mechanism for investigating a chief of police. These findings — issued four years after the Presidential Task Force and two years after the Community Safety Plan — documented that the same structural deficiencies the prior reform efforts were designed to address remained substantially unresolved.

XIII. The Structural Accountability Gap

The comparative analysis reveals what can be described as a structural accountability gap — a space between the legal obligations UC police share with all California law enforcement and the institutional mechanisms available to enforce those obligations:

1. No systemwide chief investigation policy. The 2023 ECAS audit found no standardized mechanism for investigating complaints against a chief of police. In municipal agencies, the city manager, city council, or an inspector general fills this role. In the UC system, the chancellor is responsible — but the chancellor is also the chief's employer, creating an inherent conflict of interest.

2. Advisory-only oversight boards. UC PABs lack subpoena power, cannot compel discipline, and submit recommendations that can be overridden. California's strongest municipal oversight bodies (San Francisco DPA, Oakland Police Commission, LA Board of Police Commissioners) have substantially greater authority.

3. Constitutional autonomy as insulation. Article IX, Section 9 grants UC autonomy that limits the Legislature's ability to impose governance requirements. While POST certification and SB 2 decertification apply, the day-to-day governance operates within a framework no municipal agency enjoys.

4. Documented reporting non-compliance. Nine of ten departments failed AB 48; one acknowledged inability to comply; one produced 2 of hundreds of use-of-force records; one paid what was at the time the third-largest Clery Act fine in federal history (now fourth-largest following Liberty University's $14M fine in March 2024).

5. Internal vs. external review divergence. UC Davis: internal review exonerated, external review condemned. UCLA 2024: internal process blamed the chief, independent investigation found administrators excluded police from decisions. The pattern recurs across campuses and decades.

6. Zero federal pattern-or-practice investigations. Despite a 37-year documented record paralleling or exceeding LAPD pre-consent-decree conduct in every major category, no formal § 12601 investigation has ever been opened against any UC police department. The structural insulation documented in this report may explain this gap.

7. POST has never audited a UC police department. POST has unrestricted audit authority under Penal Code § 13503(f) as amended by SB 2 (2021). POST has never decertified a UC police officer. The 2023 POSAD Annual Report references audit authority but reports no completed audits of UC departments.

XIV. Summary of Findings — Dual-Track Recommendations

The following findings are presented for independent assessment by both the California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training and any federal authority with jurisdiction under 34 U.S.C. § 12601.

Track 1: Findings for POST

POST Finding 1 — Audit Authority Unexercised: POST has unrestricted authority under PC § 13503(f) to audit any UC police department without cause and at any time. POST has never exercised this authority against any of the ten UC police departments. The documented record compiled in this series — including $10.2 million in verified settlements, nine departments in AB 48 non-compliance, a $2.35 million Clery Act fine, 1,125 misclassified crimes, and a 37-year pattern of documented institutional misconduct at UCLA — provides a factual basis for POST to exercise its audit authority.
POST Finding 2 — Decertification Authority Unexercised: POST has never decertified a UC police officer under SB 2. The documented record includes evidence of conduct falling within the nine categories of serious misconduct codified at PC § 13510.8(b)(1)–(9), including dishonesty (false reports contradicted by property logs), abuse of power (denial of rights based on shifting classifications), and failure to cooperate with misconduct investigations. POST's Peace Officer Standards Accountability Division (POSAD) has jurisdiction to review all documented allegations.
POST Finding 3 — Reporting Non-Compliance Is Systemic: Nine of ten UC police departments failed to comply with AB 48 reporting requirements as of September 2025. This is not an individual agency failure — it is a systemwide pattern that POST, as the regulatory body for all California peace officers, has authority and responsibility to address.
POST Finding 4 — The Reform Apparatus Has Failed: Three separate internal reform efforts (2019, 2021, 2023) produced documented commitments to change that have not been met. The same structural deficiencies identified in 2019 remained unresolved in 2023. The most significant police controversy in UC history occurred in 2024 — after all three reform efforts. External oversight is the remaining mechanism.

Track 2: Findings for Federal Authorities (34 U.S.C. § 12601)

Federal Finding 1 — Pattern or Practice Threshold: The documented record compiled across ten parts of this series contains evidence in every category of conduct that has triggered § 12601 consent decrees in municipal departments: excessive force (six campuses), racial profiling (UCLA, UCSD), evidence falsification (UCLA), whistleblower retaliation (UCLA, UCSB, UCSC), internal review failures (UC Davis, UCLA, systemwide), and reporting non-compliance (nine campuses). The duration of the documented pattern (37 years) exceeds the duration documented in any municipal consent decree case. The per-officer settlement exposure ($34,932) exceeds LAPD's pre-consent-decree per-officer exposure ($13,889) by a factor of 2.5.
Federal Finding 2 — No Investigation Has Ever Been Opened: Despite 71 formal § 12601 investigations of municipal and state law enforcement agencies since 1994, no formal investigation of any UC police department has been identified in the public record. The statute covers "any governmental authority." UC police departments are governmental law enforcement agencies. The absence of investigation is not explained by the absence of documented conduct — it may be explained by the structural factors documented in this report, including constitutional autonomy, appointed governance, and the political visibility of the UC Regents.
Federal Finding 3 — Internal Reform Has Not Succeeded: UC has undertaken three internal reform efforts (2019, 2021, 2023). The DOJ has specifically noted that failed internal reform efforts strengthen the case for external intervention under § 12601. The documented record — in which reform announcements are made and reported to the Regents, but documented conduct does not change — meets this criterion.
Federal Finding 4 — Structural Insulation May Require External Intervention: The Regents' constitutional autonomy under Article IX, Section 9 creates a governance framework that limits the effectiveness of state legislative oversight, limits civilian oversight to advisory functions, and removes elected officials from the accountability chain. The federal consent decree mechanism under § 12601 was designed precisely for situations in which local governance structures are unable or unwilling to correct patterns of unconstitutional policing. The documented UC police system record presents such a situation for federal assessment.

These findings are submitted to the California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training and to any federal authority with jurisdiction under 34 U.S.C. § 12601 for consideration.
