A Note on This Chapter

This chapter covers one question: who can hold the UC Regents accountable for how they run their campus police departments — and what has happened every time someone tried?

The answer rests on a principle older than the law books: no one should be judge in their own case. This chapter walks through every serious attempt to hold the Regents accountable, door by door, and shows you what happened at each one. The officers and POST staff in these pages are witnesses and casualties of the problem — not the people who built it.

Two people are named by name in this chapter because the documented record requires it. Michael Shain is named because the record documents that he falsified internal affairs reports at UCLA PD, and that the UC governance structure is what allowed that falsification to go unaddressed. Alexander Bustamante is named not as a villain, but as a demonstration: the UC Senior Vice President who chaired UC’s 2019 police reform task force also received — and did not act on — documented evidence of the exact misconduct the reform was supposed to fix. Every other individual in this chapter — commissioners, officers, administrators — appears as a witness to a structure they did not build.

The Preface of this report introduced the current UCLA Chief of Police as a named respondent in the POST complaint — and as the clearest evidence that qualifications are not the variable. He was trained inside the one municipal department that proved federal police accountability works. He runs a department where none of that training applies. He cannot say so out loud. This chapter says it for him. Three doors. Four years. The same wall at the end of each one.

I. The Wall of Silence and the Three Doors

The University of California Regents — the governing board that runs all ten UC campuses — operate under Article IX §9 of the California Constitution — the 1879 sentence that built the wall. It made UC answerable to no one except itself. It put the Regents beyond the reach of the Legislature, beyond state labor law, and beyond any external police oversight body. One sentence in the Constitution did all of that. It is still in force today.

There are only three DOORS anyone can open to reach and reform the Regents:

II. A Partnership Group Handed an Impossible Job

Before the three doors, there is POST — the California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training. POST is the body this report is submitted to. And POST is the most visible single illustration of the structural problem this chapter documents.

If every seat on the POST Commission were filled by a Regent tomorrow, the accountability gap would not close. It would deepen. The problem is not who sits in the chair. The problem is that no chair in California — filled by anyone — has the constitutional authority to compel the Regents. Structure is the issue. Not people.

POST was built to be a partner to law enforcement, not a watchdog over it. POST certifies officers and writes training standards. That is the job. Disciplining a constitutional corporation that outranks the Legislature is not the job — and no one in Sacramento ever gave POST the authority to make it the job.

Here is what the POST Commission actually looks like. Eighteen members. Ten of them are current or former sworn law enforcement. An eleventh is listed as an “educator” but served as Chief of the Sacramento Police Department for thirty-three years. Two are sitting union presidents — representing the largest state police union and one of the most politically active sworn-officer organizations in California.

This is not a commission designed to go to war with the profession. It was never meant to be. POST describes itself as a leader in “innovative partnerships” with law enforcement. The commission’s composition is exactly what a partner institution produces — people who understand and respect the profession they help oversee. That is not the failure. The failure is asking a partnership body to hold accountable an institution whose constitutional position sits above every law POST was given to enforce.

The Legislature understood this when it built POST’s companion body, the Peace Officer Standards Accountability Advisory Board: seven public members, only two current or former peace officers. The Legislature flipped the ratio on purpose. The Commission was built to serve the profession. The Advisory Board was built to discipline it.

The Core Problem: Even if the Advisory Board were made up entirely of civilians, it still only has the power of a California statute. The Regents hold their authority directly from the California Constitution — the 1879 provision that put them above the reach of state statutes. A statutory body, no matter who sits on it, cannot compel a constitutional entity. The composition problem is real but it is the symptom. The constitutional gap is the disease.

POST can reach UC officers personally — it certifies them, and SB 2 gave it authority to end their careers. What POST cannot do is reach into the institution that employs those officers and compel it to change how it governs itself. That is the same wall this entire chapter documents. The next three sections show what happened when everyone else hit it too.

III. Door 1: Let the Regents Fix It Themselves. Three Times. Still Broken.

The Regents could fix this tomorrow. No legislation, no constitutional amendment, no federal action required. They hold “full powers of organization and government” over the UC system. The entire documented record — from 1989 through 2026 — shows that when an institution is the judge of its own performance, structure governs the outcome. Not intention. Structure.

Three reform efforts. Four years. The same failures at the end as at the beginning.

Attempt 1 — The Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing (2019)

In 2018, UC President Janet Napolitano appointed a Presidential Task Force on Universitywide Policing, chaired by Alexander Bustamante, UC’s Senior Vice President and Chief Compliance and Audit Officer. The task force brought in outside experts, held five in-person sessions, and produced a 2019 report recommending transparency improvements, better complaint processes, and protest response reforms. It promised campuses would “meet all DOJ reporting standards by the 2023 deadline.”

What actually happened: nine of ten UC campus police departments failed to comply with AB 48 reporting requirements as of September 2025. AB 48 requires police departments to report their use of chemical agents and projectiles during protests — exactly the conduct the task force’s protest recommendations addressed. The 2023 deadline came and went.

The Bustamante Connection: The same Alexander Bustamante who chaired the reform task force — in his capacity as UC’s Chief Compliance and Audit Officer — received a letter in November 2021 from Officer 341. That letter documented a decades-long pattern of misconduct, whistleblower retaliation, and records problems at UCLA PD. The record shows no response, no investigation, no corrective action.

The same man who ran the reform received a letter documenting the exact misconduct the reform was supposed to address. The record shows no response. That is the principle in practice. The institution reviewing its own failures is the institution with the greatest reason to find them manageable.

Attempt 2 — The UC Community Safety Plan (2021)

Published in August 2021, the UC Community Safety Plan set thirty-one specific actions with deadlines through December 31, 2023: Police Accountability Boards at every campus, systemwide data dashboards, and IACLEA accreditation — the national law enforcement credential — for all ten departments by year-end 2023.

As of February 2026, only two of ten UC police departments — UC Davis and UC Irvine — had achieved IACLEA accreditation. Eight missed the deadline the Regents themselves set.

The Police Accountability Boards that came out of this plan are advisory only. No subpoena power. No authority to compel discipline. The chief can ignore their recommendations. These are the “independent oversight” bodies the plan promised — bodies that report to the very chiefs they are supposed to oversee.

Attempt 3 — The ECAS Audit (2023)

ECAS stands for Ethics, Compliance and Audit Services — the internal UC office that conducts systemwide audits and reports to the Board of Regents. In February 2023, ECAS issued the Systemwide Police Personnel Complaints Process and Use of Force Reporting Audit, which reviewed police complaint and use-of-force practices at all ten UC campuses. The audit found five major problems: (1) no systemwide use-of-force policy existed; (2) complaint handling was inconsistent across campuses; (3) data dashboards had deficiencies; (4) gaps in compliance with SB 16 persisted; and (5) no UC campus had a standardized process for investigating a Chief of Police.

That fifth finding is worth stopping on. No campus had a policy preventing its own police department from investigating its own chief. The audit recommended one be created — in the audit’s own words, the systemwide policy must “prohibit departments from investigating complaints against their own chief.” The fact that no such policy existed — four years after the Presidential Task Force and two years after the Community Safety Plan — is not a coincidence. When no outside authority can require the policy, the policy doesn’t get written.

The Regents cannot be held accountable by their own reform process. Not because they are dishonest — because you cannot be judge in your own case. That is not a personal failing. That is the principle.

The officers who lived through all three reform cycles had every reason to expect change. In 2019 the task force convened. In 2021 there were thirty-one actions with specific deadlines. In 2023 an outside audit confirmed the same problems in writing. At the end of all three cycles, the officer who wanted to report misconduct by his chief still had no standardized path for doing it. The Police Accountability Board could take his complaint and make a recommendation. The chief could ignore it. Door 1 knocked three times. Three times the wall held.

IV. Door 2: Change the Constitution. The People Never Got the Chance to Vote.

The Regents’ exemptions are not in a state regulation. They are not in a statute the Legislature can repeal with a simple majority vote. They are written into the California Constitution — the 1879 provision that made UC answerable to no one except itself. The only way to override them is through a constitutional amendment. That means the People of California have to vote.

The process requires two steps. First, two-thirds of the Legislature must agree to put the amendment on the ballot. Second, a majority of California voters must ratify it. Either step can kill it. Door 2 has been tried once.

What ACA 6 Was

Assembly Constitutional Amendment 6 — ACA 6 — was a state lawmaker’s attempt to change the Constitution to force UC to follow labor law. It died in committee. Assemblymember Matt Haney introduced it in the 2023–2024 legislative session. He drafted it as a constitutional amendment — not a regular bill — specifically because he knew a regular bill couldn’t reach the Regents.

ACA 6 would have required the UC Regents to follow the same labor standards as every other California public employer: minimum wage, overtime, meal breaks, paid sick leave, whistleblower protections. The same floor every other employer in the state has to meet.

The Senate Committee on Elections and Constitutional Amendments analyzed the bill and documented the scale of what the Regents’ exemption actually means. As of 2022, the UC system employed 73,012 academic employees and 143,188 non-academic employees — 216,200 workers — who are “potentially exempt from various state labor laws due to the UC’s constitutional exemption.” Two hundred and sixteen thousand two hundred workers at one institution in California, working under labor standards set by that institution — not by the People’s elected representatives.

The California Professional Firefighters wrote in support. Their letter said UC is “the only employer in the state” exempt from minimum wage, overtime, equal pay, sick leave, and more. That is not an opinion. It is a precise legal description. The exemptions that protect the Regents apply to every one of their 216,200 workers — including the police officers on every campus.

What Happened

ACA 6 failed in the Senate Committee on Elections and Constitutional Amendments. The bill never made it past a handful of state senators. It never reached the Senate floor. It never reached the voters. A small committee killed it.

The People of California have never been asked whether one institution should operate beyond the labor standards that every other employer in the state must meet. They never got the chance.

What “Failed in Committee” Means: Passing a constitutional amendment requires two steps. Step One — two-thirds of the Legislature puts the amendment on the ballot. Step Two — a majority of voters ratify it. ACA 6 never completed Step One. It did not fail on a floor vote. It died before the full Legislature ever voted. A committee of a handful of senators ended it. The voters never saw it.

For the officers working inside the UC system, ACA 6’s failure is not an abstraction. UC police officers work for the only employer in California constitutionally exempt from the labor standards ACA 6 tried to impose. They cannot bring the same whistleblower protections that every municipal officer’s employer must honor. The overtime standards at their department are set by the Regents, not by the state. ACA 6 would have helped the officers. It died in committee.

The committee that voted to stop ACA 6 was not acting in bad faith. It was responding to the same political environment that has allowed the Regents’ constitutional autonomy to survive every challenge since 1879. The Regents govern a system with a $51 billion annual budget, 216,200 employees, and alumni and donor networks that reach into every legislative district in California. They have survived every attempt to limit their constitutional power for 147 years. Door 2 required unusual political will and perfect alignment. It did not get either.

V. Door 3: Call the Feds. For 77 Years, Nobody Has.

The U.S. Constitution sits above the California Constitution. Federal law can reach the Regents even when state law can’t. Three federal mechanisms exist to hold UC accountable for how its police departments operate. None of the three has ever been used to open a sustained accountability action against any UC campus.

34 U.S.C. § 12601 — Pattern-or-Practice Investigations

34 U.S.C. § 12601 is a federal law that lets the U.S. Justice Department sue police departments that keep violating people’s rights. When the DOJ acts under this law, it can impose a federal consent decree with an independent monitor. The LAPD consent decree ran from 2001 to 2013 — twelve years of outside oversight before a federal court found the department had truly changed. The law exists specifically because Congress recognized that some departments cannot credibly investigate their own systemic failures. When the institution is the problem, the institution cannot also be the solution. That is the same principle — in federal statutory form.

Seventy-seven years of UC police department operations. Not one investigation under 34 U.S.C. § 12601. Not one.

The Clery Act — Campus Crime Reporting

The Clery Act is the federal law that makes colleges report crime. 20 U.S.C. § 1092(f) gives the federal government enforcement leverage through funding. UC San Diego paid $3.35 million — at the time among the largest Clery Act fines in federal history — for 1,125 misclassified crimes and failure to issue timely warnings. UC Berkeley paid $2.35 million in 2020 for similar violations. The fines were paid. The reporting structures that produced the violations stayed inside the Regents’ control. The Clery Act reached the institution’s wallet. It did not reach its governance.

Title VI — Federal Funding and Discrimination

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 is the federal law that cuts funding for discrimination. 42 U.S.C. § 2000d applies to any institution receiving federal money. UC receives federal money. The mechanism exists. It has not been used to compel structural accountability for UC police conduct.

The POST Non-Compliance Data

California law — Cal. Penal Code § 13510.9 — requires every law enforcement agency to report allegations of serious misconduct to POST within ten days. Not after an investigation. Not after a finding. Within ten days of the allegation.

According to POST’s own 2024 POSAD Annual Report, 174 California law enforcement agencies — 28% of all agencies covered by the law — have never submitted a single misconduct report to POST. Of those 174 agencies: 167 had fewer than 50 sworn officers; 4 had between 50 and 99 sworn officers; 1 had 100 or more. The average time for agencies to provide follow-up documentation after an initial report was 241 days. The law says ten days. The average actual response time is twenty-four times that.

In July 2025, POST’s own Advisory Board specifically identified the UCSF Police Department — a UC campus department — as one of those non-reporting agencies. It was identified. It was documented. No UC-specific enforcement action is recorded as having followed.

Key Finding: Nearly one in three California law enforcement agencies has never reported a single allegation of misconduct to POST — despite a state law requiring reports within ten days. POST’s own data documents this. The problem is not that POST lacks the will to enforce. The problem is that POST is a statutory body trying to enforce obligations on institutions whose constitutional position puts them above statutes. When the body doing the enforcing operates under state law, and the institution being enforced against operates under the state Constitution, the Constitution wins. Every time.

The 77-Year Gap

UC police departments have operated since September 19, 1947 — the date the Board of Regents formally constituted them. In those seventy-seven years, the U.S. Department of Justice has opened more than seventy pattern-or-practice investigations against municipal police departments nationwide. The LAPD (2001). Pittsburgh (1997). Cincinnati (2002). Detroit (2003). Oakland (2003). Cleveland (2015). Baltimore (2017). Chicago (2019). Seattle (2012). Albuquerque (2014). Newark (2016). Louisville (2023). And many others.

Not one of those investigations targeted a UC campus. Not one. The documented record at UC campuses — in the UC’s own reports, audit findings, settlement history, and independent reviews — includes patterns of excessive force, evidence falsification, whistleblower retaliation, internal review failures, and reporting non-compliance across multiple campuses over multiple decades. The federal mechanism to address this has existed since 1994. It has not been opened.

For the officers inside the UC system, Door 3’s failure is not an abstraction. An officer who documents misconduct, exhausts every internal channel, and looks to federal enforcement as a last resort — finds the same wall everyone else found. Federal investigators face the same constitutional wall as a state senator trying to pass a labor law. The wall is the same wall. Opening Door 3 requires the political will of the U.S. Department of Justice. That has never materialized. Every year it doesn’t is one more year of the seventy-seven-year gap.

VI. What the Federal Door Looks Like When It Opens

Door 3 has never been opened on UC. But it has been opened elsewhere. The record shows exactly what happens when a federal pattern-or-practice investigation reaches a department that cannot govern itself — and what happens when a California police department is so compromised it gets shut down entirely. Both outcomes are on the books. Both are a matter of public record. Neither has been applied to UC.

What Happens When the Feds Do Step In — The LAPD Example

A common question: does Door 3 actually fix anything? The one time it was used against the largest police department on the West Coast, it did. On November 3, 2000, the United States filed a complaint against the City of Los Angeles, its Board of Police Commissioners, and the Los Angeles Police Department in the U.S. District Court for the Central District of California (Case No. 2:00-cv-11769), alleging a pattern or practice of unconstitutional use of force, improper seizures, and improper searches. The complaint was brought under 42 U.S.C. § 14141 — the same federal statute, recodified in 2017 as 34 U.S.C. § 12601, that Door 3 is built on.

On June 15, 2001, U.S. District Court Judge Gary Feess entered the consent decree. It imposed 191 specific reforms, with a minimum term of five years. Judge Feess extended the decree when he found LAPD was not in substantial compliance with material provisions. The consent decree was superseded on July 17, 2009 under Chief William Bratton; federal oversight continued through a Transition Agreement until May 2013 — approximately twelve years of federal supervision.

The decree arose from the Rampart Division corruption scandal of the late 1990s, in which officers in the anti-gang CRASH unit admitted or were implicated in planting evidence, manufacturing evidence, beating suspects, framing suspects, perjury, and covering up misconduct. Officer Rafael Perez’s plea deal implicated more than 70 officers. The City of Los Angeles’s own reports identified 171 tainted convictions — 156 felony and 15 misdemeanor — arising from the scandal. The city paid approximately $70 million in Rampart-related lawsuit settlements per contemporaneous Los Angeles Times reporting.

What the LAPD example proves: When the federal government opens a § 12601 pattern-or-practice investigation, structural reform happens. 191 court-ordered reforms. Twelve years of federal oversight. The most significant federal intervention in California law enforcement history. LAPD was not disbanded. LAPD was not sued into bankruptcy. LAPD was restructured under court supervision and is still operating. The tool works when it is used.

When Departments Cannot Self-Govern — California Disbandment Precedents

A consent decree is not the only outcome. California has three documented precedents for what happens when a police department demonstrates a sustained inability to provide constitutionally adequate policing. These are not hypotheticals. They are the record.

Compton Police Department — Disbanded September 2000. The Compton Police Department policed the City of Compton from 1888 until its dissolution. Following a spate of eight murders in a ten-day period beginning June 28, 2000, the Compton City Council voted on July 11, 2000 to dissolve the department, with operations ceasing in September 2000. City spokesperson Frank Wheaton stated: “The crime rate has surpassed the Compton Police Department’s ability to control.” The department had approximately 113 officers at dissolution. The city then contracted with the Los Angeles County Sheriff’s Department under a $12.3 million contract — at the time, the most expensive of the 41 cities the Sheriff’s Department served. A 1974 Grand Jury had already recommended merging with the Sheriff. A 1978 City Manager memo noted the city could save $1 million against a $9 million department budget. The department’s chief told the Los Angeles Times in 1985: “Every two years we go through this sheriff’s thing.” Chief Allen retired after 31 years and refused an offer to stay. A 2004 advisory vote on reestablishing the department was defeated 67%–32%. Compton has never reestablished an independent police department.

Maywood Police Department — Disbanded 2010. In 2010, the City of Maywood disbanded virtually all municipal departments, including the Maywood Police Department, after the California Joint Powers Insurance Authority cancelled the city’s workers’ compensation and general liability coverage due to a poor claims history from 2005 to 2010. The department’s misconduct and claims record made the city uninsurable. Law enforcement services were contracted to the Los Angeles County Sheriff’s Department.

MTA Police Department — Disbanded 1997. The Metropolitan Transportation Authority Police Department, responsible for police services for much of the bus and rail public transit in Los Angeles County, was disbanded in 1997. Of the more than 300 officers, 60% were absorbed by LAPD and the remainder by the Sheriff’s Department.

The standard for disbandment: California has dissolved police departments when they demonstrate sustained inability to provide constitutionally adequate policing, when their misconduct and claims record makes the jurisdiction uninsurable, or when cost and governance realities make continued operation untenable. UC’s ten campus police departments have never faced any of these consequences. Neither has any federal investigator evaluated whether they should.

VII. What This Does to the Officers Inside

The three-door analysis is about external accountability — who can reach the Regents from outside. This section is about what the same architecture does to the people already inside.

When a city police officer wants to report misconduct by his chief, he has options. He can go to the city manager. He can go to the city council. He can file with the state. He can go to a civilian oversight panel. There are external nodes in the system. The complaint does not have to stay inside the department.

A UC officer has no equivalent path. The chancellor oversees the police chief — but the chancellor is also the chief’s employer. When the chancellor is the one involved in the problem, the complaint has nowhere to go. Above the chancellor is the Regents. Above the Regents is the California Constitution. There is no city council. There is no elected mayor. The circuit is closed.

The 2023 ECAS audit confirmed this in writing. At least one UC campus had no policy preventing its own police department from investigating its own chief. The audit recommended creating one. The reason it didn’t exist wasn’t an oversight. It was the default state of an institution where no outside authority can require it. When the institution decides for itself what rules apply, rules that inconvenience the institution don’t get written.

The Michael Shain case shows what the constitutional wall actually does to an officer’s complaint. Shain was an internal affairs investigator at UCLA PD. The documented record shows he falsified investigative reports. That finding did not come from outside — it came from the department’s own processes. An officer inside UCLA PD reported the falsification through the proper channels. The report was documented. It was preserved.

It did not produce accountability. It produced retaliation against the officer who reported it. And because no external authority had the power to compel UC to act on what its own records already showed, the documentation just sat there. The falsification was on paper. The retaliation was on paper. The constitutional wall was what prevented either paper trail from reaching anyone with the authority to intervene. That is not a failure of process. That is the process working exactly as the structure allows.

The officers and chiefs caught inside this structure are not the beneficiaries of the Regents’ autonomy — they are its casualties. A UC police chief who wants to hold his command staff accountable must navigate a governance structure that answers to no external authority. A UC officer who blows the whistle on misconduct has no city council to appeal to, no elected mayor to petition, no state labor law to invoke. The constitutional wall that shields the Regents from legislative oversight does not protect the officers. It traps them.

That is not a metaphor. The exemptions from minimum wage, overtime, whistleblower protections, and state labor enforcement — those are exemptions the Regents enjoy, not the officers. The officers carry the same badge, the same authority, and the same personal liability as any municipal officer in California. They just happen to work for the one employer in the state constitutionally exempt from the laws that protect workers everywhere else.

Picture a parent whose daughter is at UCLA. Something happened on campus. She wants to know who she calls. She calls the police department. The department investigates itself. She escalates to the chancellor. The chancellor answers to the Regents. She goes to the state — but state labor laws don’t apply to UC. She goes to POST — POST can discipline individual officers but cannot compel the institution to change how it governs. She goes to the Legislature — they can’t override the Constitution with a bill. She goes to the feds — the feds have had the authority for seventy-seven years and never used it.

That is all three doors, in sequence, hitting the same wall.

The officer documented in this report tried every door. He won every grievance he filed. The University’s answer to losing each proceeding was escalation, not compliance. He reported command staff misconduct to the UC President, the Chancellor, the DA, the FBI, the California DOJ, the Bureau of State Audits, the press. The University investigated and terminated the Chief of Police. Then they turned everything they had on the officer who reported it. Reform announcement. Institutional investigation. Retaliation against the reporter. That sequence is not bad luck. It is what this structure produces — automatically, without anyone in particular deciding to be cruel.

Act II of this report documents that officer’s thirty-seven-year case in full — with grievance numbers, complaint dates, every agency he contacted, and every institutional response. Every external channel was used. No external agency took enforcement action against the University of California. This section points to that record. It asks only one question of the reader: Who does a police officer report the Chancellor’s misconduct to?

The answer has been the same since 1947. And it is still the answer today.

VIII. What All This Proves — And What the Rest of This Report Does About It

Three doors. Three failures. One wall.

This Chapter, By the Numbers:
  • 77 years of UC police department operations. Zero federal pattern-or-practice investigations — while 70+ other departments nationwide got them.
  • $34,932 in settlements per sworn UC officer. $7,778 per LAPD officer in the Rampart era. UC’s per-officer settlement exposure is 4.5× higher than the number that triggered federal intervention at LAPD.
  • 216,200 UC workers exempt from the state labor laws every other California employer must follow — including every UC police officer.
  • 174 California law enforcement agencies have never filed a single misconduct report to POST despite a state law requiring reports within ten days. UCSF Police Department is one of them.
  • 9 out of 10 UC police departments failed AB 48 protest-reporting compliance as of September 2025 — after three reform cycles that promised exactly this.

Door 1: The Regents reviewed their own police system three times in four years and reported the results to themselves. Three times they announced reform. Three times the same structural failures persisted. Door 2: The People’s representatives tried to put a constitutional amendment before the voters. A committee killed it before the voters ever saw it. Door 3: The federal government has had the legal authority to investigate UC campus police for seventy-seven years and has never used it. Every mechanism that exists to hold the Regents accountable has been tried, documented, and found to bounce off the same constitutional wall — a wall built in 1879 that has survived every challenge since.

The problem is not the people. It has never been the people. It is the structure. The POST commissioners are good at their jobs. The Regents who approved the Community Safety Plan were not acting in bad faith. The legislators who wrote ACA 6 used the right vehicle. None of that mattered — because when you operate below the constitutional level of the institution you are trying to hold accountable, good intentions are not enough. You need authority. And no one outside the Regents has it.

POST’s own 2024 POSAD Annual Report is the clearest single proof of this: 28% of California law enforcement agencies have never reported a single misconduct allegation, despite a law requiring reports within ten days. The average actual response time is 241 days. And the UCSF Police Department — a UC campus — specifically named as non-compliant. POST did its job. It identified the non-compliance. It documented it. The institutional response was silence.

What has not been tried — what has never been applied to the University of California — is the one mechanism whose whole premise is the principle this chapter is built on: 34 U.S.C. § 12601, a federal consent decree, an independent monitor, authority from a federal court that sits above the California Constitution. That door exists. It has not opened. The rest of this report is a public record of why it should.

The UCSF Finding: In July 2025, POST’s own Advisory Board — the body deliberately built with a public majority, designed to be independent from the profession it disciplines — named the UCSF Police Department as a non-reporting agency under California law. UCSF is a University of California campus. Its police department answers to the Regents. The identification was made and documented. No UC-specific enforcement action followed. Source: citizenportal.ai/articles/6165735/; POST 2024 POSAD Annual Report.

The chapters that follow fill in the structural background. Chapter 4 documents the governance chain — ten independent campus police departments, ten chiefs, no unified command, no elected official anywhere in the line of authority. Chapter 5 walks through all ten departments one by one: staffing, budget, chief tenure, and the documented incident record at each campus. Together they show what a system looks like when no one above the Chancellor answers to a voter.

The wall was built in 1879. This report is a public record of everyone who has knocked on it since.

The Closing Observation: This report is knocking on all three doors. This chapter documents that the doors have been knocked on before. Door 1 produced reform announcements that did not produce structural change. Door 2 produced the right vehicle and the wrong outcome in committee. Door 3 has been unlocked by statute for seventy-seven years and has never been opened.

The principle is older than the California Constitution. No one should be judge in their own case. The wall was built in 1879. This report is a public record of everyone who has knocked on it since — and an argument that it is past time for the one door that was never tried.
