Officer 341’s Highly Documented Record

1989 – 1996 and Beyond
The UCLA Police Department Whistleblower Timeline

Let Bygones Be Bygones

The events documented in this chapter span thirty-seven years — from 1989, when Officer 341 joined the UCLA Police Department, on to 1996, when a line-of-duty injury ended his patrol career, and into the years that followed his post-injury all the way until he reached 65 years old.

What you are about to read is not everything that happened during that period. It is a substantial portion of it, reconstructed from contemporaneous records, grievance files, court filings, public records, eyewitness testimony, agency correspondence, and Officer 341’s own documented reports.

With one exception, the names of the UCLA police officers and supervisors involved in these events have been anonymized. Thirty-seven years have passed. Every officer referenced in this chapter has long since retired from law enforcement. Many built subsequent careers. Some are no longer living. Let bygones be bygones.

The anonymization is not a courtesy extended to protect the guilty or protect the innocent. It is a recognition — earned through decades of reflection — that the University of California system these officers operated inside was itself the primary instrument of harm.

The University of California’s constitutional structure, its institutional chain of command, and the absence of any external oversight mechanism with actual enforcement authority created conditions under which individual officers — even officers who recognized misconduct — had no viable path to correct it.

This same structural wall that prevented outside agencies from holding UC accountable also trapped the people inside. An officer who objected to what command staff was doing had two choices: comply or become the next target. Many complied. Officer 341 did not.

That is not a defense of what they did. The conduct documented here — the fabricated investigations, the retaliatory transfers, the threats, the lies — was real, and it was wrong. But the honest assessment, after thirty-seven years, is that the institution bears more responsibility for the pattern than any individual actor within it.

The Regents of the University of California built a system with no external accountability. Command staff operated exactly the way a system with no external accountability would predict. The command officers and staff who carried out the retaliation were themselves subject to the same institutional pressures that made whistleblowing career suicide.

The one exception is Acting Chief Karl T. Ross, who assumed command after the Chief was terminated as a direct result of Officer 341’s whistleblower complaints. In that role, back in the day, Ross represented to 341’s attorney that 341’s personnel file had been destroyed — which, as it turned out, happened to be untrue according to the present police commanders at the UCLA Police Department.

This one act by Ross set in motion a chain of downstream consequences that are still unfolding thirty years later. These are not the acts of an administrator navigating a difficult situation. They are deliberate, targeted, and malicious — too central to the current POST complaints to anonymize. His acts are not institutional byproducts. They are individual choices with individual consequences that persist to this day.

From the “Isn’t that ironic” department, one final detail. Before Officer 341 carried that badge number, it was Karl T. Ross’s badge number when he was a patrol officer. After Officer 341 was forced out of UCPD, a lieutenant informed him that Ross retired Badge 341 permanently. It was never issued again.

Every fact in this chapter is sourced from the original documented record. Every grievance number, complaint date, agency contact, and institutional response is independently verifiable in the University of California’s own records — and in case anyone on the University’s legal team was wondering: yes, Officer 341 still has all his files.

I. Before UCLA — Six Years on the Street

Before joining the UCLA Police Department in December 1989, Officer 341 had served as a sworn peace officer for approximately six years. He graduated from Rio Hondo College Police Academy (Class 61) in 1983, earning California POST certification.

San Gabriel Police Department (1983–1985): Dispatcher. Patrol. Burglary Suppression. Officer in Charge of San Gabriel Police Explorer Post. School Liaison Officer.

Culver City Police Department (1985–1988): Patrol. Burglary Suppression. HS 11550 Drug Enforcement. K-9 Agitator. Certified Field Training Officer. CHP-trained Motor Officer. Traffic Reconstruction Expert. Drug and Alcohol Recognition (DAR) Expert.

Across his 13-year sworn career: 25 Bureau Commendations. 16 Citizen Commendations. 31 Field Commendations. He was bringing a personal computer to the station to write reports in 1983 — before most departments had one. POST maintains his complete training record and can verify many additional skill sets beyond those listed here.

In 2013, at the age of 52, Officer 341 completed the POST Requalification Course — a 160-hour minimum program — at the Napa Valley College Criminal Justice Training Center, which reactivated 341’s eligibility to be hired back as a peace officer in California. Under POST’s three-year rule, that eligibility stays active until another three-year break in qualifying employment accrues — in plain English, a rolling three-year hiring license, renewable by repeating the course.

He arrived at UCLA as a POST CERTIFIED FIELD TRAINING OFFICER, a certification most of his UCPD colleagues did not have. He was a highly experienced street officer — not a recruit, not a probationer learning the job, but a working cop with specialized certifications and real casework behind him. What he walked into at UCLA was a department that operated under a set of rules he had never encountered in municipal policing — not because the officers were fundamentally different, but because the institution above them was.

II. The First Year — 1990

July 1990
The Regent’s Parking Ticket

During a UC Regents meeting on the UCLA campus, Officer 341 personally wrote a parking ticket to a vehicle driven by a UC Regent. Two command officers ordered him to cancel the ticket. He documented the directive as a ticket-fixing case “directed by the Chancellor, the Regent, and UCLA police administrators.”

He was not a secondhand reporter. He was the officer who wrote the ticket and was directly ordered by his chain of command to void it. Ordering a police officer to cancel a lawfully issued citation is illegal. Both command officers who issued the order became central figures in the retaliatory campaign that followed.

Ticket-Fixing — Reported

During this period, a lieutenant told Officer 341 and numerous other officers that he was the Chief’s and Assistant Chief’s “AX MAN” and “HATCHET MAN — the one responsible for getting rid of troublemakers, writing paper on people and initiating investigations.”

This is a direct admission of an assigned role in a retaliatory scheme — he identified himself as the designated enforcement tool of the two officers above him in the chain of command. The statement was made to multiple officers, establishing potential corroborating witnesses.

Admitted Retaliation Scheme

UCLA Medical Center Criminal Investigation — Offense Report #01-90-004164

Officer 341 personally initiated and conducted a criminal investigation into systematic criminal activity inside the UCLA Center for Health Sciences. Through eight tape-recorded conversations with a confidential informant, he documented:

He placed the eight tape-recorded conversations into evidence under Property Report #0190000204.

III. The Grievances — 1991–1992

Beginning in 1991, Officer 341 filed a series of formal grievances against UCLA PD command staff. Every grievance he filed against the University was won. The University’s institutional response to losing each proceeding was escalation rather than compliance.

May 12, 1991
The Quid Pro Quo — “Drop Your Claims and We’ll Transfer You”

Officer 341 and his supervisor met with the Chief of Police and two Assistant Chiefs. He had requested transfer away from the lieutenant who had identified himself as the department’s “Ax Man.” The three command officers agreed to the transfer — on the condition that he drop his workers’ compensation claim and grievances.

This later turned out to be a fatal mistake. The Chief used the opportunity to try to claim the department had never received a workers’ compensation filing from 341 at all — an assertion contradicted by the claims administrator’s own file, by Applied Risk Management’s interview notes, and by Officer 341’s retained correspondence.

He complied. He was transferred. From June 1991 to January 1992, his documented stomach and heart problems improved and he was asymptomatic.

On January 6, 1992 — seven months later — against his numerous documented protests, he was transferred back to the same lieutenant’s supervision. His medical and personnel problems immediately resumed. As documented below, this same supervisor was subsequently demoted for sexual, racial, and general harassment.

Three command officers had conditioned relief from documented harassment on the surrender of statutory rights, with a supervisor as witness. When the officer complied, the University broke its own agreement. The fact that his medical conditions resolved during the transfer period and resumed upon return establishes that the University knew the source of the harm.

Quid Pro Quo — Agreement Broken
1991
Applied Risk Management Claims Administrator — The Interview Notes That Disappeared

Applied Risk Management claims administrator John Hodson interviewed Officer 341 about job stressors. At the end of the interview, Hodson stated that Officer 341’s filing of a workers’ compensation claim “would not help the grievances” he had filed — a statement by the University’s own claims administrator tying the WC claim to the grievance record.

Hodson’s interview notes later disappeared. When Officer 341 asked for them, Michael Lehman told him the notes “don’t exist.” Officer 341 subsequently saw a letter from Hodson at his treating physician’s office referencing the same conversation — confirming the notes had existed and had been removed from the claims file.

Every Grievance Won
May 13, 1992
Command Obstruction of the Hospital Criminal Investigation

Officer 341 sent a written protest through the chain of command to the Assistant Chief. The protest objected to the Assistant Chief having declared the Center for Health Sciences criminal investigation “closed” while simultaneously assigning an unqualified officer to conduct further investigation into the same matter.

He warned the Assistant Chief directly in writing: “I feel you are mismanaging this investigation and allowing CHS management personnel to break the law and further damage the University.” He concluded: “If you do not care to investigate these allegations, I will find someone outside our department who will.”

The investigation was never prosecuted.

This occurred just prior to the UCLA Medical Center coming under the new requirements of AB 508 (Chapter 936, Statutes of 1993) — the California law that added Health & Safety Code §§ 1257.7 and 1257.8, requiring California hospitals to conduct annual security and safety assessments, implement a written security plan, and report injuries sustained by on-duty hospital personnel to local law enforcement. Officer 341 later verified that his investigation of criminal activity at the UCLA Center for Health Sciences was never submitted to the reporting stream the new law required.

May 18, 1992
Two Simultaneous Retaliatory Acts — Same Day

Act 1: A lieutenant circulated a completely fabricated rumor that Officer 341 “had tried to obtain an appointment with the Chancellor, was refused, became upset and had to be physically restrained and removed from his office” and “had been sent to a psychologist for evaluation.” None of this occurred. The rumor was circulated before the officer had written to the Chancellor — establishing preemptive retaliation against protected petition activity that had not yet occurred.

Act 2: On the same day, the officer discovered a memo in the computer printer from the Assistant Chief to the Chief stating: “I believe 341 will again come down with stomach problems and seek a medical retirement. This may be his actual intent. Enough is enough!”

Two command staff officers engaged in simultaneous documented adverse actions on the same day — coordinated institutional action.

VI. The Investigation That Proved Him Right — 1992–1993

December 9, 1992
The Tape That Proved He Was Telling the Truth

The lieutenant attended a mutual aid training session in Orange County and told officers in the class — including a friend of Officer 341 — that Officer 341 was “a paranoid schizophrenic, cocaine user,” that he filed workers’ compensation claims for stress, and that the department “was definitely going to force him out — he won’t get his job back.”

The friend telephoned 341 from the training course. 341 immediately called Karl Ross, who recalled the lieutenant to the station and placed him on administrative leave. A captain was assigned to investigate and concluded 341 was lying.

341’s friend then came in for his interview with the captain and produced a tape recording of the entire training session that inadvertently captured the lieutenant’s statements verbatim — conclusively proving 341 was truthful and the investigation conclusion was factually wrong.

Tape Recording — Proved Truthful
January 29, 1993
Campus Human Resources Fact-Finding — Lieutenant Found in Violation

Campus Human Resources fact-finder Tina Simmons completed her investigation and found that the lieutenant had violated University policy. The fact-finding report is a University-generated document confirming Officer 341’s complaints were substantiated by the University’s own investigator.

Contained within the fact-finding was a statement from the Assistant Chief in which he stated Officer 341 was “riding the worker’s comp gravy train” and that his goal was to “milk the department and sue.” He added that the claims were “not as large as I reported them to be and were manipulated, orchestrated, and blown out of proportion.”

This statement was made eight months after the Assistant Chief’s own “Enough is enough!” memo — documenting an eight-month progression of institutional animus by the same command officer against the same whistleblower.

Complaints Substantiated by University

The lieutenant was demoted two ranks from lieutenant to police officer. After an administrative hearing, he retained the rank of sergeant due to a POBR technicality — the failure to provide Lybarger/Miranda warnings before the compelled investigation, as required by Government Code § 3300 et seq.

VII. The Civil Rights Case and the Escalation That Followed

January 29, 1993
UCPD 341 v. The Regents of the University of California — Case No. SC022125

Filed in Santa Monica Superior Court. Named defendants included the Regents, Acting Chief Karl T. Ross, and other UCLA PD officials. Claims included discrimination, whistleblowing, and acts against public policy arising from his employment and his role as founding president of FUPOA.

The case received contemporaneous coverage in the Los Angeles Times on February 25, 1993 and April 24, 1994. The Santa Monica College Corsair published a two-part investigative series on the case. Officer 341’s October 1993 escalation correspondence was transmitted to the Daily Bruin, the Los Angeles Times, and 60 Minutes — the highest-profile media escalation available to him at that time. UCLA’s own Luskin Center for History and Policy subsequently cited Officer 341’s case by name in its March 2022 institutional publication, “The History of Racism and the Quest for Racial Justice at UCLA.”

Civil Rights Case Filed

Letter to the Chancellor

Officer 341 wrote directly to the Chancellor, stating he had been on paid leave “for no apparent reason or justification, getting paid full salary and benefits of over $42,000.00 a year, to sit at home and do nothing,” and reminded the Chancellor: “I am not the person who was investigated for misconduct. I was the victim of the misconduct.”

May 7, 1993
Next-Day Retaliatory Escalation

The day after the letter to the Chancellor, Acting Chief Karl T. Ross changed Officer 341’s leave status from Administrative Leave to Investigatory Leave and ordered him to surrender his gun, badge, and identification card — items he had already surrendered eleven months earlier.

He was not informed what he was being investigated for until almost a year later. When he asked the Internal Affairs investigating officer why he was being investigated, the officer stated he “did not know and he was just following orders.”

Under Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, the one-day temporal proximity is sufficient to establish that the petition was a “contributing factor” in the adverse action.

Dr. Merz recommended evaluation for cardiomyopathy. July 5, 1994
Formal Statutory Complaint — Government Code § 8547.10

Officer 341 sent identical formal complaints simultaneously to the Chancellor and the UC President, invoking the Reporting of Improper Governmental Activities Act and formally evoking his right to pursue civil action and collect punitive damages and attorney’s fees. Both letters were signed under penalty of perjury. Copies were transmitted to the Bureau of State Audits and the Los Angeles Times.

The complaint named seven individual UC employees as having engaged in acts of fraud in connection with the fabricated Internal Affairs investigation, including altering official documents, omitting witnesses, concealing evidence, and lying about facts that would prove Officer 341’s innocence.

Under Government Code § 8547.10, the University was required to reach a decision regarding the complaint “within the time limits established by the Regents.” Read that again. The statute delegated the decision back to the Regents themselves — on their own timeline. The fox investigates the henhouse on the fox’s schedule. The documented record does not reflect that the University ever reached the required decision or notified him of any investigation. The 33-year-old officer used every tool available to him. The 65-year-old now understands why none of them worked.

VIII. The Skelly Hearing — The Property Log That Ended It

The Internal Affairs investigation (IA 93-08) that formed the basis for the University’s termination attempt was conducted by a lieutenant who was subsequently terminated himself — for falsifying the investigation report.

At his Skelly pre-termination hearing (Skelly v. State Personnel Board (1975) 15 Cal.3d 194), Officer 341 produced the UCPD Property Sheet Log — the department’s own official evidence intake record — which proved that the investigation report was fabricated. The Chief who presided over the Skelly hearing ended the hearing and walked out upon being presented with the property log.

Officer 341 was not terminated. He was reinstated to full active patrol duty in approximately 1995.

Institutional Outcome: As a direct result of Officer 341’s whistleblowing activities and the investigations that followed, the University of California investigated and terminated the Chief of Police and several members of his command staff. The lieutenant who conducted the fabricated Internal Affairs investigation was terminated from the UCLA Police Department for falsifying the investigation report.

2002 — Los Alamos National Laboratory
The Same Playbook, Different UC Property — Walp & Doran

The pattern documented in this chapter is not isolated to UCLA PD. The University of California managed Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL) under contract with the United States Department of Energy from 1943 until 2006 — a sixty-three-year span. During the late 1990s, LANL was rocked by multiple national security scandals, including missing classified hard drives and failed security tests in which mock intruders successfully penetrated the facility.

In 2002, UC hired two former federal investigators, Glenn Walp and Steven Doran, to investigate theft and security breaches at LANL. Walp and Doran uncovered documented misconduct. UC’s response was to fire them. They lost their livelihoods. Their disclosures were later vindicated in congressional hearings. UC’s contract to manage LANL was not renewed after 2006.

Different property. Different era. Same institutional response to whistleblowing: terminate the reporter, protect the institution. The structural wall that produced the outcomes documented in this chapter produced identical outcomes at a national security laboratory six hundred miles away.

In 2016, Officer 341 met Steven Doran through a mutual security-industry contact. The two became good friends, and years later — telling war stories — they realized they had walked the same road inside the University of California. As part of his 2003 settlement with UC, Doran was reinstated and placed at the UC President’s Office in Oakland as Director of Public Safety and Systems Security — a role whose portfolio, according to Department of Defense reporting at the time, included security oversight at the three UC-managed national laboratories and at all ten University of California campuses (Military.com — “Fired Whistleblower Now Labs’ Security Chief,” Feb. 26, 2003; Los Angeles Times — “Fired Whistleblower at Los Alamos Wins $930,000 Settlement From UC,” Aug. 21, 2003). In cop shorthand: they made him the chief over all the UC chiefs. The institution fired him for doing his job, then — to buy silence — gave him the job of watching everyone else’s.

What happened to that consolidated “chief over all UC chiefs” authority in the years that followed — and why no single executive holds it today — is examined in Chapter 4: Ten Chiefs, No Boss, No Voter.

The Pattern: When every internal channel had been exhausted or was structurally compromised — including the Chancellor himself being implicated in the ticket-fixing conduct Officer 341 was reporting — the officer exercised his right to petition external authorities including the District Attorney, the FBI, the Bureau of State Audits, and the press. Every channel was used. Every channel is documented. And not a single external agency took enforcement action against the University of California.

This is the documented pattern the Preface told you was coming. 341 paid the price. The University kept its authority and the agencies that were supposed to provide oversight discovered — if they even tried — that the constitutional wall surrounding the Regents made enforcement structurally impossible.

Thirty-seven years later, 341 filed these POST complaints against the next generation of UCLA PD command officers. The University’s response has been identical: characterize the personnel file as located, then in transit, then archived, then possibly non-existent. The same playbook. The same institution. The same wall.
