THE PARKING TICKET THAT STARTED IT ALL.

July 1990 — Officer 341 wrote a parking ticket to a Regent’s vehicle at a Regents meeting on the UCLA campus.

Two commanding officers ordered 341 to cancel the ticket.

He refused — and reported the incident as a ticket-fixing case directed by the Chancellor, the Regent, and UCLA police administrators, and filed a formal grievance, AR 91-01-14 — the first formal grievance in the record.

Ordering a peace officer to void a lawfully issued citation is illegal under California law. Both commanding officers who issued the order became central figures in the six-year retaliatory campaign that ended with Officer 341 being forced out of UCPD in 1996 — the same year the current UCLA Chief of Police graduated from this university.

Both of those commanding officers became central figures in the retaliation lawsuit that followed.

Section I The Chief Who Cannot Say It Out Loud

The current UCLA Chief of Police is a named respondent in the POST complaint this Preface introduces. He is also, in the view of the complainant, the most qualified chief UCLA has ever hired — and the clearest proof that qualifications are not the variable. The complaint had to name him because the office he holds is the one that took the action under review. But the argument this Preface makes is not that a different chief would have done differently. The argument is that no University of California Chief of Police could.

The current UCLA Chief of Police reports to work every morning inside a contradiction. He was trained to enforce accountability. He runs a department that has never been subject to it.

The current UCLA Chief of Police was a UCLA undergraduate from 1992 to 1996. He was a political science major on this campus during the final four years of Officer 341’s UCPD career — the years documented in Chapter 05 of this report. He was here for all of it. The same campus. The same institution. Different side of the badge.

The year he graduated UCLA — 1996 — was the same year Officer 341 was forced out of UCPD. He then joined the Los Angeles Police Department and spent the next twenty-nine years inside the single municipal department that proved federal police accountability works. He served at LAPD during the 2001–2013 federal consent decree — 191 reforms ordered under what is now 34 U.S.C. § 12601 — and rose to command rank inside the reformed department. He attended the FBI National Academy. He completed the Sherman Block Supervisory Leadership Institute and the LAPD Command Development Program. He holds a Master of Public Administration from USC. He commanded North Hollywood and served in Metro Division.

He is, on paper, the most qualified chief UCLA has ever hired. He is over-qualified for a system that does not use what he was trained to provide. He was sworn in as UCLA Chief of Police in October 2025. He is the fourth chief at UCPD in three years.

Every morning he walks into a department where none of what he was trained on applies. No federal consent decree has ever touched a UC police department. No pattern-or-practice investigation. No external civilian oversight body with subpoena power. The tool he watched work at LAPD — 34 U.S.C. § 12601 — has never been used at any UC campus in the statute’s entire existence. He is running a police department in the one corner of California where his training does not apply.

Isn’t there a law? Can’t the state do something? Can’t the federal government do something? Those are the chief’s questions. He cannot answer them out loud without ending his tenure. He is trapped inside a Kobayashi Maru scenario that sits inside the oldest principle in law: nemo iudex in causa sua — no one should be judge in their own case. When the institution being investigated is also the institution running the investigation, the principle says: that is not a fair process. That is not a process that produces real accountability. The chief knows this. His training is built on it. His job forbids him from saying so.

If you think the chief is in a tight spot, you’re right, he is — look two buildings over. On February 13, 2026, UCLA’s own Chief Financial Officer, Stephen Agostini, told the Daily Bruin that the university’s annual financial reports since 2002 were “erroneous and unaudited,” that UCLA carried a $425 million structural deficit, and that a $150 million finance-modernization project had been wasted. Four days later, on February 17, Chancellor Frenk fired him.

On March 30, 2026, Culver City hired him as their Chief Financial Officer — and in the hiring announcement, Culver City credited him with closing a $300 million budget gap at UCLA and delivering a balanced budget. The same institution that fired him for speaking became the line on his resume that got him his next job. That is the proof of concept for this Preface. A UCLA official told the truth out loud, was removed in four days, and was publicly vindicated six weeks later by the next employer who read his record. The chief has read that record too. He knows exactly how the sentence ends. That is why he cannot say it himself.

That contradiction — a qualified chief who cannot deploy what he was trained on, named as a respondent for conduct the office itself required — is the frame for everything that follows. The rest of this Preface documents the specific case that brought the complaint, and the structural reasons the POST process it was filed under cannot, by its own design, resolve it.

Section II Schrödinger’s “Retired” Peace Officer, Badge 341

In quantum physics, Schrödinger’s cat exists in a superposition of two states — simultaneously alive and dead inside an unopened box — until an observer opens the box and collapses the wavefunction into one, and only one, measured outcome.

For over thirty years, the University of California has held Peace Officer 341 in precisely that superposition: simultaneously a Duty Disability Income recipient and a Service Retiree — two mutually exclusive eigenstates under the UC Retirement Plan — and the wavefunction – is the cat dead or alive? – collapses the moment somebody opens the box.

The box is Officer 341’s personnel file.

The Employment Misclassifications

On August 25, 2023, and again on February 10, 2026, three command-level officers inside a University of California police department — the Captain, the Chief, and the Lieutenant — participated in classifying Peace Officer 341’s employment, in writing, as “status was designated as medically separated on March 1, 1996,” and used that classification to cancel his concealed-carry permit. That written determination directly contradicts the University’s own eleven years of treating him as “retired” — including two CCW permits issued by UCLA PD itself.

Then after three formal requests, the officers withheld his legally protected personnel file — the file Government Code § 3306.5 guarantees every California peace officer the right to inspect — the one file whose contents would collapse the superposition and fix his employment classification as one state or the other.

Opening Schrödinger’s box, Officer 341’s personnel file, will confirm the employment classification. Either eigenstate costs the University money.

A) Observed state: “Medically Separated” / Duty Disability Income. The exact status the University itself assigned him in 2001, retroactive to March 1, 1996, when its own claims administrator, Octagon Risk Services, declared him permanently and stationarily disabled. The University’s own 1099-R records confirm it: from 2009 through 2014, every year, UC reported his pension under Distribution Code 3 — Disability, with the taxable amount correctly shown as a small fraction of the gross. Then, in 2015 a University UCRAYS rep called Officer 341 and told him he needed to convert his disability income to a service retirement or he would lose all his income (turns out that was a lie). So, on June 1, 2015, without a hearing, and without Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board approval, UC flipped the code and reported Officer 341’s 2015 1099-R as Code 2 — Early Retirement. UC then reported his income that way for ten straight years (2015–2024); earlier 1099-R records will confirm the entire period back to 1996. If state A is true, then the University owes him hundreds of thousands of dollars in back pay plus the tax consequences of its own misclassification.

B) Observed state: “Retired” / Service Retirement. The way the University began treating his record on June 1, 2015 and continued to treat it for over a decade. If that is the state the file confirms, then under Labor Code § 5804 — which grants the Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board continuing jurisdiction over every settlement it approves — they owe him back pay for unilaterally separating him from his workers’ comp case without court approval, and the CCW they just took away.

Either Way the University Loses

Either employment classification measurement collapses against the officers and the University. So they refused to measure. They locked the box and made the file disappear — with cover from twenty-five documented data breaches inside the University’s own record-keeping systems, and from the University’s own Chief Financial Officer, who stood in front of the Daily Bruin on February 13, 2026 and said UCLA’s annual financial reports posted on its website “since 2002 are erroneous” and unaudited, that the $213 million project to modernize UCLA’s financial systems produced “nothing to show for it,” and that he had “rarely seen the kind of financial management flaws and failures” he saw at UCLA. Four days later, UCLA fired him.

Why Is This Complaint Important to Officers Everywhere?

If a department can make a peace officer’s personnel file disappear when that file becomes evidence against the department itself, it can happen to any officer in California — or any officer in America.

This report does not present an abstract regulatory dispute. It presents documented conduct by three POST-certified commanding officers whose collective actions and inactions directly and presently interfere with 65-year-old Officer 341’s cancer and A-Fib treatment and his ability to obtain and maintain the continuous, uninterrupted medical insurance and treatment he is entitled to and protected by the Fourteenth Amendment’s Due Process Clause, and by the Fifth Amendment where federal law reaches, including the Law Enforcement Officers Safety Act.

The Fourteenth Amendment says the government can’t take your property without giving you a hearing first. Health insurance that’s part of your retirement is property — courts have said so since Goldberg v. Kelly (1970). They took it. No notice. No hearing. That’s the violation. The University of California is a state actor. The commanding officers named in this complaint are state actors. The retiree whose coverage was canceled mid-treatment is the person the Amendment was written to protect.

Officer 341’s personnel record is not a bureaucratic record of historical interest. These commanding officers have variously reviewed it, cited it, and relied upon it for official determinations — and then simultaneously characterized it as located, in transit, archived, and possibly non-existent. Impossible simultaneous states of existence.

Officer 341’s personnel record is the one record whose production resolves his employment classification — and that classification determines whether he is entitled to disability retiree health insurance coverage, tax-exempt disability income, and the protections of the federal Law Enforcement Officers Safety Act.

Without definitive resolution of that classification, the University of California’s benefits administration has unilaterally canceled his health insurance without notice, reinstated it without notice, then set a future cancellation date that ran afoul of federal insurance regulations, all while Officer 341 was undergoing active treatment for concurrent life-threatening conditions.

The conduct by the UCLA commanding officers documented herein is the direct and proximate cause of that cancellation’s ability to persist unresolved.

Under Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court, 18 Cal.4th 1, 11–12 (1998), and Williams v. The four Lieutenant statements catalogued above are the documentary record on which both presumptions rest.

The Department has therefore placed itself, in writing, in the position of having admitted — through the Lieutenant, the custodian of the file the Captain quoted from to issue Classification #4 (the “medically separated on March 1, 1996” designation) — that the foundational document supporting Classification #4 may no longer exist, after the institution received a litigation hold expressly forbidding its destruction. The classification stands. The file does not. The captain who issued the classification, the captain who twice wrote that he had reviewed the file, and the captain who was copied on every custodial statement that followed, has not explained the contradiction.

The custody-of-file statements catalogued in the table above were issued by sworn California peace officers acting under color of authority, in writing, on University police-department letterhead, after Officer 341 had served a written litigation hold (EX 20) on the Captain on February 9, 2026. The file at issue is Officer 341’s peace-officer personnel file — a category of record protected under California Penal Code § 832.7 and the Pitchess-discovery framework. Where peace officers, acting in their official capacity, issue written statements that cannot be reconciled with their own prior written statements regarding a protected personnel file subject to a known preservation demand, the conduct triggers, at minimum, the following authorities:

  • California Penal Code § 118.1 — A peace officer who files any report relating to the commission of a criminal offense or a peace officer’s investigatory function knowing the report to be false, in any material respect, is guilty of a misdemeanor (or, where the report concerns alleged criminal conduct of another, a felony). The custody-of-file statements relate directly to a personnel file Officer 341 had identified as the predicate for a Cal. Labor Code § 132(a) crime report filed February 19, 2026.
  • California Penal Code § 135 — Willful destruction, alteration, or concealment of any document subject to legal process. The February 9, 2026 litigation hold was such process.
  • California Penal Code § 135.5 — Destruction or concealment of physical evidence relating to a public investigation. The Cal. Labor Code § 132(a) report and the parallel POST complaints (PUBLIC-26-000212, -000213, -000214) are public investigations.
  • California Government Code § 1222 — Every wilful omission to perform any duty enjoined by law upon any public officer is punishable as a misdemeanor. The duty to preserve evidence after written notice not to destroy it is such a duty.
  • California Penal Code § 832.7 — The peace-officer personnel file at issue is itself a statutorily protected record. Sworn personnel issuing materially false written statements about the custodial status of such a file is misconduct on its face.
  • California Penal Code § 13510.8 (SB 2) — Mandatory POST reporting of allegations of serious misconduct by peace officers, including dishonesty in the reporting or investigation of misconduct.
  • UC Whistleblower Policy 1100171 — UC personnel are required to report suspected improper governmental activities. False statements by sworn UC personnel regarding the custody of a protected personnel file fall squarely within the policy’s definition of an improper governmental activity.

The campus compliance office is hereby placed on notice that the custody-of-file statements catalogued above are not a clerical inconvenience. They are written statements by sworn peace officers about a protected personnel file, made after a documented litigation hold, that cannot be reconciled with the same officers’ own prior written statements that they had reviewed the file. The conduct fits, on its face, the authorities listed above. Any “matter closed” determination by the campus compliance office is incompatible with the statutory framework that governs the same conduct.

Two POST Authorities Apply Here, Not One

The University police department’s voluntary participation in the POST program rests on two distinct statutory authorities, and the conduct catalogued in this section triggers both.

AUTHORITY 1 — Individual Officer Decertification. Senate Bill 2 / Penal Code § 13510.8 + Title 11 CCR § 1205. The Peace Officer Standards Accountability Division (POSAD) holds jurisdiction to revoke the certification of any individual sworn peace officer whose conduct meets one of the nine grounds for serious misconduct, including dishonesty in the reporting or investigation of misconduct. Three POSAD complaints have already been filed by Officer 341: PUBLIC-26-000212 against the Captain; PUBLIC-26-000213 against the Chief; PUBLIC-26-000214 against the Lieutenant (ret.). All three have been acknowledged by POSAD.

AUTHORITY 2 — Agency Compliance Audit. Penal Code § 13512 + Title 11 CCR § 1010. Separate from individual decertification, POST holds statutory authority to audit any agency in the POST program for non-compliance with selection, training, and recordkeeping standards, and with all Commission regulations. PC § 13512 directs POST to “make inquiries as necessary to determine if agencies in the POST program are adhering to selection and training standards.” Title 11 CCR § 1010 conditions agency POST eligibility on continuing adherence to all Commission regulations. POST’s own published guidance confirms that the Standards & Training Bureau conducts agency compliance audits under these authorities.

Where an agency cannot, on its own written record, sustain a coherent classification of one sworn officer’s employment status — and where the agency cannot, after a documented litigation hold, locate the personnel file that supports its own classification decision — the agency’s compliance with the Commission’s recordkeeping, CCW, and personnel-records regulations is itself a question for audit. Per the POST Commission Policy Manual (2024 edition), agency audits result in formal findings of “Voluntary Compliance” or “Voluntary Non-Compliance” — the latter defined as the situation in which the agency is aware of its deficiencies and is making little or no progress to correct them.

The two authorities are complementary, not duplicative. Individual decertification under SB 2 holds named officers personally accountable for serious misconduct. An agency audit under PC § 13512 examines whether the institution itself is in compliance with the rules of POST participation. Both are warranted on this record. A formal request for a Standards & Training Bureau agency audit under PC § 13512 and Title 11 CCR § 1010, cross-referenced to the three pending POSAD complaints, has been transmitted in parallel to this filing.

Legal Significance — Chain of Custody, Admissibility, and Spoliation

The seven classifications above were issued in writing by sworn California peace officers — the Captain, two Lieutenants, and the Chief in his capacity as the final decisionmaker on these matters — speaking as state actors with custodial authority over Officer 341’s personnel file. The legal weight of those statements, in any forum that adjudicates Officer 341’s classification, is governed by California’s evidentiary framework:

• California Evidence Code § 1280 (Public Employee Records Exception). Records made by a public employee within the scope of duty are admissible to prove the act or condition stated. The Captain’s August 25, 2023 letter, his February 10, 2026 email, and the Lieutenant’s February 24, 2026 email were issued on University police-department letterhead by sworn personnel acting in the scope of their administrative duties. They are admissible as substantive evidence of the Department’s official position on Officer 341’s status — against the agency that issued them.

• California Evidence Code § 1271 (Business Records Exception). The Department’s internal personnel file — the source the Captain and the Lieutenant expressly cited — is itself admissible business-record evidence. The Captain’s direct quotation from that file (the “medically separated on March 1, 1996” designation, classification #4 above) carries the file’s own evidentiary weight as a matter of law.

• California Evidence Code § 413 (Failure to Produce Evidence). The Department’s documented inability to locate Officer 341’s archived personnel file (the Lieutenant, February 24, 2026, 3:04 PM, classification #7 above) permits the trier of fact to draw the inference that the missing file would, if produced, support Officer 341’s position rather than UC’s.

• Spoliation doctrine. Under Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, and its progeny, where a party with custodial duty over evidence loses, destroys, or fails to produce that evidence, the trier of fact may presume the lost evidence was unfavorable to the custodian. The Department, as the custodial agency for Officer 341’s sworn-officer personnel file, bears that risk of presumption on every classification question now in dispute.

In any forum that adjudicates Officer 341’s classification — the Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board, an administrative review, or a court of competent jurisdiction — the Captain’s and Lieutenant’s emails are not opinion, hearsay, or one party’s side of the story. They are the official position of the University police department, admissible on UC’s own records, against UC.

The One Classification That Controls the Other Six

Of the seven classifications the Department has issued Officer 341 in 30 months, one controls them all. On August 25, 2023, the Captain — a sworn California peace officer acting as a state actor with custodial authority over Officer 341’s personnel file — stated in writing on University police-department letterhead: “Your status was designated as medically separated on March 1, 1996.” That single sentence, issued by the agency with the most authoritative records UC possesses on this question, resolves the binary by force of UC’s own controlling case law.

Under Jacobs v. Regents of the University of California (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 17 — the very case the Captain himself attached to his denial letter as “the most recent case that transpired through the court of appeals” and identified as the controlling authority on Officer 341’s status — and its predecessor Alberts v. Regents (Alameda Super. Ct., September 24, 2012) [unpublished], the legal term that describes Officer 341 is fixed: he is a Duty Disability Income recipient who, in the words of UC’s own Gold Book Policy 1700.1(b)(1) (August 18, 2020), “has not retired or separated from the University of California.” Jacobs holds expressly that “the UCRP contains no disability retirement” and that DDI recipients “are not retired under the UCRP.” The two states — DDI recipient and retiree — are mutually exclusive under UCRP and under the controlling case law.

The Department, by the hand of its own captain, citing the very case that controls the question, classified Officer 341 as medically separated due to industrial injury — which under Jacobs and the Gold Book is the precise factual predicate for the legal classification “Duty Disability Income recipient.” The agency with custodial authority over the underlying personnel record has therefore confirmed Officer 341’s UCRP status, by name, on University police-department letterhead, in writing, citing the same case law that binds UC.

Every contradictory classification UC has issued since — the 2015 reclassification of Officer 341 to “regular service retirement” without WCAB approval; the 2015 dual-coded IRS Form 1099-R (Code 2 and Code 3 in the same tax year); the 2016–2024 Code 2 and Code 7 distributions; the $727.48-per-month premium deductions taken from a DDI recipient whose medical coverage is employer-paid under the workers’ compensation settlement; the Williams-Cook “non-compliance with retiree health policy” determination of February 27, 2026; and the April 1, 2026 Anthem termination — collides head-on with the Captain’s own statement, with Jacobs, and with UC’s own Gold Book policy that binds the institution.

UC cannot, on a single set of facts, treat Officer 341 as both medically separated (the Department’s position, classification #4 above) and retired (the systemwide benefits office’s position) when its own controlling case law and its own published policy expressly forbid the hybrid. One office is right and the other is wrong; under Jacobs, the office that is right is the one whose classification matches the underlying factual record — and on that record, the Department’s “medically separated” finding is the one supported by the August 8, 2001 Octagon permanent-and-stationary determination, the WCAB-approved settlement in Claim No. 199650445, the WCAB’s exclusive jurisdiction under California Labor Code §§ 5803–5804, and the federal tax treatment Officer 341 actually received from 2010 through 2014 (Code 3, Disability, 92.7% tax-free).

The name UC’s own case law gives Officer 341 is therefore not “retiree.” It is Duty Disability Income recipient — and that classification was confirmed on University police-department letterhead, by the Captain himself, on August 25, 2023, citing the very appellate decision that bound UC then and binds UC now.

Section V Is POST Participation Mandatory?“Participation in the POST Program is Voluntary.” — Cal. Code Regs. tit. 11, § 1010(a)(2)
⚠  Walking Away from POST Has Almost No Consequences  ⚠
  • A city, county, or UC campus chooses to be a POST-participating agency. The governing statute, Cal. Penal Code § 13510.1, describes agencies that “participate” in the POST Program — not agencies required to hold a POST-issued operating license. If a department walks away from POST, or if POST suspends the agency from the program, the department still keeps its badges, guns, and arrest powers under local authority.
  • Losing POST participation can mean losing training money or access to POST resources. It does NOT mean the department stops being a police department or has to shut down.
  • POST’s own spokesperson confirmed this gap on the record. In response to questions about AB 48 reporting, POST spokesperson Meagan Poulos told LAist: “The way the law is written, there is no stated enforcement mechanism. The individual agencies are responsible.”
  • The California Department of Justice separately confirmed that AB 48 “does not include requirements for DOJ to review, audit, or enforce law enforcement agency requirements.” The bill’s author, former Assemblymember Cristina Garcia, asked in frustration: “If we don’t have enforcement, do we really have a law?”
Section VI Where Are the Laws That Apply to This Complaint?

For the Record — UC Police Are Full State Peace Officers

  • §
    UC police officers are not campus security. They are classified under California Penal Code § 830.2(b) — the same tier of the peace officer hierarchy that holds the California Highway Patrol (subdivision a) and CSU Police (subdivision c). Full statewide peace officer authority. Fully subject to POST oversight. Appointed under Education Code § 92600. This is not in dispute.

POST Certifies Individual Officers — It Does Not License Departments

  • 1
    California’s Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST) certifies individual peace officers and can decertify them for “serious misconduct” under SB 2, even without a criminal conviction.
  • 2
    POST keeps a list of certified officers and a list of participating agencies. It does not issue a charter that makes a city, county, or campus “a police department,” and it does not have the power to take that charter away.

SB 2 — The Kenneth Ross Jr. Police Decertification Act of 2021

  • 3
    SB 2 finally gave California a system for decertifying individual officers who commit serious misconduct, after decades as one of the few states without general decertification authority. However, SB 2 created no automatic fine, no funding cutoff, and no “department license” for POST to pull when a department fails to report. At most, POST can audit or question the agency’s voluntary participation status — but the department itself keeps right on operating.
  • 4
    Penal Code § 13510.9 says agencies must report allegations of serious misconduct to POST within 10 days — not just sustained findings, but any allegation that could lead to decertification.
  • 5
    POST has confirmed there are no statutory exceptions: departments must report even allegations they think are frivolous or intend to clear.

The Enforcement Gap — Voluntary Participation and the “Penalty” of Ineligibility

  • 6
    11 CCR § 1010(a)(2) — “Participation in the POST Program is voluntary.” The foundational regulation that frames everything below.
  • 7
    11 CCR § 1010(a)(9) — Non-Compliance. If POST finds an agency violated regulations, the Commission shall “reject all of the agency’s requests for services and benefits (refer to Penal Code Section 13523).” Reinstatement happens “when the agency has demonstrated, to the satisfaction of the Commission, that it will adhere to the prescribed Commission Regulations.”
  • 8
    Cal. Penal Code § 13523 — The “penalty” statute: ineligibility for POST aid. Not a fine. Not a shutdown.
Section VII Who Has Jurisdiction to Resolve These Issues?

POST already has direct personal jurisdiction over every commanding officer who signed off on a late report, buried a complaint in “other,” or retaliated against a whistleblower. Most of the people running California police departments — the chiefs, captains, lieutenants, and commanders — are themselves POST-certified peace officers. POST was built to uphold the Law Enforcement Code of Ethics, and the certification lever already exists. The gap is not a missing law or a missing agency. It is a missing decision — by the body that already holds the power — to use the tool it already has.

Section VIII What Has POST Actually Done in the Past?

  • 2
    Approximately ten percent of those public complaints involve chiefs or sheriffs — roughly 300 complaints. “Complaints involving chiefs or sheriffs represent approximately ten percent of all public complaints and are prioritized for review by the Professional Conduct Bureau Chiefs to determine the next appropriate steps.” POSAD 2025 Annual Report, p. 7, verbatim.
  • 3

Read the list yourself. Then read the news coverage of the cases. Look at the language the reporters use: “while on patrol,” “responded to the call,” “during the traffic stop,” “working the shift,” “arrested for,” “falsified records during a stop.”

Count how many read like a patrol officer’s shift. Count how many read like a chief’s calendar.

The only command-rank case this reporter could identify in public press coverage is San Rafael Lieutenant Dan Fink, who voluntarily surrendered his certification in May 2023. POST did not push the case. He walked away on his own terms.

300 chief complaints in. Zero chiefs out.

The Vote Threshold

To decertify a peace officer, California Penal Code § 13510.85(a)(5) requires “a two-thirds vote of Commissioners present.”

On an 18-seat commission at full attendance, that means 12 yes votes to decertify. Seven no votes block it.

Ten of the sixteen publicly-listed Commissioners come from sworn law enforcement or police-union leadership.

Section XI What Are the 9 Categories of POST Violations?

California Penal Code § 13510.8(b) lists nine categories of serious misconduct on which POST can strip a peace officer’s certification. Read them in order. Watch what happens at Category 1. Then watch what happens again at Category 5.

Category 1

Dishonesty. False reports, perjury, tampering with or destroying evidence, body-worn camera tampering.

Most people, hearing “police misconduct,” picture force first. Category 1 is what the public almost never sees and what every command staff knows is the hardest charge to survive: dishonesty in writing, on department letterhead, by sworn personnel. Once a peace officer’s candor is documented as compromised, every prior arrest report, search-warrant affidavit, and use-of-force narrative that officer authored becomes Brady material the District Attorney must disclose to defense counsel for the rest of that officer’s career.

Section IV of this Preface catalogues, in the Department’s own words, seven mutually inconsistent written classifications of the same retired officer’s status, issued by the same agency over a 30-month window, and four mutually inconsistent written statements — from the same Lieutenant, on the same day, in a single afternoon, then again 51 days later — about whether the personnel file supporting one of those classifications is located, archived, mis-shipped, or no longer in existence. All of it postdates a written litigation hold served on the Captain in February 2026. These are not anecdotes. They are the documentary record of Category 1 conduct, on the agency’s own letterhead, available to POST without a single subpoena.

CATEGORY 2 — Abuse of Power. Intimidating witnesses, knowingly obtaining a false confession, knowingly making a false arrest.

CATEGORY 3 — Physical Abuse. Excessive or unreasonable use of force.

CATEGORY 4 — Sexual Assault.

Category 5

Demonstrating Bias — on the basis of race, national origin, religion, gender identity or expression, housing status, sexual orientation, mental or physical disability, or other protected status.

CATEGORY 6 — Acts That Violate the Law — sufficiently egregious or repeated.

CATEGORY 7 — Participation in a Law Enforcement Gang.

CATEGORY 8 — Failure to Cooperate with a Misconduct Investigation.

CATEGORY 9 — Failure to Intercede when observing another officer use unnecessary force.

Most people, if they think about civil-rights violations at all, picture Category 3. Rodney King. Video. Blood. That is the only one the public recognizes on sight.

But read Category 5 again. Every word of it is 42 U.S.C. § 1983. Same protected categories. Same constitutional source — the Fourteenth Amendment. One gets prosecuted administratively by POST. The other gets prosecuted in federal court by the person who was harmed. Same conduct. Two doors.

The filing to POST does not beg POST to recognize a civil-rights violation. The filing to POST says: you already codified it at Category 5. Here is one. Act on it — or explain in writing why the state’s own statute does not apply when the officer being harmed is one of your own.

Playbook No. 341 is about one of those.

Section XII How Will This Complaint End With POST?
Nothing Will Be Done

Officer 341 already knows the outcome. Not because the complaints lack merit. Not because the evidence is insufficient. But because he went down this road thirty-seven years ago and faced off against the Regents of the University of California — which, as we will see later, is literally the fourth branch of government in California.

Back in the day, he reported corruption. He documented command-level misconduct. He filed grievances, contacted the FBI, the DA, the Bureau of State Audits, the Little Hoover Commission. He did everything the system says he was required to do.

And not a single thing was done.

What follows in this report is not a prediction. It is a documented pattern — decades of evidence showing exactly what happens when an officer inside the University of California system reports the truth up the chain. The officer pays the price. The University retains its authority, and the agencies that are supposed to provide oversight discover — if they even try — that the constitutional wall surrounding the Regents of the University of California makes enforcement structurally impossible.

This is not the story of one campus. The chapters that follow document a pattern that repeats across all ten University of California police departments — the same structural failures, the same lack of external accountability, the same constitutional shield. Ten departments. Ten campuses. One system. No oversight with teeth.

These POST complaints will follow the same path. Not because POST lacks the power to act against the individuals involved — it has that power today. But because the institution those individuals work for operates under a set of rules that no other employer in California is subject to. In fact, as the chapters that follow will demonstrate, virtually no other employer in America is subject to them.

The chapters that follow explain why.

The University of California is recognized worldwide as a leader in medicine, health sciences, public health research, and patient care. UCLA Health consistently ranks among the finest medical institutions on earth. The University produces the physicians, researchers, and public health leaders who set the standard for how healthcare should work.

Officer 341 respectfully suggests that the University’s mistreatment of its own retired peace officer’s retirement status and medical benefits — a peace officer who served and protected the leaders of UCLA — falls far short of the standard of excellence, integrity, and adherence to law that the University demands of its students, its faculty, and its physicians.

Heroes of medicine and science — that’s the world’s view of the University of California. Officer 341 asks only that UC behave like heroes — the heroes the world believes them to be.
