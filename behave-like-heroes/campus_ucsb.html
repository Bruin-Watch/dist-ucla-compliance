B-1. February 25–26, 1970 — Isla Vista Riots: Bank of America Burning

FieldDetailSource(s)
DateFebruary 25–26, 1970[1][2]
Incident DescriptionFollowing a speech by Chicago 7 attorney William Kunstler at Perfect Park, approximately 7,000 protesters burned the Bank of America building in Isla Vista. The riot was preceded by two precipitating events: the firing of popular anthropology professor William Allen by the university, and the beating and arrest of student Richard Underwood by police. Police did not respond during the burning. The Bank of America building was widely perceived as a symbol of corporate financing of the Vietnam War.[1][2]
Applicable Law / StandardCA Penal Code §451 (Arson); CA Penal Code §404–405 (Riot/Unlawful Assembly); POST use-of-force reporting standards (if applicable at time)[1][2]
Reported to POST?Unknown — predates modern POST reporting requirements[1][2]
Pre-Law or Post-LawPRE-LAW (predates SB 1421, AB 48, and modern POST standards)[1][2]
Outcome / StatusBank of America branch destroyed. No arrests reported during the burning. Federal and state investigations followed.[1][2]

B-2. April 18, 1970 — Death of Kevin Moran

FieldDetailSource(s)
DateApril 18, 1970[3][4][5]
Incident DescriptionDuring the second riot at a temporary Bank of America trailer in Isla Vista, UCSB student Kevin Moran, 22-year-old economics major, was shot and killed by Santa Barbara City Police Officer David Gosselin while Moran was attempting to extinguish a fire at the trailer. Police initially claimed the shot came from a sniper; ballistics testing subsequently proved the fatal round came from a .33 caliber police rifle. Officer Gosselin was exonerated. A memorial plaque at Embarcadero Hall (the current site of the former Bank of America building) reads: "For Social Change, Fair Play, and Peace."[3][4][5]
Applicable Law / StandardCA Penal Code §196 (Justifiable homicide by peace officer); Fourth Amendment (excessive force); POST officer-involved shooting reporting[3][4][5]
Reported to POST?Unknown — predates modern POST reporting requirements. SBPD jurisdiction, not UCSB PD primary jurisdiction.[3][4][5]
Pre-Law or Post-LawPRE-LAW (predates SB 1421, AB 748)[3][4][5]
Outcome / StatusOfficer Gosselin exonerated. Kevin Moran memorialized at Embarcadero Hall. Bank of America closed its Isla Vista branch in 1981.[3][4][5]

B-4. April 5, 2014 — Deltopia Riot

FieldDetailSource(s)
DateApril 5, 2014[8][9][10][11]
Incident DescriptionThe annual unsanctioned spring break party in Isla Vista drew an estimated 15,000 people. At approximately 9:30 PM on the 6700 block of Del Playa Drive, a UCSB police officer was struck in the head with a backpack containing large alcohol bottles, causing a significant head injury. During the arrest of the assailant, the crowd began throwing rocks, bricks, and bottles at law enforcement. Unlawful assembly was declared.[8][9][10][11]
Law Enforcement ResponseResponse involved 130+ law enforcement personnel from Santa Barbara County Sheriff, UCSB PD, CHP, Lompoc PD, Santa Maria PD, Ventura County, and San Luis Obispo County. Tear gas and rubber bullets were deployed. Multiple fires were set (mattresses, couches). Damage to public property included street signs and police vehicles.[8][9][10][11]
ChargesA 17-year-old male from Los Angeles was charged as an adult with mayhem, resisting arrest, and assault with a deadly weapon on a peace officer for the backpack attack on the officer.[8][9][10][11]
Applicable Law / StandardCA Penal Code §245(c) (Assault with deadly weapon on peace officer); CA Penal Code §404–405 (Riot/Unlawful assembly); CA Penal Code §148 (Resisting arrest); CA Penal Code §203 (Mayhem); POST use-of-force reporting for tear gas and rubber bullets; AB 48 (2021) — retroactive reporting not required but establishes standard for crowd control weapons[8][9][10][11]
Reported to POST?Unknown — AB 48 not yet enacted at time of incident. POST use-of-force reports not publicly confirmed for this event.[8][9][10][11]
Pre-Law or Post-LawPRE-LAW for AB 48 (enacted 2021). POST use-of-force standards applicable at time of incident.[8][9][10][11]
Outcome / Casualties130 total arrests; 190 citations issued; 50 individuals hospitalized; 6 officers injured (including one struck in the face with a brick). No deaths. Subsequent Deltopia events saw dramatically reduced attendance and arrests due to increased law enforcement presence.[8][9][10][11]

B-5. April 30, 2014 — Welfare Check Failure: Prior Police Contact with Elliot Rodger

FieldDetailSource(s)
DateApril 30, 2014 — approximately three weeks before the May 23, 2014 mass killing[12][13][14][15][16][17][18][19]
Incident DescriptionIsla Vista Foot Patrol deputies — a joint unit staffed by SBSO/UCSB PD/CHP — conducted a welfare check on Elliot Rodger at his mother's request after she discovered disturbing YouTube videos he had posted. Deputies found Rodger at his apartment. He presented a normal affect and explained the videos as expressions of loneliness.[12]–[19]
Critical Omissions Documented(1) Deputies did not check the California gun registry. (2) Deputies did not review the YouTube videos that had prompted the welfare check call. (3) Deputies did not initiate a 5150 hold (involuntary psychiatric evaluation under CA Welfare & Institutions Code §5150). Rodger subsequently wrote in his 141-page manifesto, "My Twisted World," that he feared the welfare check would end his plans and was relieved when deputies left without searching his room, where three legally purchased firearms and 440 rounds of ammunition were stored.[12]–[19]
Applicable Law / StandardCA Welfare & Institutions Code §5150 (Involuntary psychiatric hold); POST training standards for crisis intervention and threat assessment; CA DOJ firearms registry access protocols[12]–[19]
Reported to POST?Unknown — welfare check records maintained by SBSO/IVFP. POST training adequacy for threat assessment became subject of review following the May 23, 2014 attack.[12]–[19]
Pre-Law or Post-LawPOST training standards applicable at time of incident.[12]–[19]
OutcomeWelfare check concluded without further action. No 5150 hold initiated. No firearms check conducted. The failure to identify a credible threat was identified in subsequent investigations as a critical missed intervention point. Led to statewide review of threat assessment protocols.[12]–[19]
KEY FINDING — Welfare Check Failure: The April 30, 2014 welfare check on Elliot Rodger, conducted by the joint Isla Vista Foot Patrol (SBSO/UCSB PD/CHP) approximately three weeks before the mass killing, is documented in the SBSO investigative summary as a critical missed intervention point. Deputies did not check the California gun registry, did not review the YouTube videos that prompted the check, and did not initiate a 5150 psychiatric hold. Rodger himself documented his relief at the conclusion of the welfare check in his written manifesto, stating that he had feared it would end his plans.

B-6. May 23, 2014 — Isla Vista Mass Killing (Elliot Rodger)

FieldDetailSource(s)
Date and TimeMay 23, 2014, beginning at approximately 9:30 PM[12][13][14][15][16][17][18]
PerpetratorElliot Rodger, 22-year-old Santa Barbara City College student and Isla Vista resident. Son of Hunger Games assistant director Peter Rodger.[12][17]
Sequence of Attacks(1) Stabbed three roommates to death at Capri Apartments: Weihan "David" Wang (20), Cheng Yuan "James" Hong (20), George Chen (19). (2) Drove to Alpha Phi sorority house; attempted entry; failed to gain access. (3) Shot three women outside the sorority house, killing Katherine Cooper (22) and Veronika Weiss (19); injured one. (4) Shot and killed Christopher Michaels-Martinez (20) at the IV Deli Mart. (5) Drove through Isla Vista shooting at and ramming pedestrians. (6) Exchanged fire with Santa Barbara County Sheriff's deputies. (7) Crashed his BMW vehicle; died of a self-inflicted gunshot wound to the head.[12][13][14][15][16][17][18]
Weapons UsedThree legally purchased 9mm handguns: two Sig Sauers and one Glock. 440 rounds in 10-round magazines. Also used his BMW vehicle as a weapon against pedestrians.[12][17][18]
Pre-Attack CommunicationsManifesto titled "My Twisted World" (141 pages) emailed to approximately 34 people including his parents and therapists approximately 30 minutes before the attack began. YouTube video titled "Elliot Rodger's Retribution" posted hours before the attack.[12][17][18]
Total Casualties6 killed, 14 injured, perpetrator dead. 10 separate crime scenes investigated by SBSO.[12][13][14][15][16][17][18]
Victims (Named)Weihan "David" Wang (20, UCSB); Cheng Yuan "James" Hong (20, UCSB); George Chen (19, UCSB); Katherine Cooper (22, UCSB); Veronika Weiss (19, UCSB); Christopher Michaels-Martinez (20, UCSB)[12][13][14]
Applicable Law / StandardCA Penal Code §187 (Murder); CA Penal Code §245 (Assault with deadly weapon); CA Penal Code §664/187 (Attempted murder); Fourth Amendment (adequacy of prior welfare check); POST training standards for threat assessment and welfare checks[12]–[18]
Jurisdiction / POST ReportingSBSO held primary jurisdiction — POST reporting obligations fall primarily to the Sheriff's Office. UCSB PD assisted. SB 1421 (2018) would require disclosure of investigative records if UCSB PD maintained case files.[12]–[18]
Post-Event ConsequencesMultiple wrongful death lawsuits filed against Santa Barbara County, UCSB, and Rodger's parents — most dismissed on governmental immunity grounds. Memorial service at UCSB Harder Stadium drew 20,000+ attendees on May 27, 2014. California became the first state in the nation to pass a red-flag law permitting family members to petition courts for removal of firearms from dangerous individuals. Rodger's manifesto became a foundational document in online incel communities.[12]–[18]
