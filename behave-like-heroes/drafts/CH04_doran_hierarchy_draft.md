## Proposed section title
**The Chief Over All UC Chiefs — and What Happened to That Position**

## Section copy (verified, sourced)

For a brief window after the Los Alamos scandals, the University of California had exactly what its governance chart is missing today: a single executive at the President's Office whose portfolio spanned security at every national laboratory and every campus. That role was created as part of the 2003 Steven Doran whistleblower settlement, which placed Doran at UCOP Oakland as **Director of Public Safety and Systems Security** with oversight of the three UC-managed national labs and all ten UC campuses ([Military.com — "Fired Whistleblower Now Labs' Security Chief," Feb. 26, 2003](https://www.military.com/defensetech/2003/02/26/fired-whistleblower-now-labs-security-chief); [Los Angeles Times — "Fired Whistleblower at Los Alamos Wins $930,000 Settlement From UC," Aug. 21, 2003](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-2003-aug-21-me-alamos21-story.html)). A structural check that, if it had existed in 1990, would have placed someone above Karl T. Ross answerable to the Regents rather than to the Chancellor.

That consolidated authority no longer exists.

In its place, UC has fragmented the role across multiple offices that together look impressive on an org chart but leave one question unanswered: **who is actually in charge?**

- **"Coordinator of Police Services" at UCOP** — a low-profile staff role whose only systemwide body is the **Chief's Council**, an advisory group consisting of the ten campus chiefs plus a UCOP representative. The Chief's Council has no operational command over any chief. ([UC Academic Senate — Systemwide Public Safety Task Force Report, June 2018](https://senate.universityofcalifornia.edu/_files/reports/SNW-JN-gold-book-task-force-report.pdf))
- **Chief Risk Officer, Kevin Confetti** — oversees enterprise risk, environmental health & safety, and security-resiliency programs. Does not command sworn policing. ([UCOP — Kevin Confetti](https://www.ucop.edu/risk-services/kevin-confetti.html))
- **Systemwide Director of Community Safety, Jody Stiger** — appointed July 18, 2022 and publicly described as UC's "first" in that role. Stiger's office runs policy, accountability data, and community engagement. It does not exercise operational command over the ten campus police chiefs. ([UCOP — Jody Stiger Appointed UC's First Systemwide Director of Community Safety, July 18, 2022](https://www.ucop.edu/uc-operations/systemwide-community-safety/reports-and-updates/jody-stiger-appointed-ucs-first-systemwide-director-of-community-safety.html))
- **Executive Vice President & Chief Operating Officer Rachael Nava** — all of the above reports up to Nava, whose portfolio also includes HR, IT, and UCPath payroll systems. Policing is one item on a long operations list. ([UC Tech News, Nov 7, 2024](https://uctechnews.ucop.edu/fall-fireside-chat-with-rachael-nava-uc-office-of-the-president/))

And then there is the language itself, which UC has deliberately muddied. Every UC campus operates a **Community Service Officer (CSO)** program — student employees stationed inside the police department and reporting to sworn UCPD personnel. So when UC uses the phrase "community safety," it can mean any of four different things: Stiger's systemwide policy office, the community-safety plan implementation on each campus, the student CSO program inside each PD, or the sworn police department itself. Four different meanings. Four different offices. One question the Regents will not answer: **who is in charge?**

Campus police chiefs still report to their Chancellors. Just as Karl T. Ross did in 1990. The 2012 Robinson-Edley Report, commissioned after the UC Davis pepper-spray incident, put it in writing: *"Although the Office of the President coordinates certain systemwide police functions, it is not involved in the day-to-day management of any police department"* ([Robinson-Edley Report, May 4, 2012](https://campusprotestreport.universityofcalifornia.edu/documents/Robinson-Edley-Report-043012.pdf)).

**The question the Regents have never answered: if a consolidated security authority over the labs and campuses was good enough to offer Steven Doran as the price of buying his silence in 2003, why was that same consolidated authority never reestablished to prevent the next cover-up?**

Officer 341 believes Steven Doran belongs in the same chapter as the chiefs — not because his case happened at UCLA, but because the *structural remedy UC built for him in 2003, and abandoned in the years that followed, is the exact remedy that would have prevented the events of 1989–1996 from happening in the first place.*
