Finding 4A — The Two-Door Problem

UC’s own 1099-R for tax year 2009 — the same year the Herz correspondence began — reports Harold’s distribution as Code 3 (Disability) with $30,059.57 in gross distributions and $2,200.24 taxable (7.3%). Every January, UC mailed Harold a 1099-R — the same tax form every retiree in America receives from their pension. On that form, there is a small box labeled “Distribution Code.” UC filled in Code 3 — the IRS code that means “this person is disabled.” That code told the IRS to treat most of Harold’s income as tax-free, because federal law protects disability income differently than regular retirement income. In January 2010, UC mailed Harold that form for tax year 2009. That same September, Thomas Herz — a UC employee in the same retirement office that prepared the form — was writing Harold emails calling him a “retiree on disability.” The form and the emails said the same thing. UC knew what Harold was. They put it in writing to him and filed it under penalty of perjury with the IRS.

What Herz did next presents UC with a problem that has only two doors, and both lead to the same place.

Door 1: Herz knew Harold’s earnings. He worked in the same RASC office that prepared the 1099-R showing $30,059.57 in gross distributions for 2009. That is over $30,000 a year — well above the SSDI Substantial Gainful Activity threshold of $12,000 per year ($1,000 per month). If Herz knew what Harold earned, then he knew Harold could never qualify for SSDI, which means the entire compliance campaign — the nine months of emails, the threats, the May deadline, the demand that Harold physically go to a Social Security office — was pointless from day one. Herz was sending Harold on a fool’s errand. The question becomes: why?

Door 2: Herz did not know Harold’s earnings. He did not have access to the file. He did not know about the workers’ compensation settlement. He called the settlement a “myth.” He did not know Harold’s classification history. If this is the case, then UC’s records were already so broken in 2009 that a RASC employee administering disability retiree compliance did not know what his own office was paying Harold or how it was classified. Karl T. Ross said Harold resigned. Ida Fong, eleven years later, could not find Harold’s disability records. The 1099-R said Code 3 — Disability. Herz said “retiree on disability.” No one was looking at the same file because there was no single file.

Both doors lead to the same place. Either UC’s RASC knowingly pushed a disabled retiree through a process he could never qualify for — or UC’s record-keeping was so fragmented that the office administering disability compliance did not know its own retiree’s classification and earnings. Either way, the institution failed. Either way, the direction of the push was the same: toward SSDI, toward Medicare eligibility, toward shifting Harold off UC’s books.

◆ Foreshadow — Chapter 12

The reader will encounter this pattern again. In Chapter 12, the Department of Justice alleges that Elevance Health — the parent company of Anthem Blue Cross, Harold’s insurer throughout this entire period — paid brokers $262 million to steer Medicare beneficiaries away from coverage that was expensive for the insurer and into programs that shifted costs to Medicare. The Shea complaint alleges that Elevance Health’s broker network used different tools — kickbacks, call routing, agent coaching — but always in the same direction: push disabled beneficiaries away from coverage that is expensive for the insurer. UC used different tools — compliance threats, coerced conversion, a Via Benefits transfer — but always in the same direction: push Harold away from coverage that is expensive for UC and its insurer, Anthem Blue Cross, an Elevance Health subsidiary.

Harold had Anthem Blue Cross coverage the entire time. Every institutional push moved in the direction that would benefit both UC and Anthem. The documented record speaks for itself.
Finding 5 — Compliance Threat Pattern: Herz threatened Harold with “termination of UC medical coverage” and an “offset fee” (Email 15) for failure to provide SSA documentation. In 2026, Williams-Cook terminated Harold’s UC medical coverage based on a determination citing “our records indicate.” The pattern across sixteen years: UC repeatedly threatens or executes termination of Harold’s medical coverage while simultaneously failing to maintain, locate, or correctly interpret Harold’s own records. The institution demands compliance from the retiree while being unable to achieve compliance within its own record-keeping systems.
Finding 6 — Failure to Investigate Settlement Terms: Harold explicitly asked Herz — twice, in writing — to verify his settlement agreement before taking any action regarding his insurance (Emails 10 and 16). Herz did not do so. Instead, Herz applied general policy, dismissed Harold’s settlement as a “myth,” and assured Harold that SSA compliance would not jeopardize his settlement — without having read the settlement. When a plan administrator applies general policy to a beneficiary who has explicitly invoked a specific legal agreement governing their benefits, and the administrator refuses to verify that agreement, that constitutes a failure of the fiduciary obligation to administer benefits in accordance with the governing documents under California Government Code § 53213.5 (governmental plan fiduciary standards).

V. The Legal Void — Labor Code § 5803 and § 5804

Under California Labor Code § 5804, the Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board (WCAB) retains continuing jurisdiction over workers’ compensation awards. Under Labor Code § 5803, the WCAB has exclusive authority to “rescind, alter, or amend any order, decision, or award” relating to disability, and this requires notice and opportunity to be heard.

Harold’s disability classification and lifetime medical coverage were established by the 2003 workers’ compensation settlement (Claim No. 199650445, attorney Dennis Hargrove). That settlement is a WCAB-approved disposition. The Octagon permanent disability determination of August 8, 2001 has never been revoked.

For Harold’s disability classification to be legally changed — for him to move from disability status to regular retirement status — a WCAB proceeding was required. Not a phone call from a UC benefits employee. Not a form. Not an “election.” A formal WCAB proceeding with notice and opportunity to be heard, resulting in a modification order.

No such proceeding ever occurred. No WCAB modification order exists on the record.

(A) Harold could not legally “elect” to change his own disability classification. A workers’ compensation beneficiary cannot unilaterally waive or modify a WCAB-approved settlement by signing an enrollment form or agreeing on a phone call. The WCAB’s jurisdiction is exclusive under § 5803. Harold’s “election” had no legal effect on his disability status because he lacked the legal authority to make it.
(B) UC could not legally change Harold’s disability classification by phone call or administrative action. UC is a party to the workers’ compensation settlement. For UC to change Harold’s status from disability to regular retirement, UC was required to petition the WCAB for a modification under § 5804. UC did not do so. There is no WCAB modification order in the record.
(C) Both parties acted outside the WCAB’s exclusive jurisdiction. Harold “agreed” to something he had no legal authority to agree to. UC “processed” a change it had no legal authority to process. The 2015 “election” is void as a matter of law because it was effectuated outside the exclusive jurisdiction of the WCAB.
(D) UC encouraged and facilitated the unauthorized modification. The UC employee who called Harold in 2015 did not advise Harold that a WCAB proceeding was required. The UC employee did not consult Harold’s settlement file (because she claimed not to know about it). UC processed the classification change administratively without petitioning the WCAB. Under § 5803, only the WCAB can rescind, alter, or amend an order relating to disability. UC bypassed that requirement entirely.

This connects directly to the Herz correspondence: Herz in 2010 was already treating Harold’s disability classification as subject to general UC administrative policy — telling Harold he had to comply with SSA documentation requirements “as it does to all other retirees on disability,” and threatening termination of coverage for non-compliance. Herz never mentioned the WCAB. Herz never referenced the settlement. Herz applied administrative compliance rules to a workers’ compensation beneficiary whose status could only be modified by the WCAB. Then in 2015, a different UC employee did the same thing — applied administrative process (a phone call and an enrollment form) to accomplish a classification change that legally required a WCAB proceeding.

The pattern across 2010 and 2015: UC has repeatedly treated Harold’s workers’ compensation settlement as if it were a standard administrative enrollment decision subject to UC’s internal policies, when in fact it is a WCAB-approved legal disposition subject to the WCAB’s exclusive continuing jurisdiction under Labor Code §§ 5803 and 5804.

The question for the Plan Administrator: Please produce the WCAB modification order authorizing the change in Harold’s classification from disability to regular retirement in June 2015. If no such order exists, please state for the record by what legal authority UC changed a classification established by a WCAB-approved workers’ compensation settlement.
