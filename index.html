1989–1994

UCLA Service, Retaliation, Whistleblowing

Employment, the Regents' parking ticket, the founding of FUPOA, and the first cardiac and retaliatory record.

Entries 1–22

1

Entry 1

DECEMBER 1989 — EMPLOYMENT AND PRE-UCLA MEDICAL RECORD

§

I was employed as a sworn peace officer with the UCLA Police Department, having previously served with the San Gabriel Police Department (1983–1985), Culver City Police Department (1985–1988), and graduated from Rio Hondo Police Academy in 1983. My pre-UCLA medical record —

five separate examinations across three employers from December 29, 1980 through December 1, 1989 — uniformly documented a normal heart with no cardiac abnormalities of any kind.

2

Entry 2

JULY 1990 — THE REGENTS' PARKING TICKET

§

During a Regents' meeting on the UCLA campus, I wrote a parking citation to a vehicle operated by a UC Regent. I was immediately ordered by Assistant Chief Allen Cueba and Lt. James Vandenberg to cancel the citation. I was told the cancellation was directed by UCLA Chancellor Charles Young and UC Regent Dean Watkins. I complied with the direct order of my supervisory chain.

I was not a secondhand reporter of this event — I was the officer who wrote the ticket and was directly ordered to participate in its cancellation.

This event is documented in my contemporaneous records (SCOTT_WC.docx, entry dated 7/90). Cueba and Vandenberg — the same two officers who ordered the cancellation — subsequently became central figures in the retaliatory campaign documented below.

3

Entry 3

JULY 1990 — UNION NOMINATION THREAT

§

At department elections for the newly formed Police Officers' Association (POA), I was nominated for president. Lt. Vandenberg and others informed me it would not be a good idea to accept the nomination because management was looking at releasing me from probation due to my "outspoken attitude." I declined in fear of termination.

Threatening an employee with termination for accepting a union nomination constitutes a per se unfair practice under the Higher Education Employer-Employee Relations Act (HEERA, Government Code § 3571(a)).

4

Entry 4

APPROXIMATELY 1990–1991 — FOUNDING OF FUPOA

§

I founded the Federated University Police Officers' Association (FUPOA), the first National Labor Relations Board-recognized statewide law enforcement bargaining unit in the history of the University of California police system. I named it FUPOA rather than "University Police Officers Association" specifically to comply with California Education Code § 92000, which restricts use of the word "University" — demonstrating my knowledge of and compliance with applicable law from the outset.

5

Entry 5

NOVEMBER 26, 1990 — CRIMINAL INVESTIGATION; FIRST DOCUMENTED WHISTLEBLOWING

§

I personally initiated and conducted a criminal investigation (Offense Report #01-90-004164) documenting systematic criminal activity inside the UCLA Center for Health Sciences, including: systematic theft of $10,000–$15,000 in hospital supplies by housekeeping supervisor Issac Thompson; an extortion and job-selling scheme ($600–$2,000 per hire); prostitution on duty; and narcotics in the facility. I recorded eight conversations with a confidential informant and placed the recordings into evidence (Property Report #0190000204). Detective Longo conducted follow-up investigation.

Entry 6

JANUARY 1991 — ONSET OF PHYSICAL SYMPTOMS

§

I began experiencing stomach problems every night before work, increasing when working, not occurring on days off.

Entry 7

MARCH 23, 1991 — FIRST DOCUMENTED CARDIAC EVENT

§

I believed I was having a heart attack. I experienced chest pains, shortness of breath, left arm pain, nausea, and dizziness. My wife drove me to St. John's Hospital Emergency Room.

Entry 8

APRIL 4, 1991 — FIRST WORKERS' COMPENSATION FILING

§

Under the direct order of supervisor Lt. Michael Shain, I filed my first workers' compensation claim for my heart and stomach conditions.

Entry 9

APRIL 12, 1991 — FIRST DOCUMENTED CARDIAC DAMAGE

§

Heart tests at St. John's Hospital under Dr. Joyce Herrold's direction. Nuclear medicine report by Dr. Julian R. Karelitz documented "right ventricular enlargement" — thallium dye showed "non uniform" distribution with "minimal reperfusion in the inferoapical region" — with recommendation for evaluation for cardiomyopathy. Stress treadmill by Dr. Harry Rockoff showed occasional PVCs with exercise and recovery.

This is the first documented cardiac damage in my medical record, occurring sixteen months after I started at UCLA. Every pre-UCLA examination had documented a normal heart.

Under California Labor Code § 3212.5, heart trouble developing or manifesting during a period in which a peace officer is in active service is presumed to arise out of and in the course of employment.

Entry 10

APRIL 15, 1991 — INTERVIEW NOTES SUPPRESSED

§

Applied Risk Management Claims Administrator John Hodson interviewed me about job stressors. At the conclusion of the interview, Hodson stated that my filing of a workers' compensation claim "would not help the grievances" I had filed. Hodson's interview notes subsequently disappeared — Michael Lehman told me the notes "don't exist." I later personally observed a letter from Hodson at Dr. Reynolds' office that referenced that very conversation, proving the notes existed and had been withheld. The notes were never produced.

Entry 11

MAY 12, 1991 — QUID PRO QUO; RIGHTS SURRENDERED UNDER COERCION

§

At a meeting with Chief John Barber, Assistant Chief Karl Ross, and Assistant Chief Allen Cueba, I was offered a transfer away from Lt. Vandenberg's supervision on the express condition that I drop my workers' compensation claim and grievances. I agreed and was transferred. From June 1991 to January 1992, my stomach and heart problems improved and I was asymptomatic.

12

Entry 12

JANUARY 6, 1992 — AGREEMENT BROKEN; SYMPTOMS RESUME

§

Against my numerous and documented protests to Chief Barber, Karl Ross, and Allen Cueba, I was transferred back to Lt. Vandenberg's supervision. My medical and personnel problems immediately resumed.

Three command officers had conditioned relief from documented harassment on my surrender of statutory workers' compensation and grievance rights, and the University broke its own agreement upon my compliance.

The resolution and resumption of symptoms upon transfer-in and transfer-back respectively establishes that the University's own chain of command knew Vandenberg's supervision was the cause.

Vandenberg was completed and that this would take approximately three to four weeks. I remained on paid administrative leave for two years and two months.

This is a documented next-day retaliatory cancellation in direct response to my exercise of protected statutory rights.

Entry 21

APPROXIMATELY 1994–1995 — THE SKELLY HEARING; SHAIN TERMINATED

§

While I was on paid administrative leave, UCLA PD assigned Lt. Michael Shain — a named defendant in my active civil rights case SC022125 — to conduct an internal investigation of me. Lt. Shain's investigation report alleged I had displayed my personal firearm and police identification to obtain information from a bank. I obtained the UCPD Property Sheet Log from the UCLA PD Property Division, which documented that Lt. Boyarski had taken possession of my personal weapon, badge, and police identification approximately one year before the date of the alleged bank incident in Shain's report.

The UCLA Police Department had my gun, badge, and police identification in its own property room, per its own official property records, at the time the alleged incident was claimed to have occurred.

Chief Chapman convened a pre-termination due process hearing consistent with Skelly v. State Personnel Board (1975) 15 Cal.3d 194. When I produced the UCPD Property Sheet Log proving Shain's report was fabricated, Chief Chapman stated "This conversation is over" and walked out. I was not terminated. I was reinstated to full active duty. Lt. Michael Shain was terminated from the UCLA Police Department for falsifying the investigation report. The University had assigned an officer who was a named defendant in my pending civil rights lawsuit to build the case for my termination, and he fabricated the evidence.

UC's RASC letter of March 2, 2015 told me the opposite — that Retirement Income was the lifetime benefit and Disability Income was not — to induce a conversion that cost me $727.48 per month in premiums and converted my tax-free income to fully taxable income.
