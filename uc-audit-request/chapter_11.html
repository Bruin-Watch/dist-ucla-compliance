I. An Invitation and a Promise

This chapter presents the complete legal architecture underlying three published evidentiary reports — Bruin Watch, Schrödinger’s UC Retiree, and Behave Like Heroes — together with formal POST complaints filed against three UCLA police officers. Below the opening statement, 282 statutes, constitutional provisions, case authorities, regulations, and UC internal policies are catalogued and cross-referenced to the specific chapters where each appears.

The record speaks for itself. What follows is an invitation to read it — and a promise about what happens next.

Charles A. Harold Jr. was a UCLA peace officer who, 33 years ago, blew the whistle and exposed top-down rot in police command — resulting in the termination of the Chief and his commanders, events UCLA considered significant enough to document in an academic study at its own Luskin Center for History and Policy.

He was then injured on duty. UC’s own claims administrator determined his disability “permanent and stationary.” For a decade, UC honored every term — tax-free income, paid health coverage, CCW permits. Then his file disappeared, four systems classified him four different ways, and they tried to move him to a medical insurance program he was not required to utilize and was ineligible for. He caught it by accident — with his cancer surgery eight weeks away.

The University of California has two paths forward. It can reconcile the record, restore the classification its own documents confirm, and reimburse the financial harm its own tax filings prove. Or it can defend its position — in which case it will need to explain, under oath, how a single human being can occupy six mutually exclusive employment classifications at the same time.

Harold has walked both paths before. He filed grievances. He filed a state tort claim. He filed a federal lawsuit. That was thirty years ago, and the record was thinner then. It is not thinner now.

Since those early filings, the evidentiary record has grown to include UC’s own RASC correspondence confirming disability status, UC’s own 1099-R forms contradicting each other across a decade of tax filings, UC’s own employee admitting records were “lost during computer system upgrades,” UC’s own $28 million pension system lawsuit proving the system of record was broken, 25 documented data breaches, a premium cessation timed to regulatory complaints, and three POST complaints against officers who relied on classifications the system itself could not maintain.

And then there is Stephen Agostini. UCLA’s own Vice Chancellor and Chief Financial Officer — a man who had served as CFO of the United States Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and testified before Congress — told the Daily Bruin on February 6, 2026 that UCLA’s annual financial reports posted on its website since 2002 are “erroneous and unaudited.” He identified a $425 million annual structural deficit caused by “financial management flaws and failures” predating his arrival. He stated that the Ascend Finance Transformation Project — UCLA’s initiative to replace a legacy mainframe designed in the 1980s — had consumed $213 million over seven years, including $150 million with “nothing to show for it.” Four days later, Chancellor Julio Frenk fired him. The Daily Bruin editorial board responded: “Agostini’s departure after interview with The Bruin shows UCLA punishes transparency.”

UCLA called his claims “inaccurate.” Then the record caught up. UCLA’s own Academic Senate Council on Planning and Budget — having voted 115–1 to demand financial transparency — issued an interim report on January 29, 2026 confirming the campus structural deficit approaches $400 million annually. By March 26, even UCLA’s interim CFO admitted a $220 million deficit on central accounts alone, while acknowledging that additional shortfalls had not yet been captured. On March 31, the City of Culver City announced it had hired Agostini as its new CFO — lauding him for having “closed a $300 million budget gap” at UCLA and “delivered a balanced budget with a surplus.” UCLA has not published a campus-wide budget since fiscal year 2022–23.

The “since 2002” window Agostini identified encompasses every year of Harold’s records dispute — from the 2003 workers’ compensation settlement through the 2015 reclassification through today. Agostini confirmed from the executive suite what Ida Fong admitted from the benefits processing floor: the systems were broken, the records were wrong, and the institution knew it. UCLA fired the one who said it out loud. Culver City hired him — the same Culver City where Harold began his law enforcement career before joining UCLA PD. Some circles close themselves.

The 282 entries that follow are not threats. They are citations. Every one of them is already in the published record. An attorney reviewing this table will recognize the pattern for what it is: a case that was documented from the inside, using the institution’s own paperwork, by someone who understood that the record would eventually have to speak for itself.

This is that record. It is now speaking.

III. The Big Picture

The strength of this record does not rest on any single violation. It rests on the pattern. Over more than three decades, the University of California simultaneously classified Harold one way for tax purposes and another way for benefits, one way for IRS certification and another way for LEOSA eligibility, one way for premium deductions and another way for coverage termination. The Schrödinger metaphor that runs through these manuscripts is not literary flourish — it is a precise description of an institutional impossibility: no human being can occupy six mutually exclusive employment classifications at the same time. Every new document UC produces either confirms one classification or contradicts another. The University cannot have it both ways. The record proves it has tried.

The weakest link across all claims is damages quantification for the non-tax injuries — emotional distress, loss of CCW rights, dignitary harm, and the ongoing uncertainty of living without stable health coverage at age 65 and beyond. The tax damages and premium overcharges are precise to the penny. Everything else will require expert testimony to reduce to a number. But the liability exposure is real, and the documentary foundation is unusually strong.

What makes this body of work exceptional is not inference, not theory, and not speculation. It is the sheer volume of the University of California’s own documents contradicting the University of California’s own positions. Most civil rights cases are built on what the plaintiff can prove the institution did. This one is built on what the institution proved about itself.
