RE: CMP373 — Rejection of UCLA Compliance Office’s April 17, 2026 “Matter Closed” Determination; Demand for Referral to an Outside Agency for Independent Review.

Dear UCLA Compliance Office:

I respectfully disagree with your April 17, 2026 determination in CMP373 and request that this matter be referred immediately to an outside agency for independent review. The grounds for this request are set forth below.

UCLA Compliance’s April 17, 2026 letter states on its face that the determination was based on “your complaints and documentation we received from UCLA PD.” UCLA Compliance did not interview the complainant, did not request Harold’s own documentary record, and did not independently gather evidence. UCLA Compliance’s conclusion rests entirely on a record curated and delivered by UCLA PD — the agency whose officers are the subjects of the complaints. A Compliance determination that relies on evidence hand-selected by the accused agency is not an independent review; it is a procedural rubber stamp.

Stated more directly: the office charged with ensuring UC’s compliance with the law has, in this matter, conducted itself in a manner that bears the hallmarks of collusion with the investigated agency, extrinsic fraud as to the complainant, and — given the underlying California Labor Code § 132(a) crime report UCLA PD has now ignored for fifty-seven days — obstruction of justice. UCLA’s own Compliance Office is, at this moment, out of compliance.

Specific UC and California authorities violated by the April 17, 2026 determination:

1. UC Standards of Ethical Conduct — “Compliance with applicable laws and regulations” and “Reporting violations and protection from retaliation”: managers and supervisors “are required to report allegations presented to them.”

2. UC Whistleblower Policy 1100171, § II.B.3 — supervisor duty to escalate to the Locally Designated Official when a matter “involves allegations or events that have a significant possibility of being the result of a criminal activity.”

3. UC Whistleblower Policy 1100171, § II.C.2 — “A copy of communications sent to the Systemwide LDO shall be sent to the respective UC Police department if on the basis of the allegations it appears that a crime may have been committed.”

4. UC Police Personnel Complaints Policy 1008.2 — “The Department will accept and address all complaints of misconduct in accordance with this policy and applicable federal, state, and local law.”

5. UC Police Policy 1008.3 — complaints “may be investigated by a department supervisor of a rank greater than the accused employee.” The provision further requires that when the Chief of Police is the subject of a complaint, the recommendation must be made to the appropriate Vice Chancellor.

6. UC Police Policy 1008.11 (Handling of Complaints Alleging Crimes) — “When an employee is accused of potential criminal conduct, a separate supervisor or investigator shall be assigned to investigate the criminal allegations apart from any administrative investigation. The Chief of Police shall be notified as soon as practicable when an employee is accused of criminal conduct. The Chief of Police may request a criminal investigation by an outside law enforcement agency.”

7. ECAS February 2023 Systemwide Police Personnel Complaints Process and Use of Force Reporting Audit, Finding 5 — “No UC campus had a standardized process for investigating a Chief of Police,” with the audit recommending that systemwide policy must “prohibit departments from investigating complaints against their own chief.”

8. California Penal Code § 13510.8 (SB 2) — mandatory POST reporting of allegations of serious misconduct by peace officers; reciprocal to the three POST complaints filed February 25, 2026 (PUBLIC-26-000212, -000213, -000214).

9. California Labor Code § 132(a) — the substantive workers’ compensation retaliation crime that is the subject of the February 19, 2026 crime report UCLA PD has not investigated for fifty-seven days. California Government Code § 8547.2(c) defines “improper governmental activity” to include “willful omission to perform duty” and “coercion” — both directly applicable here.

Three structural failures appear on the face of the April 17, 2026 letter:

A. Procedural invalidity. UCLA Compliance never interviewed the complainant. There was no telephone contact, no email correspondence, no in-person meeting, and no written questionnaire issued to Harold. A determination issued without any contact with the complainant fails UC Whistleblower Policy 1100171 § II.B.6, which provides that “internal communications regarding allegations of improper governmental activities should normally be in writing” and that supervisors should “document oral reports by written transcription.” UCLA Compliance produced a determination without ever creating, requesting, or receiving any oral or written record from the person who filed the complaints.

B. Cognitive dissonance and the halo effect in PD command-staff self-curation. The April 17, 2026 letter expressly identifies UCLA PD as the source of the documentation on which the determination relied. UCLA PD — the agency containing Captain Jeff Chobanian (Badge #307) and the Chief of Police whose conduct is the subject of the underlying POST complaints — selected, organized, and delivered the evidentiary record on which UCLA Compliance then ruled. This is the exact structural conflict the ECAS February 2023 audit identified as systemwide policy failure and directed UC to prohibit. The April 17, 2026 determination demonstrates that, three years after the ECAS finding, the conflict the audit warned of is operative at UCLA.

C. UC’s own 1996 institutional precedent contradicts the April 17, 2026 determination. In the 1990s, UCLA Police Officer Charles Harold conducted a criminal investigation and exposed institutional corruption at UCLA PD. UC investigated, substantiated the report, and terminated the UCLA Chief of Police and members of command staff. Those events are documented in UCLA’s own Luskin Center for History and Policy publication, The History of Racism and the Quest for Racial Justice at UCLA (March 2022, pp. 35–36, footnotes 147–164). The officer who conducted that investigation is the same complainant whose successor pattern is now before UCLA Compliance. UC’s 1996 finding stands as UC’s own institutional admission that the conduct Harold reported — then and now — was wrongful. UCLA Compliance has issued a “no policy violation” finding on substantively similar facts from the same complainant, without interviewing him, on a record curated by the agency he reported.

Remedy requested. Pursuant to UC Whistleblower Policy 1100171 § II.C.2, this matter is hereby referred for independent review to an outside agency. The undersigned requests that UCLA Compliance refer this matter to one or more of the following: (i) the California State Auditor (Whistleblower Hotline, (800) 952-5665); (ii) the California Attorney General; (iii) the California Commission on Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST), in connection with already-pending complaints PUBLIC-26-000212, -000213, and -000214; (iv) the UC Systemwide Locally Designated Official at the Office of the President; or (v) a UC sister-campus compliance office free of conflict. A response identifying the agency or agencies to which this matter has been referred is requested within thirty (30) days of receipt of this letter.

Preservation notice. All UCLA PD, UCLA Compliance, UCOP, UC General Counsel, and Regents of the University of California records relating to CMP373, the February 19, 2026 California Labor Code § 132(a) crime report, the three POST complaints, and the April 17, 2026 determination are subject to litigation hold as of the date of this letter. Spoliation of any such record will be addressed under California Evidence Code § 413 and applicable common law.

II. Preliminary Notice Regarding Premium Non-Payment, Direct Payment Offer, and Coverage Certificate Discrepancy

NOTICE I Non-Payment of Medigap Premium Was Caused by UC — Not by Harold

I am aware that the University of California has ceased deducting my UC High Option Supplement to Medicare premium ($727.48 per month) from my UC Retirement Annuity pension, effective with the January 2026 payroll cycle (first reflected in the February 1, 2026 deposit), without notice to me and without my authorization. I am further aware that these premiums have not been remitted to Anthem Blue Cross on my behalf since that date.

My Unify Financial Credit Union bank records document the following UC Retirement Annuity deposits:

January 2025 through June 2025: $1,777.15 per month

July 2025 through November 2025: $1,834.33 per month (annual adjustment — increase of $57.18)

December 2025: $2,174.78 — increase of $340.45 from the prior month (source of this increase remains untraced and is itself the subject of audit inquiry)

January 2026: $2,174.78 — no change from December 2025

February 2026: $2,902.26 — increase of exactly $727.48 from January 2026, matching to the penny the monthly premium for UC High Option Supplement to Medicare PPO covering both Charles A. Harold Jr. and Silvia Z. Harold

March 2026: $2,902.26 — no change from February 2026

April 2026: $2,902.26 — no change from March 2026 (fourth consecutive month without High Option premium deduction)

(Source: EX 25 — Unify Financial Credit Union Account Details, January 1, 2025 through March 31, 2026; April 2026 deposit per Harold’s Unify Financial Credit Union account record — file not yet uploaded)

UC controls the sole mechanism by which my Medigap premium has been collected — payroll deduction from my UC Retirement Annuity pension. I did not request this change. I did not authorize this change. I was not notified of this change. The January 29, 2026 Immediate Hold Request (EX 29) specifically demanded that no changes be made to my coverage.

I do not accept cancellation, termination, non-renewal, or any adverse action against my UC High Option Supplement to Medicare (Medigap) coverage on the basis of non-payment of premium when that non-payment was caused exclusively by UC’s unilateral cessation of the premium deduction mechanism — the sole mechanism available to me for premium payment — without my knowledge, consent, or authorization.

NOTICE II Standing Offer to Pay Premium Directly to Anthem Blue Cross

On March 31, 2026, I notified Anthem Blue Cross Compliance ([email protected]) in writing that I am prepared to pay my $727.48 monthly premium directly to Anthem Blue Cross, including all back premiums from February 2026 forward, to ensure continuous coverage. I requested the correct payee name, mailing address, account or reference number, and written confirmation that direct payment would be accepted.

I further stated that if Anthem Blue Cross refuses to accept direct premium payment from a policyholder whose employer has unilaterally stopped remitting premiums, I request that refusal in writing with the specific legal or contractual basis cited.

That offer remains open and is hereby restated for this record. I am prepared to remit all back premiums from February 2026 through the present month, plus ongoing monthly premiums of $727.48, directly to Anthem Blue Cross upon receipt of payment instructions. UC’s cessation of the deduction mechanism does not reflect any unwillingness on my part to pay. The non-payment is UC’s act, not mine.

NOTICE III Federal Coverage Certificate Replaced Without Record of Termination Event

On April 1, 2026, Anthem Blue Cross generated a Certificate of Creditable Coverage documenting the termination of my UC High Option Supplement to Medicare coverage effective that date. This certificate is a federally required document under 42 U.S.C. § 1395ss, and it is the document a beneficiary presents to a new insurer to establish guaranteed issue rights for Medigap enrollment. I preserved a copy of this certificate. (Source: EX 17 — Anthem portal screenshots documenting cancellation and certificate, April 1–2, 2026)

On April 7, 2026, at 3:40 PM, Anthem Blue Cross generated a new Proof of Insurance letter within the member portal showing both Charles A. Harold Jr. and Silvia Z. Harold as covered members effective January 1, 2026, with no termination date stated. On the same date, the Anthem member portal dashboard restored the plan status from a red INACTIVE badge (documented April 2, 2026 at 10:12 AM) to a green ACTIVE badge.

The April 7, 2026 Proof of Insurance reflects coverage effective January 1, 2026 — the same start date as the original coverage period. If coverage was terminated on April 1, 2026 and subsequently reinstated, the new coverage period should reflect a start date of April 1, 2026 (or April 7, 2026) forward — not January 1, 2026. By backdating the reinstatement to January 1, 2026, the April 7 document effectively overwrites the April 1 termination, making it appear as though the termination event never occurred.

The Anthem member portal now reflects no record of the April 1, 2026 termination. The federally required Certificate of Creditable Coverage documenting that termination — a document that triggers guaranteed issue rights under 42 U.S.C. § 1395ss(b)(1)(A) — has been effectively removed from the accessible portal record.

I have preserved the original April 1, 2026 Certificate of Creditable Coverage, the April 2, 2026 INACTIVE status screenshots, and the April 7, 2026 ACTIVE status and Proof of Insurance documents. These records are maintained on the DEVORATOR evidence archive and have been submitted to both CMS (Case #7100158) and DMHC (Case #1409109) through prior supplemental filings.
